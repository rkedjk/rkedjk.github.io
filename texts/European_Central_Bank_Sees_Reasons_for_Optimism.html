European Central Bank Sees Reasons for Optimism

https://www.nytimes.com/2013/12/06/business/international/european-central-bank-stands-firm-on-rates.html?partner=rss&emc=rss

The central bank’s economists on Thursday lifted their forecast for growth next year to 1.1 percent, compared with the forecast in September of 1 percent. For 2013, the bank estimated that economic output in the 17 countries in the euro zone collectively shrank 0.4 percent. By recent European standards, 1.1 percent would be a torrid economic expansion, and it was enough that the bank did not see a need on Thursday to announce any new measures to stimulate the euro zone economy. Mr. Draghi said the bank. had considered a program similar to one used since last year by the Bank of England, which offers cheap cash to commercial banks on the condition that they lend it to customers. The central bank has been allowing banks to borrow as much money as they want at the low official rate. But as Mr. Draghi noted Thursday, they have mainly used the money to buy government bonds and profit from the small difference in interest rates. In almost the same breath, though, Mr. Draghi questioned whether a program like the Bank of England’s would work in the euro zone, which will have 18 countries when Latvia joins in January. “We should not ignore the practical complications stemming from measures like these,” he said. He also dampened speculation that the central bank, now that it has cut rates almost as far as possible, would buy huge quantities of bonds to stimulate the economy, as the United States Federal Reserve has done. Quantitative easing of that sort would be more difficult in the euro zone, because there is no Pan-European counterpart to the Treasury bonds that the Fed has purchased. “People say the E.C.B. should buy assets,” Mr. Draghi said. “What assets?” European Central Bank economists also forecast that inflation would remain well below the bank’s official target of close to, but below, 2 percent through 2015. That would be an unusually long period of muted price increases.
