Door number three?

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2010/06/climate_policy

AT THE New York Times Alex Tabarrok discusses President Obama's Tuesday speech on energy policy: [T]he president called for innovation and hard choices but offered little new or courageous thinking of his own. ... Most important, nowhere did the president mention two hard ideas that the public must accept if we are to move to a cleaner energy future: nuclear power and carbon taxes. Nuclear power is among the cleanest sources of energy, power plants can be built when and where needed and the combination of nuclear-generated electricity and hydrogen can serve virtually all of our energy needs. Is nuclear power safe? Oil spills and coal-mine disasters should remind us that safety is always relative. Mark Thoma adds his thoughts: I was on the fence before and not really sure whether to go with the seemingly emerging consensus that nuclear power is the answer to our energy needs, there were always nagging doubts, but the problems in the gulf make me hesitant to embrace nuclear power despite assurances that the risks are minuscule. When you're faced with a challenge like global warming—that is, an one of unprecedented scope—you don't rule anything out. Nuclear power will play a role in reducing emissions. Will it (and should it) play a major role? There are reasons to be sceptical. Nuclear power plants cost a fantastic amount of money to set up, and the process of planning, permitting, and construction means that there is an extremely long lead time for new plants. America alone would have to construct hundreds of new reactors to bring nuclear's generation share up to the level in France, and of course, the biggest source of new energy demand—the emerging world—would require thousands or tens of thousands of reactors, very rapidly built. It's not clear that this is at all realistic. It's important to keep tail risks in mind, as we're repeatedly reminded these days. The fall out from catastrophic failure at a nuclear plant is quite a bit worse than that from failure at a coal plant, to say nothing of a wind turbine. Mr Tabarrok's other hard idea—carbon taxes—actually contains multiple strategies for the reduction of emissions. If you make dirty power more expensive, people will consume less dirty power, but this shift can occur in a number of different ways. Consumers can simply cut back. Or they can switch to alternatives. Or they can improve efficiency, so that they can engage in the same activity as before in a less energy-intensive manner. This is the beauty of a carbon price; it encourages everyone exposed to the price increase to reduce emissions in the way cheapest and easiest for them. It encourages households to tap efficiency possibilities in hundreds of small ways, covering areas government regulators couldn't begin to see ahead of time. This micro-efficiency is the door number three of climate policy, the great policy hope unleashed by carbon pricing. Unfortunately, carbon pricing is all but dead as a policy choice in America: Unfortunately, the great economic strength of market systems like cap and trade also happens to be their political weakness. They set prices and allow people to react. In the process, market systems acknowledge that reducing pollution may actually cost a little bit of money. Politicians don't like to admit this, because voters don't like it. Accepting higher costs is especially hard when the economy is weak. So Congressional Democrats have been repackaging their energy bills to make them look less and less market-oriented. Senator John McCain, who supported a permit system for carbon as the Republican presidential nominee, no longer does. Senator Lindsey Graham, the South Carolina Republican, has reversed his position as well. So then, what's on the table? Well, here's a snippet from the president's speech: Some have suggested raising efficiency standards in our buildings like we did in our cars and trucks. Some believe we should set standards to ensure that more of our electricity comes from wind and solar power. Others wonder why the energy industry only spends a fraction of what the high-tech industry does on research and development -- and want to rapidly boost our investments in such research and development. These aren't terrible ideas. Relative to what we'd see from an appropriate carbon price, there is a lot of low-hanging fruit being left on the tree. But in general, policies like this are best understood as efforts to change the elasticity of demand for dirty energy—to make increases in the price of dirty energy less painful for consumers while increasing the demand response. But without a price increase, emission cuts will still be pretty insignificant. The hope, then, must be either than market prices for dirty energy will rise of their own accord (which may happen, but this is a risky bet, as a lot of fossil fuels may be burned before they do), that increased elasticities will ultimately make the voting public more receptive to a carbon price, or that EPA source regulation will effectively increase energy prices. None of this is particularly pretty. But it's what you get when politicians are afraid to tell voters the truth—that the world is warming because households aren't paying a fair price for energy, and to keep it from warming catastrophically they'll have to pay more.
