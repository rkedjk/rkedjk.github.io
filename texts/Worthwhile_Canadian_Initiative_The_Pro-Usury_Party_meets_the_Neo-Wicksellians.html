Worthwhile Canadian Initiative: The Pro-Usury Party meets the Neo-Wicksellians

http://worthwhile.typepad.com/worthwhile_canadian_initi/2011/01/the-pro-usury-party-meets-the-neo-wicksellians.html

What happens? In particular, will AD fall and will output fall with it? Will the PUP cause a recession? I want to consider a thought-experiment: assume the economy is initially in equilibrium at a 3% real interest rate. (The natural rate of interest is 3%). The Pro-Usury Party then gains power. The new PUP government passes a law that says all loans must be indexed for inflation, and the real rate of interest must be a minimum of 10%. The governor of the central bank pleads for an exemption from the law, on the grounds that money is barren. The PUP allows that central bank liabilities, like currency, can pay 0% interest. The central bank is also granted the power of compulsory purchase of bonds, if it wishes to do an open market operation. Most macroeconomists believe that the real interest rate is what equilibrates AD and AS. If the real interest rate is too high, AD will be too low, and lower than AS, so there's excess supply of output. And the fall in AD will cause an equivalent fall in output. That's the orthodox Neo-Wicksellian position. Here's my answer: no. There is a market for output, where goods exchange for money. And a market for bonds (loanable funds), where bonds exchange for money. The bond market will not clear. There will be an excess demand for bonds, since the binding pro-usury law keeps the rate of return on bonds above equilibrium and the price of bonds below equilibrium. Savers will want to buy more bonds than dissavers and investors want to sell. The market for output will continue to clear. The price of output may have to adjust up or down, or the central bank may have to adjust the money supply up or down, to make it clear. But it can still clear, given the right level of the real money supply. (That's why I stuck in that proviso about the central bank being able to buy bonds by compulsory purchase if it want to increase the money supply.) With the bond market failing to clear, desired investment by anyone who needs to borrow to finance that investment will be lower, since the rate of interest is higher. But desired investment by people who would otherwise lend their savings will be higher. Since they cannot buy as many bonds as they want, given the excess demand for bonds. Overall investment will probably be lower, since the savers don't have good direct investment opportunities themselves. That means there will be slower growth in the capital stock, plus malinvestment, so the long run growth rate will be reduced. But I don't see an excess supply of goods. I don't see a recession. The demand for money may increase a bit, since some people will be unable to buy bonds and will choose to hold money instead rather than consume or do direct investment. So the central bank may need to increase the money supply to match the increased money demand. (Or a lower price level may do the same job of increasing the real money supply). People will want to buy bonds. But they can't. So they will buy goods instead. Despite the real rate of interest being above the natural rate, there will be no recession. An excess supply of output is not the flip-side of the excess demand for bonds. An excess supply of output is the flip-side of an excess demand for money. We do not exchange output for bonds; we exchange output for money. In my view, high real interest rates themselves do not cause a recession. The tight monetary policy, of which high real interest rates may or may not be a symptom, is what causes a recession. This thought-experiment is designed to elucidate this point, by seperating monetary policy from interest rate policy.
