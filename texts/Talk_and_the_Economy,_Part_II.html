Talk and the Economy, Part II

https://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/jamessurowiecki/2009/02/talk-and-the-ec-1.html

Felix Salmon’s post on the rose-colored glasses being worn by Barack Obama and Ben Bernanke also touches on a subject I wrote about yesterday: namely, the connection between what people (particularly policymakers) say about the economy and what happens in the economy. In his recent column on the subject, economist Robert Shiller suggested that constantly raising the spectre of the Great Depression and emphasizing the dire state of the economy (as Obama did while pushing for his stimulus package) may actually exacerbate the economic downturn by making people excessively cautious and risk-averse. Felix, by contrast, suggests that false hopefulness is worse than doomsaying: “All of this noise out of Washington just makes me feel that policymakers are as disconnected from the real world as ever. And that feeling is not conducive to getting me to go out and hire, or spend.” It’s certainly true that we don’t want politicians to seem disconnected from what’s really happening—that’s why John McCain’s pronouncement last fall that the U.S. economy’s fundamentals remained strong hurt him so much. But that’s not the same as saying that the economy as a whole benefits when someone like Obama stresses the gloominess of our circumstances and the tremendous difficulties we face. Would it really have been better for the U.S. economy if, instead of “The only thing we have to fear is fear itself,” F.D.R. had said, “There is are many, many things that we should be really terrified of”? People, after all, know how bad things are—they’re dealing with the recession every day, and they’re adjusting accordingly by cutting back on spending, increasing savings, delaying investment, and so on. What people don’t have right now, and what they need—at least if you follow Shiller’s logic—is a sense that things can, in fact, get better, and that the challenges we face can, in fact, be met (which they can). From a strategic point of view, it made sense for Obama to stress the grimness of current economic conditions in order to get the stimulus package passed. But now that his policies are falling into place, it seems like it’s time for a rhetorical shift, away from bleakness and toward possibility. And I hope (there’s that word) that we’ll see that shift in his speech tonight.
