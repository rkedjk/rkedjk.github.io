The Global Innovation Revolution by Laura Tyson

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/the-global-innovation-revolution

With world wide scientific knowledge increasing at an exponential rate, it will not necessarily result in innovation, if nothing can get implemented. Even with the scientific knowledge itself being very global, specific countries and areas can inhibit or destroy the ability of that knowledge being utilized to create innovative solutions to problems. In particular, the USA is presently preventing innovative solutions. The concept of the "Tragedy of the AntiCommons" < http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommons > describes a situation where multiple people, organizations, bureaucrats have to ability to block action on a proposed innovative project and no single person or group can say "YES" and actually implement an innovative concept. This situation results in a major miss-allocation of resources and prevents innovation along with job creation. For example, if Apple computer wanted to build i-pads in California in an innovative state of the art new facility, obtaining permits for a 100,000+ employee new business using hazardous materials with significant traffic impacts along with possibly impacting an endangered fairy shrimp species living in a mud hole on the proposed site would be effectively impossible, in a time scale relevant for the market. In California, people have been working for 10 years to get permits to desalinate sea water in water short So. California. Studies have shown that we could have a multi-billion dollar offshore aquaculture industry in Southern California, but all attempts have met with at least one of dozens of bureaucrats saying NO, so the industry is proceeding in its innovative direction south of the border. Along with the official bureaucrats, almost every activist organization needing a fund raising campaign can create delays and lawsuits resulting in the good innovations moving to other more receptive countries. Having good science and research is only part of innovation and without the ability to follow through, the ideas and knowledge will leave the country -- I know, I help it leave with my consulting and scientific journal editorial activity. Having more R&D may be irrelevant if we don't fix the "tragedy of the AntiCommons" problems ranging from an absurd patent systems (patent thickets preventing innovation) to multiple "stakeholders", with no real skin in the game, having veto power and using that power for self advantage and extortion or bureaucratic turf battles.
