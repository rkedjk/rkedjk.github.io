The Dollar and its Discontents by Barry Eichengreen

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/dollar-could-lose-global-hegemony-by-barry-eichengreen-2018-10

Barry Eichengreen says the sanctions that Trump reimposes on Iran following his unilateral withdrawal in May from the nuclear deal – the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) – that Iran signed with world powers in 2015, could spell the end of the dollar as the global reserve currency since the end of World War II. The other signatories are looking for a way to facilitate transactions between Iran and foreign companies without using dollar. As the US threatens to penalise companies that do business with Iran by denying them access to US banks, Britain, France, Germany, China and Russia are considering to replace the dollar with the euro, although much of the world trade is dollar-denominated. The trillions of dollars in circulation across the globe have helped the US run permanent trade deficits. But in 2003, the world also saw a quiet emergence of the “petroeuro.” The Iranian and Russian parliaments discussed adopting the euro for oil sales. The Kremlin entered into negotiations with Germany over the establishment of an exchange to sell oil futures denominated in euros. Russia was an important oil exporter and was awash with dollars, but traded mainly with Europe. Russia's foreign exchange holdings reached an all-time high of $50bn then. Major European oil producers such as TotalFinaElf and Shell saw their gains gobbled up by a tumbling dollar, as their profits were calculated in euros. The fledgling eurozone was the world’s biggest importer of oil and 45% of Middle East imports were from Europe. Even major US oil exporters were persuaded to do business in euro. Iraqi oil, two-thirds of which was being snapped up by US companies, could only be traded in euros. It was a political move on the part of the Iraqi government to show that the euro could be a substitute for the dollar in oil trade. The Bank of China and the Russian Central Bank were both rumoured to be waiting for the best moment to increase the holdings of euros. Only 5% of Chinese reserves were held in euros, but more than 20% of its trade was with Europe. Unsurprisingly oil exporters in the Middle East held $700bn of US assets, but comparatively little in Europe. The euro gained “safe haven” status when the dollar lost it. But even before Trump ditched the JCPOA, a year ago China announced that it intended to introduce a new way to trade oil by pricing international oil contracts in Chinese yuan rather than in US dollars, which has been the benchmark currency for pricing commodities. In 2016 the yuan was added to the IMF's special drawing rights basket, joining the US dollar, the euro, the yen, and the British pound. But yuan has not been used for most international transactions. As the dollar is the dominant international currency serving as a vehicle for clearing international payments between banks, it has allowed the US to set rules for transparency and integrity in the global financial system. The US can advance its foreign policy interests by dictating who – both individuals and countries – can and cannot conduct international financial transactions, and US authorities can extend their criminal jurisdiction to investigate and prosecute rogue actors. Global banks have to check they are not doing business with PEPs—Politically Exposed Persons— who are looting their countries and moving sovereign funds into private accounts. The US regulatory and legal system also enforces a variety of sanctions programmes, barring US financial institutions from performing transactions involving pariah states or officials – including Russia, Syria, North Korea and Iran – and blocking any such transactions. The author says, “if the geopolitical shock of Trump’s unilateralism spurs an institutional innovation that makes it easier for European banks and companies to make payments in euros, then the transformation could be swift (as it were). If Iran receives euros rather than dollars for its oil exports, it will use those euros to pay for merchandise imports. With companies elsewhere earning euros rather than dollars, there will be less reason for central banks to hold dollars in order to intervene in the foreign exchange market and stabilize the local currency against the greenback. At this point, there would be no going back.” The reason to set up the euro was “to free Europe from excessive dependence on the dollar. This is likewise one of China’s motivations for seeking to internationalize the renminbi. So far, the success of both efforts has been mixed, at best,” because both yuan and euro do not have a long history as a reserve currency. It certainly takes decades to prove oneself strong and viable.
