Fearing fear

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2008/10/fearing_fear.cfm

WHEN last we discussed James Surowiecki, he was warning the world's headline writers to write accurate headlines, lest they wind up worsening financial conditions. I wish we could stop sayingthingslike “markets swoon on recession fears,” with its implicit assertion that investors are still worrying about whether or not there’s going to be a recession...[T]he problem with saying that investors are only worried about the possibility of a recession is that it implies that the markets haven’t yet fully come to terms with reality. And that, in turn, is likely to make investors even more skittish than they already are...Let it be resolved, then: no more talk about investors fearing a recession. Instead, let’s talk about investors dealing with a recession. Today, Mr Surowiecki broadens his observations on market fear: [O]ur expectations and assumptions will have a tremendous amount to do with what happens over the next couple of years. This is, on the one hand, an obvious point, but in the midst of all this economic chaos, there’s a tendency to assume that a long downturn is a foregone conclusion, dictated by economic reality. But that’s a mistaken assumption. It’s true that many of us are much poorer (much much much poorer) than we were a year ago, and that there are fewer people employed. But the whole point of the Fed flooding the system with liquidity is, in effect, to make sure that the economy has all the money it needs, and to ensure that people with good ideas and good businesses can get the credit they need to invest and grow. If the banking system works as it should (and that is, to be sure, a big if), then that money will find its way to those who need it, and if the past is any indication, they will do good things with it. But this can only work if, in the simplest sense, people think it can work. F.D.R. may not have been right when he said that the only thing we had to fear was fear itself. But he was surely right that fear was something we needed to overcome. I find it interesting that he assumes that we all assume the downturn will be long. My expectation, based on recent economic experience, was that the downturn would be short. The lesson learned during the long Great Moderation, was that we don't do long or deep downturns anymore. We barely do downturns at all. It's also funny to hear Mr Surowiecki say that many of us are much, much poorer than we were a year ago. In fact, many of us are not much poorer than we were a year ago, because many of us were not invested in asset or housing markets as of a year ago. Of course, those of us who are not much poorer than we were a year ago are also barely any richer than we were eight years ago, while those of us that are much poorer than we were one year ago may still be much richer than we were eight years ago. In short, there is an experiential disconnect on the economy. And this disconnect surely influences expectations of the length and depth of the current recession.
