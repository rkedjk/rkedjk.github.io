Will This Jobs Bill Do Any Good?

https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2010/02/25/will-this-jobs-bill-do-any-good/

The Senate passed a jobs bill on Wednesday that, among other things, would give employers a temporary exemption from payroll taxes for hiring people who had previously been out of work for at least 60 days. So far it is being hailed as a major bipartisan success. But political image aside, will it do any good for the job market? The idea of a subsidy for new hires has received support from many economists who study labor issues, and the Congressional Budget Office has said that a payroll tax credit targeting new hires could be a relatively cheap and effective way to stimulate the job market. But the policy proposal that the budget office scored looks pretty different from the one that the Senate just passed. In fact, there are myriad different ways to construct such a credit, and they are not all equally effective at creating jobs, and creating them cheaply. A few elements that an effective formulation of this idea needs to consider are: A balance of simplicity and fairness: The policy needs to be simple enough so that employers can understand it and are interested in taking advantage of it. But on the other hand, a policy that’s too broad and simplistic might encourage employers to game the system — by firing and rehiring their existing staff to claim the credit, for example. Size: How big of a subsidy is the tax credit? Should the policy cut the cost of a new hire by 5 percent? Or 15 percent? Or 30 percent? A bigger subsidy is more expensive for taxpayers (not desirable), but it is more likely to actually entice employers to hire (very desirable). Timing: When employers can get the subsidy makes a difference. A tax that goes unpaid upfront might be more enticing than a tax that gets paid initially, but is then refunded at the end of the year. When the subsidy comes can be especially important if firms are have cash-flow problems. Targeting: Should the tax credit target hiring of low-wage workers? Minorities? Workers who have been jobless for a longer period? Which workers and firms are targeted are questions of both fairness (i.e., do low-wage earners “deserve” more help?) and effectiveness. Daniel Hamermesh at the University of Texas, Austin, for example, says that employers are more price-sensitive with low-wage workers than with high-wage ones, and so a tax cut for hires of people earning below a certain threshold might be more effective. Similarly, a tax credit for hiring the long-term unemployed might seem more “fair” — since this group of people is especially needful of a job — but might not actually encourage much more hiring if employers feel that the condition narrows the pool of eligible job candidates too much. Most of the criticisms I’ve heard from economists about the Senate bill have to do with the size (that the individual tax cuts employers would get may not be big enough, and the overall cost of the program is too small); the simplicity versus fairness issue (that the bill may encourage cheating because credits are given even if new employees are just replacing old ones); and targeting (that the requirement to hire only the long-term unemployed may be an obtrusive condition, though there is disagreement on that point). Timothy J. Bartik, who had helped design an alternative version of the hiring tax credit, summarizes some of these concerns in a piece titled “Not all job creation tax credits are created equal.” On the other hand, I have heard praise about the simplicity of the bill, as well as the timing of the subsidies (because for the most part they are given upfront rather than refunded). Of course even if the job bill were perfectly designed it could still end up worsening the job market if it encouraged employers to delay hiring because they’re waiting for the best possible deal.
