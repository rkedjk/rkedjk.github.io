A. If you actually go and look at the statement it has very nice language that essentially is, “We’re state contingent.” And then it has that second part that has “considerable time” in it. I think that second part focuses people’s attention too much on a time period and that’s why I don’t like that phraseology. In the markets and in the public and in the media it’s been interpreted as a six-month time frame. So to me that doesn’t give us enough flexibility to be responsive to changing conditions if things pick up or if things slow down. Just kind of dropping that out and emphasizing the state contingency part would be fine. More forward looking, I personally think the statement of economic projections isn’t a bad tool because it does show the diversity of views across committee participants. So I think that ‘s a good way of conveying that different forecasts result in different policy paths. It’s not perfect. I know some of my colleagues don’t think it conveys that much information. But we’re also in a period in which we’re changing how we think of forward guidance. At the zero lower bound I think of forward guidance as a tool of policy. You want to change expectations and you want to be much more explicit about the path of policy. As we get to more normal times, I don’t think of forward guidance as a tool for policy; I think of it as a transparency and communications device. We want to basically be as transparent as we can. We want people to know what data we’re looking at and see how it’s moved and be able to infer what we’re going to be doing. Q. But it’s hard to get that information from the economic projections because each official uses their own unspecified assumptions about future policy. A. Pre-crisis I think the public had a pretty good sense of how the Fed would react. Now we’re in a new world and there’s more uncertainty about what the steady state Fed reaction function is going to be, and so I think it’s incumbent on us to try to articulate that as much as we can. But I don’t think that happens over night. I think that takes some time. There’s a lot of things in the economic projections you could think about improving. For one thing, it doesn’t link variables together. If one person is forecasting 3 percent growth, what are they forecasting for employment and what are they forecasting for inflation? You’d like to see that linked up because that’s a way of conveying the reaction function. Q. The House has passed a bill that would require the Fed to publish a policy rule, and to explain any deviations. Wouldn’t that improve transparency? A. Here’s the problem: There isn’t one rule. There isn’t one model. You wouldn’t want to commit to a particular rule because even the ‘robust rule’ literature that says you want to pick rules that work across models, even that doesn’t suggest there’s one rule that would work in all circumstances. I like to look at the rules — I look at a spreadsheet that has all of these rules in it — as information for me when I’m doing my forecast, because I want to see what they say, but I would not feel comfortable picking one rule right now and saying that’s how we’re going to do policy. I don’t think we’re at the state of the art yet of being able to do that. Q. Do you see a difference in the Fed under the leadership of Janet Yellen? A. I think Janet is doing a lot of the things that Ben did in terms of collegiality, letting people voice their views. I don’t see it as a big shift. Of course the economy is in a different place, so the issues we might talk about are different. But I think of it as a continuum in terms of the focus on collegiality and letting people speak, and that’s very good. I think she’s an excellent chair.
