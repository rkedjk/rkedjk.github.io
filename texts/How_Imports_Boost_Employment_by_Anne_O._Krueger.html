How Imports Boost Employment by Anne O. Krueger

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/us-manufacturing-employment-imports-by-anne-krueger-2017-02

In part, this summary misses the point. Displaced workers don't just need a job, they need money. The mythical "golden age" of US manufacturing, exaggerated as it was, and irreversibly gone as it is, did bring not just jobs to unskilled or semi-skilled blue collar workers. It brought negotiating power, and relative increases in standard of living. Those 2 are real in-your-face issues that are consistently ignored by articles such as this. As an engineer, I interact enough with workers, tradespeople, and of course with robots that I think maybe I can see some of this in real-time. In theory, whenever a tedious repetitive job is eliminated by automation, we should all cheer, since a human being is now going to be spared the drudgery of that task (and if technology is properly applied, quality may increase too). But ! Taking that same person who used to do that now worthless task, and celebrating by throwing them out on the street, is not the happy vision of technological advance. This is completely and utterly ignored. They're not going to be retrained as a computer programmer. Maybe if they're not too old they can go sell cel phones at the mall. The obvious solution is to reduce the hours worked by the same class of worker, while keeping pay the same. This solution is resisted tooth and nail. (interestingly, the Obama administration actually finally did do this with an alteration to overtime rules, but it was shot down in the courts). Also, in ADDITION to automation -- A whole lot of US manufacturing supply chain did in fact move to Mexico and China, not in the 1970s but in the 1990s. Other than medical or high precision stuff, high volume manufacturing processes are designed from the beginning to be BOTH outsourced and automated. And for exactly the reason critics cite. Evidence of this is everywhere - pop open any mass produced product and find the labor intensive parts -- electronic and electrical assemblies, anything with lots of parts - and look at the country of origin. The operations workers involved are now a pretty small percentage of the US (and western europe) and decreasing. But the overseers of the process are leaving too - there are perfectly good engineers in Brazil or India. Perfectly good lawyers and economists too, by the way. And the same in robot-land. The effect is indistinguishable from the dilemma of automation. We should celebrate the freedom from having to do repetitive tasks. But seeing all the benefits go to one group while another group is not allowed to share is bound to cause problems. Ones which cannot be smoothed over with the power of positive thinking. Redistribution, or reduction of hours worked per person, however, would go a long way to help for real.
