Is Plutocracy Really the Problem? by J. Bradford DeLong

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/krugman-plutocracy-economic-policy-by-j-bradford-delong-2019-07

Extreme concentration of wealth/power is both symptom and cause. We should include not just individuals but corp's, by the way. Diverging top vs bottom wealth and income - also both symptom and cause. There is a self-reinforcing feedback, and the author correctly calls out many of the elements. Rightward ideological shift, dominance of neoliberalism, absence of competing voices. For the latter there was once the role played by labor unions domestically, and socialism internationally. This is fixable in principle, but probably we will end up with a pretty bad democratically elected right-populist government before any such socialist awakening would happen. It would be nice if there was a way to avoid that historical path. In the US at least, the political system itself is also part of the unfortunate self-reinforcing feedback. The electoral system is set up for an equilibrium of two parties, (anti-proportional representation, single-seat winner take all, etc) and this means alternative voices on the left and right are locked out at the general election level. This is actually fixable too, but at the state rather than national level. I have to think such a state level tweak (PR + RCV), to let in third parties, is a prerequisite to addressing the problem of big$$/business-ideology influence rising to a level where they can successfully lock out discussion of alternatives. It's a long shot -- PR+RCV is a complicated thing to explain and it would take radical campaign of mass awareness, though two states already did it. Once in place, a whole new party structure would be built and it's no guarantee by itself ... plenty of EU countries have pluralistic multi-party political systems, and they are still hit and miss. Necessary condition, not sufficient condition. Another electoral system feature is the geographic effect, with a large bonus given to small states, while simultaneously their population is self-selected (by emigration) to be more conservative and vulnerable to manipulation. Not that coastal/urban populations aren't vulnerable to manipulation in their own way. This is not fixable, for historical / constitutional reasons. Constitutional amendment to abolish electoral college is not happening (and also, wouldn't address big$$ influence IMO).
