Are the numbers wrong?

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2011/03/manufacturing

EZRA KLEIN links to a new study of sorts by economist Michael Mandel which suggests that America's datakeepers are getting something seriously wrong. According to Mr Mandel, American data do not take into account the discount implicit in cheap imports or the import content of American exports. The figures may therefore be overstating American productivity gains and understating the trouble in American industry. It sounds bad. At the heart of the complaint lies a paradox. On the one hand: Domestic and international statistical agencies put forth a vast array of economic data each month—but virtually none of the data directly support the propositions that the United States is falling behind other countries in competitiveness or being significantly hurt by trade. In other words, the official economic statistics do not confirm the starting negative premise for either the competitiveness worriers or the trade skeptics. And on the other...well, people seem to worry about competitiveness and are sceptical of the benefits of trade. Faced with the need to square these two pieces of information, Mr Mandel suggests that the data are wrong. Honestly, I'm not sure why. The data appear to be perfectly consistent with a story in which rising manfacturing productivity displaces workers while output and costs fall. If those workers aren't reabsorbed elsewhere, that could be due to too-tight monetary policy rather than bad data. And I suspect that you could go to a city with no manufacturing history and tight labour markets and still hear people fret about lagging competitiveness and the trouble with trade. I should think we'd want some evidence apart from public impressions that the data are amiss. If it's there, I'm missing it. Mr Mandel also acknowledges that overstated productivity would mean that the American economy was closer to inflation than previously believed. But does this really square with recent data? In 1999, manufacturing employment was around 17.5m, a typical level for the period from the 1960s through the 1990s. Thereafter, employment fell sharply; it's currently around 11.6m. For most of the past decade, both interest rates and inflation have been extremely low. Indeed, the Fed was worried about deflation in the early 2000s. The Fed then stood on the gas by dropping rates to 1%, manufacturing employment briefly leveled off, and the economy grew at more than 3% in 2004 and 2005. And yet annual inflation hugged 2% through this period. If the Fed was really goosing the manufacturing sector beyond potential growth, why didn't prices rise faster? Again, this looks to me like a case in which the Fed was reluctant to take additional action once rates fell to 1%, and it therefore failed to facilitate the rehiring of manufacturing workers displaced by productivity gains. I may have Mr Mandel wrong, and I'll be interested to see more from him on this topic. At the same time, I'm a little confused by his argument for why we should care about these potential mismeasurements: Note that the defense industrial base is the manufacturing output of the U.S. economy, short-term and long-term, if some portion of global supply chains were cut. The size of the defense industrial base depends on knowing the domestic productivity of U.S. manufacturing, rather than the supply chain productivity. And that, in turn, depends on knowing the discount... And finally, data on discounts by industry will be particularly useful for assessing state-level economic development. After we have adjusted for the correct price of imports, which industries in a state are true job creators and which ones merely assemble imported components? Equally important, which industries have a small discount, so are potentially competitive against imports. And perhaps more interesting, state economic development agencies can identify industries where the discount is closing because of cost increases overseas. This data could drive an import recapture strategy of assisting domestic industries that are potentially competitive against imports. Regarding the first paragraph, is this really a concern? America's military is already an unparalleled force. Mr Mandel seems to be arguing that despite this, Americans must be sure that they're capable of far greater mobilisations. Why? In the event of all-out conventional warfare with China? Doesn't that seem incredibly unlikely for a whole host of reasons? And second, Mr Mandel wants state development agencies to try and recapture imports from foreign countries. Again, why? How can these state agencies be sure that they wouldn't be better off preparing workers for jobs in other industries? How confident is Mr Mandel that this wouldn't lead to terrible waste or result in interstate competition? Why should consumers be asked to make up the difference in cost through (one assumes) subsidies or import tariffs? Is such a strategy even legal? And why, above all else, shouldn't we expect simple retaliation, which would negate local efforts to recruit industry? Perhaps there's something to this mismeasurement argument, but I'm not convinced and not sure I want to find out what the proposed policy reponse would be.
