Not Enough Skin in the Game

https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2010/05/27/not-enough-skin-in-the-game/?partner=rss&emc=rss

On Wednesday, I mentioned four big issues to watch as the House and Senate try to put together a final financial regulation bill. But, obviously, there are more than four issues that matter. David A. Levine — an investor and economist in New York, formerly with Sanford C. Bernstein — offers this additional idea in an e-mail message: There is a fifth way to improve the financial reform bill that has received scant notice. Both the House and Senate versions are almost identical on this point, because the authors of the pertinent section were evidently not familiar with current industry practice. The bills require that the packagers of asset-backed securities that fail to meet certain underwriting standards (which have yet to be specified) retain ownership of 5 percent of those securities. The basic idea is that by requiring issuers of subprime securities to have some “skin in the game” (a phrase used in the Senate bill summary), they will have the incentive to be more careful underwriters. The reason the provision will be ineffective (and this probably explains why the industry has not fought this particular provision) is that issuers of subprime securities have long had the practice of not only retaining (roughly) a 5 percent ownership stake, but they made that 5 percent stake the most junior part of the structure. What I mean by junior, of course, is that the issuers who retained 5 percent would have to absorb 100 percent of the first 5 percent of losses. The current House and Senate legislation would require them to absorb only 5 percent of the first 5 percent of the losses — 1/20th of the burden that was already in place because of industry practice. The reason, incidentally, that issuers were willing to keep the most junior 5 percent is that it enabled them to structure deals that would be incredibly profitable to them — so profitable, that even if the deals went bust and they had to absorb the 5 percent loss, their lifetime profit and loss on each deal was guaranteed to be a meaningful plus. The upshot is that the current legislation will have no impact on underwriting standards, since the issuers themselves already require more onerous terms of themselves than the legislation would impose on them. The way the 5 percent retention promoted both the issuance of subprime mortgage-backed securities and immense profitability for the issuers is this: By promising to absorb the first 5 percent of losses, a large package of shaky mortgages was deemed to have enough protection to be able to earn high ratings from Standard and Poor’s and Moody’s. As a result, the average yield that needed to be paid to investors was typically less than 35 basis points over Libor [a benchmark interest rate]. Since the mortgages themselves could yield 3 percent or more over Libor, all of that excess profit went to the packagers of the securities The solution, in briefest outline, would be for packagers to be required to retain much more: 20 percent of all subprime packages and 10 percent percent of those packages that meet the specified (presumably prime) underwriting standards. Importantly, the packager should be required to stand first in line to absorb the losses.
