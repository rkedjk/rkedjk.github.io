“I would say there was even a more compelling case for some Asian countries to have kept low interest rates than for what we are doing,” argued Mr. Stiglitz. Asian countries might have been able to lower interest rates and recover if immediately repaying foreign creditors in full hadn’t been such an imperative. Some afflicted countries could have afforded more public spending to mitigate the impact of the financial crises because they had healthier budget balances than the United States has today. The economic differences between the Asian crises and the subprime mess are real. Still, Mr. Stiglitz argues that the main reason behind the diametrically opposed approaches to crisis resolution is political rather than economic. Of course, these countries didn’t have a choice if they wanted help from the I.M.F. The United States, which has a much larger economy and borrows from abroad in its own currency, hasn’t had to ask for outside help. The response to the American subprime mortgage crisis is being crafted within the American political system where policy makers are inevitably much more sensitive to domestic social and political consequences. That is also why economists  and some government officials  can contemplate even more unorthodox approaches, including subsidizing banks and mortgage holders. Beyond the issue of fairness, there is an irony to the disparate approaches to financial troubles in the North and South. One of the consequences of the I.M.F.’s harsh medicine was that governments in developing countries vowed never again to rely on the fund and set out to hoard piles of foreign reserves in case a crisis were to strike again. Flooding back into Treasury bills, this cash has contributed to keep American interest rates at rock bottom and fueled a bubble of reckless mortgage lending. That bubble is now bursting.
