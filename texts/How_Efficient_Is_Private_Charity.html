How Efficient Is Private Charity?

https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2011/01/14/how-efficient-is-private-charity/

Uwe E. Reinhardt is an economics professor at Princeton. Americans tend to view themselves as the most charitable people in the world, as many presidents proclaim with eloquence whenever the occasion arises. That judgment is based on what we report as “private charitable giving” as a portion of gross domestic product, although, as I noted last week, the bulk of that giving is not charitable in the sense that it benefits the poor and needy and a good fraction of it involves tax-financed transfers from government to private charities. Canadians or Europeans might disagree with our felicitous self-assessment, especially if official foreign development assistance as a percentage of G.D.P. is included in calculations of charity. Although in absolute dollar terms the United States ranks high in that category as well, as a percentage of G.D.P. many European nations outrank us (see Table 1, Annex A, on page 7). Citizens of other countries may remind us that there is a trade-off between channeling dollars from citizens to charitable or civic activities through the government’s budget and channeling these funds through the budgets of private organizations that we label charitable, whether they truly support charitable or civic activities. Many charitable or civic activities financed in the United States with private giving are financed elsewhere through government — health care, education and museums among them. Why do Americans make so different a trade-off between private charity and government than people in most other nations? One persuasive reason is that through private charitable giving, the donor can direct where his or her funds go. Americans do not trust their government as much as citizens elsewhere seem to. Yet it is not always clear in whose pockets private charitable donations end up. A second reason is that many Americans have the notion that private charities are more efficient than government can ever be. My experience is that to many Americans this notion, which is nothing more than a hypothesis, is an axiom, a statement so self-evident that it does not require proof. The relative efficiency of private “charity” and tax-financed governmental “charity” is an empirical question. The proper criterion is what fraction of our charitable donations actually flows directly to the activities that we seek to support. Ideally, we would have a standard metric by which we could define units of the actual activity we seek to support and then quantify the total volume of activity produced by a charity in a given year – e.g., number of patient seen in a clinic or number of students granted scholarships or the dollars of direct grants to the targets of our compassion, and so on. With such a measure, we could meaningfully compare organizations in terms of the total revenue from donations received per unit of the desirable activity delivered. It is reasonable to suppose that charitable organizations would vary substantially in their performance. For one, there are unavoidable administrative costs, which are probably subject to significant economies of scale. Thus, there is apt to be considerable variation across charities in the ratio of administrative costs to total revenue from donors. Among these administrative costs are fund-raising, whether done in-house or subcontracted to fund-raising companies. Unfortunately, in the keen competition for charitable dollars among the more than one million private charities in the United States, fund-raising alone can chew up large fractions of total funds raised, particularly for smaller charities. As Charity Navigator, a well-known independent charity evaluator, reports on charities using outside fund-raisers: “Of the six states reporting aggregate data, none reported charities receiving more than 59 percent of the donations raised on their behalf, and five showed average returns to charity below 50 percent.” The charts in that report give one pause, as do the statistics for particular charities mentioned in the report. Particularly troubling are some – though by no means all — of the charities aimed at assisting veterans of the armed forces. Such charities have enormous emotional appeal, especially during times of war, and that emotion can be exploited. In his 2008 report “Scrooges and Angels,” Mark Hammond, secretary of state of South Carolina, listed charities that spent more than 98 percent of their funds on programs, rather than administration and fund-raising (the angels) and those that spend less than 40 percent on programs (the Scrooges). Two charities for veterans were among the worst performers. The use of funds by some veterans’ charities was the focus of Congressional hearings in 2007 and 2008, with some astounding disclosures. One charity, the Coalition to Support America’s Heroes, paid $100,000 to retired Gen. Tommy Franks, who led the invasion of Iraq, for allowing his name to be used on the letterhead of a solicitation letter, a Congressional committee said. A spokesman for General Franks said at the time that the general also made several speeches for the charity and ended his support “when he learned that the percentage of money raised that was going to the troops was less than 85 percent. These extreme cases are hardly representative of the universe of American charitable organizations, including charities aimed at the military and veterans. Many of these are admirable and eminently worthy of our support. I merely wish to register two points on this topic. 1. Even if we leave aside that much of private charitable giving is matched by sizable tax-financed government subsidies, it is not clear that private charities are invariably the most efficient way to support a desired activity, in terms of the conceptual metric I proposed above. It is entirely an empirical question, whose answer is likely to vary from case to case. 2. The label of “charitable organization” embraces a wide range of organizations that range, as Mr. Hammond said, from “Scrooges to angels.” And among the Scrooges may well be more than a few scoundrels. So how is one to know the angels from the Scrooges? A number of independent charity evaluators are available, among them Charity Navigator, which rates 5,300 organizations; the Better Business Bureau Wise Giving Alliance, rating about 1,200; the American Institute of Philanthropy, rating about 500, and GuideStar, which does not rate organizations but provides financial information on them, albeit some of it for a sizable fee. Unfortunately, rating charities objectively and fairly is much harder than one might imagine. Charity Navigator does a good job of explaining the complexity of the task. That evaluator assigns scores to each of a number of factors, including the fraction of donations actually spent on program expenses (distinct from administrative and fund-raising expenses). It then aggregates these scores and converts them into a star system. (This is analogous to assigning students one letter grade for all of the possibly varied performance on a number of tests and assignments done during a semester.) It is not surprising that, from time to time, individual charities feel themselves unfairly treated under such a rating system. For that reason, one should explore alternative charity evaluators for particular charities, to the extent alternative ratings are available.
