Initial Coin Scams by Nouriel Roubini

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/ico-cryptocurrency-scams-by-nouriel-roubini-2018-05

I lost all credibility for the author when he stated that Jay Clayton made it clear that he regards all cryptocurrencies as securities with the exception of Bitcoin. This is not even taking something out of context, it is purely false. He stated that all of the ICOs that he has personally looked at likely are securities and that each case depends on a facts and circumstances test under Howey. He also said some may start out as securities and then evolve to not being securities. It is also false that most cryptocurrencies are not subject to KYC and AML. These are now being implemented by exchanges and many of the recent ICOs have sold only to accredited investors with full KYC compliance. If the statistic of only 8% of cryptocurrencies get listed on exchanges is true (I doubt that it is since there are many less reputable exchanges who are more than willing to break the law) it is because the exchanges are unregistered and only list cryptocurrencies that they believe are not going to be deemed securities, not because the project has failed. If a startup raises money through VC’s like Uber but does not trade on an exchange, it does not mean it has failed. Other more reputable studies have suggested the number of frauds is only around 20% not 81% (still extraordinarily high). A significantly higher percent will fail because of incompetence, not fraud. I do agree though that most ICOs were structured purely to attempt to circumvent the securities laws which has gone down significantly due to more guidance from the SEC and CFTC and most did/are currently in violation of the laws. However, comparing them to public companies doesn’t make sense since they are more similar to private funds that fall under exemptions and do not have many reporting obligations. The benefits are a way to store value just like other commodities such as gold, no inflation or impact from monetary policies, greater security, almost instantaneous transfer anywhere in the world as opposed to waiting a week or more for a bank wire, and saving banks billions of dollars by no longer needing to act as a middle-man to verify transactions. This is why most banks are creating their own and heavily looking into this. Contrary to perception, the transactions are also easier to trace than current methods, unless Monroe is involved, which makes AML enforcement and prosecutions easier. The real benefits are the underlying blockchain technology which will impact all facets of life going forward, unrelated to cryptocurrency which was merely the first use of the technology.
