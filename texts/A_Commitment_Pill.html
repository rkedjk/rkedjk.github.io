A Commitment Pill?

https://judson.blogs.nytimes.com/2008/09/16/a-commitment-pill/

A couple of weeks ago, the arginine vasopressin receptor 1a gene sprang into notoriety: in a just-published study of Swedish couples, variation in this gene was found to be associated with difficulties, for men, in maintaining long-term monogamous relationships. Which suggests the following mischievous thought: could such restlessness be cured one day? First, some background. Among mammals, the sort of lifestyle that many 21st century humans idealize and strive for — a man and a woman living together for decades in a committed, sexually faithful relationship, rearing children together — is rare. Fewer than 5 percent of mammal species have evolved even an approximation of it. (The approximation is a couple living together as a social arrangement — so-called “social monogamy.” They may or may not be sexually monogamous as well.) Examples of the socially monogamous? They include Kirk’s dik-dik, a small African antelope; the fat-tailed dwarf lemur, a small primate from Madagascar; the prairie vole, a North American rodent; some human beings. Monogamy evolves when stable couples are more successful at rearing offspring than, say, a female on her own, or a family group. Judging by the low frequency of monogamy in nature, this is rarely the case. (A situation where the father does all the work of rearing young, though common in fish and frogs and occasionally found in birds, is more or less absent in mammals for the obvious reason that females must give the young milk after birth.) However, when monogamy is a successful strategy — when monogamous couples do tend to have more surviving offspring than fickler types — any genes involved in promoting the behavior will begin to spread. So it wouldn’t be surprising if the (in)ability to form attachments to other individuals has a genetic underpinning of some sort. A full understanding of the genetics of attachment is still some way off. But one of the genes that has already been implicated is the arginine vasopressin receptor 1a gene. Arginine vasopressin is a hormone that’s involved in a variety of behaviors. In general, it’s more important in males than in females: in many male mammals, vasopressin helps mediate activities such as aggressive posturing, scent marking of territories, courtship and sex. Vasopressin receptors, then, are molecules in the brain that respond specifically to vasopressin; genes for the receptors thus influence the effects the hormone will have. The most famous studies of these receptors have been done on voles. Prairie voles often mate for life, and both parents look after the offspring. Once a couple has formed — a process that involves lots of sex — the pair spend much of their time huddling together and grooming each other. (Sex releases vasopressin.) Meadow voles and montane voles don’t form stable partnerships; instead, both males and females pursue a more promiscuous lifestyle. Rather than grooming a sex partner, a male montane vole is likely to groom himself. A large part of the difference in behavior between the two species appears to be due — in males, at least — to differences in the vasopressin 1a receptor gene. Differences in the gene seem to lead to differences in the way the receptor is distributed in the brain, and thus to where vasopressin acts. Intriguingly, laboratory mice — which are not normally monogamous — that have been genetically engineered to have the prairie vole version of the gene become interested in huddling and cuddling with a partner. Normal lab mice don’t do this. More intriguing still, inserting the prairie vole version of the gene into the brains of adult male meadow voles alters their behavior. (The gene was inserted into a region of the brain called the ventral pallidum; the insertion resulted in an increased density of vasopressin receptors.) In an experiment where male meadow voles were allowed to spend 24 hours with a receptive female, and were then allowed to choose between spending time with the “old” female or with a new, unfamiliar female, males who had received a dose of the prairie vole gene preferred to consort with their old partner. Males who hadn’t, didn’t. In other words, getting the prairie vole gene had a strong effect on the meadow voles — and allowed males who would not normally form an attachment to do so. In humans, the vasopressin receptor 1a gene comes in a number of forms. In the Swedish study, men who had two copies of one particular form of the gene — the variant known as RS3 334 — were less likely to be married, and more likely to report difficulties in their relationships, than other men. Their partners were also more likely to report relationship difficulties. An experiment suggests itself. It would be fascinating to see whether inserting a “regular” vasopressin receptor gene into the brains of men who have two copies of the “difficulties” variant would ameliorate their problems, insofar as they have them. It would be fascinating to test the extent to which different forms of a single gene affect a man’s behavior. (What about women? That’s a big subject: I’ll take it up another time.) At the moment, of course, such an experiment would be unethical. Too little is known about what the gene does in humans, or what other behavioral differences the different forms of the gene may cause. Nor is this gene the whole story. Other genes play a role in the biology of attachment, and differences in environment, especially in childhood, matter too.
