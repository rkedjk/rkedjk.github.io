Worthwhile Canadian Initiative: Canada's housing starts are following the recovery script

http://worthwhile.typepad.com/worthwhile_canadian_initi/2009/12/canadas-housing-starts-are-following-the-recovery-script.html

The rebound in the sales and prices of houses in Canada has been making quite a few headlines. That's not bad news, but for those of us looking for signs of recovery, what really matters is whether or not it is being translated into real economic activity. Calculated Risk noted awhile ago that recoveries typically start with a rebound in residential investment. What makes the US recovery atypical is the excess inventory left over from their housing boom: even though the US housing market has picked up, it will still be a while before housing construction becomes a source of economic growth. But Canada is not the US: we're pretty much following the standard script. Low interest rates have driven down borrowing costs, and since we don't have the same overhang in housing, housing starts have increased more than 30% from the trough in April: And this is also showing up in construction employment, which has increased over the past three months: I put the US series in that graph because the contrast is just so startling. One point I suppose I should address is the possibility that we're seeing the beginning of a housing bubble. This isn't something I'm inclined to worry about just now. We're trying to crawl out of a recession, and housing construction is exactly the sort of economic activity that we're looking for. I don't see the point in trying to stamp out a recovery after going through so much trouble to get it started. I may change my mind about this point in a year or two, but that will be because we'll have put the recession behind us.
