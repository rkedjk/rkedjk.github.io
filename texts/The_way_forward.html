The way forward

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2012/08/monetary-policy-0

I'VE been moaning all summer about the world's central bankers, twiddling their thumbs while disaster looms. They haven't exactly done nothing, however. Rather, central bankers have responded to changing circumstances with what a colleague of mine has called "open mouth operations", reinforcing the point that when conditions deteriorate enough some action will be forthcoming. There is a central bank put across the economy, such that markets know to expect action under certain circumstances and respond by placing a floor under sagging markets. These open-mouth operations seem, for now, to have halted the second-quarter swoon, in America at any rate. A brief recap of the recent economic timeline is in order. America's economy was relatively well positioned to start the year, and both markets and real economic variables looked fairly hale in the calm generated by the European Central Bank's €1 trillion bank-lending plan. From late March, however, backsliding began. Yields on the European periphery began rising again. Treasury yields began sinking again. Equity and commodity prices and inflation expectations dropped, and the real economic data began turning up disappointing datapoints. Things came to a head in early June, punctuated by a shockingly poor employment report from America. Then some central bankers began dropping heavy hints of additional action (including the Fed), while others, like the People's Bank of China, took concrete easing steps. Expectations found a bottom, which shows up clearly in the S&P 500 and 2-year breakevens. Open-mouth operations were enough to convince markets that doom wasn't imminent. Of course, headwinds continued to blow. Growth in several large emerging markets looked quite fragile in early summer. Between Greek elections and Spanish banks, the euro crisis continued to boil. Lower inflation expectations dampened American investment and growth, and manufacturing activity slowed across the world. By the middle of July, it looked as though a month of sideways movement could fall back into a new round of panic. Then central-banker tongues began wagging once again. More hints dropped concerning firming support for additional Fed action, but the biggest mouth of all was Mario Draghi's, whose "it will be enough" speech sent markets on a tear. Since then, equities have risen sharply and peripheral bond yields are way down. The reversal shows up very clearly in 10-year breakevens; Mr Draghi apparently gave markets hope that the next decade wouldn't be completely miserable. Europe's crisis is far from resolved, and there is always the possibility that panic will return and the whole thing will come undone, dragging down large swathes of the global economy. With this latest reprieve, however, the American economy is actually in a relatively salutary position, thanks mostly to the long-awaited recovery in housing markets. Construction has been way too low for way too long. Vacancies and inventory have been tumbling and rents and—finally—prices are rising. New construction is beginning to pick up, and that will shortly translate into rising construction employment growth. The renewed rise in inflation expectations is particularly useful in this dynamic. It reduces the real interest rate for everyone paying fixed rates, and it helps clear markets where nominal prices remain too high. That should support further accumulation of economic momentum which will support inflation expectations and so on, in a virtuous loop. Now the Fed can, will, and should step in to slow this process at some point. The trillion dollar question is just what that point should be. With inflation now running at or a bit below 2%, there is a little room for accelerating activity before hitting the Fed's target, but not much. The question then becomes precisely how much above-target inflation the Fed is willing to tolerate. If the answer is next to none, then America's new, housing-led recovery will proceed at the pace to which we've become accustomed. Given a 2% inflation ceiling, more of a demand contribution from one corner of the economy simply replaces or crowds out contributions from other corners of the economy. You might ask just how that's supposed to work, given that no one expects the Fed to actively tighten policy anytime soon. Federal fund futures contracts provide one glimpse. At the moment, according to the Fed's most recent policy statement, the central bank: ...currently anticipates that economic conditions--including low rates of resource utilization and a subdued outlook for inflation over the medium run--are likely to warrant exceptionally low levels for the federal funds rate at least through late 2014. This language is meant to be stimulative, by encouraging markets to feel comfortable that rates will be very low for a very long time and by helping to bring down long-term rates, which are just compounded short rates plus a term premium. But this is not an unconditional promise. Rates will be low if conditions warrant. And markets firmly believe that one condition which would quickly un-warrant those low rates is inflation above 2%. And so we observe some very instructive movements in fed fund futures prices, which can be used to determine market expectations of future federal fund rates. At the beginning of 2012, for instance, the December 2014 contract indicated that the Fed's policy rate could be as high as 0.75% by that date. As the economy strengthened over the course of the first quarter, the price changed, signalling in late March that the rate might actually be 1% in December of 2014. After months of deteriorating conditions, the expected rate fell back to near 0.25%, but since late July it has been rising once again. Looking across contracts, it appeared in March as if markets expected that the first rate increase could come as early as late 2013, despite the Fed's language. By mid-July, the date of the first firming was pushed back to October of 2014. After the recent rally, markets now put the date at roughly April of 2014. Put simply, markets don't see the Fed's language as a commitment to keep rates low despite above-target inflation. Rather, as inflation rises, the date at which markets expect interest rates to begin rising moves closer to the present. That, in turn, has a direct effect on the outlook in interest-rate sensitive industries—like housing. As I see it, this is a major reason why the most stimulative policy the Fed could adopt at the September meeting would be a promise to tolerate above-target inflation for a year or two so long as unemployment remained elevated. New QE is unlikely to help much while markets see the 2% target as a serious constraint. And if 2% weren't a binding short-term constraint QE wouldn't be necessary, so long as markets took the Fed at its word when it promised to allow a bit more inflation. Temporarily higher inflation would answer a lot of the monetary policy concerns people now voice. Given a minimal need to pair a short-term change in target with new asset purchases, the financial market distortions that could accompany a larger balance sheet wouldn't be much of a risk. Time inconsistency wouldn't be a problem either; Ben Bernanke might not be able to enforce a particular interest rate promise beyond 2014, but he can certainly accept more inflation now. The only serious worry would be the threat of "runaway" inflation. It is remarkable how view monetary economists see this as a serious risk, however. The experience of the past few decades leaves little doubt that a determined central bank can rein in inflation; if anything, they've often been too successful in doing so. Give the Fed credit; since the end of the recession, it has proven capable and willing to prevent a relapse into recession and deflation. The Bernanke put is proving its worth. Yet is has also (wittingly or not I can't say) kept a tight lid on recovery, preventing catch-up growth for fear of inflationary side effects. As Tim Duy notes here, the recovery has been notable for the remarkable stability in both output and employment growth. There are short term fluctuations, but both have on average kept within a narrow, Fed-maintained band centred on a trend line with a Fed-approved slope. We'd all love to see the slope of those lines change. That simply will not occur without a change in the Fed's tolerance for inflation.
