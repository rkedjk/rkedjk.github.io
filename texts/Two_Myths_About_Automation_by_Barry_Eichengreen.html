"So here’s an idea. Instead of a “tax reform” that allows firms to expense their capital outlays immediately, why not give companies tax credits for the cost of providing lifelong learning to their employees?" --------------------------------------------------- Good idea. The challenge, currently, is that any 'life-long learning' ("3L") tax credit received would reduce the salary and wages expense. For example, a payroll expense of $10 million plus 3L of $1 Million would result in taxable income of: $10 M + $1 M - 3L costs (say) $1 M = $10 M = no change Of course, tax reform may incent companies to invest in employees skills development and training, including unincorporated entities that employ the majority of employees, but the issue of lost productivity of those involved in training for new skills would entail two costs - actual costs for skills training and temporal productivity loss and flight loss - the loss of an employee trained with marketable skills that attract higher compensation from other employer prospects. We can construct the policy, methods and tax reform required to improve productivity to mitigate lost producvity and skills development costs, without a net benefit to the employer that incurs expenses for training the application of employee 3L tax credits would be less under current tax reform than on status quo tax rules. How so? If you reduce tax rates, each dollar of expense would receive less tax refund or return (yield) than under a higher tax rate. I think a better idea would be to tax on the basis of revenue by industry sector - i.e.) processing, manufacturing and assembly versus services and banking industries. Apply 3L as a tax-free subsidy to allow employers free money to develop new employee skills.
