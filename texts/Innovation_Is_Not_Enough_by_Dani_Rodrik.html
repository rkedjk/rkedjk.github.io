Innovation Is Not Enough by Dani Rodrik

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/innovation-impact-on-productivity-by-dani-rodrik-2016-06

I wish I had any faith in GDP and productivity figures. I know I think that inegalitarianism in the traditional sense of the word is not the issue. Krugman only speaks for the top 5% who are jealous of the top .1 of 1%. Average people don't mind if the rich get richer. They worry about stagnation in their own living standards. The income distribution that average people worry about is that near them. They want better paying jobs above them that they can aspire to, and they want to be rewarded for working harder and being more skilled than those below. They want well-paying jobs for their children if they go to college. Trump and Sanders will have made a real contribution if they have taught the Establishment (including economists) why people are angry. The Establishment, including economists, have very thick skulls and still has no idea. Obamacare is a disaster because it benefitted only the poor and, as Schumer recognizes, not the middle income. The minimum wage is a political disaster. $15 an hour means $600 a week and $30,000 a year. Two illegals, a husband and wife, working at McDonalds earn $60,000. I know a person at $15 now as asst. manager of a fast food place. She is absolutely convinced that if her near minimum wage coworkers get $15, hers will rise only marginally. She is angry at the thought. So is the white working class who now vote for Trump and Sanders and are extremely likely to elect Trump. Those in the middle must at least get cheap Canadian health care. We must move away from supply side taxation that only encourages the highly innovative sections and those already benefitting from them. And what developing country economists such as Rodrik must worry about is that the US must seriously limit legal and illegal immigration, including work permits. It must change a supply-demand relationship in labor in which the supply of labor through immigration and outsourcing is infinite. Or maybe outsourcing can be made palatable by taxing foreign earnings for something specific like health care. But the political pressures in the West are enormous and a wide range of European parties are going to copy Trump and Sanders. Those who have seen the Dow go from 750 in 1982 to 18,000 while wages stagnate are saying "Let them eat cake" when this is 1785 in France and 1928 in Germany. We, for sure, need a Trump now. But what are the implications for the developing world and the peculiarities of technology there today? What are the examples of Trump going to do to their politics. Developing economists have a lot to think about.
