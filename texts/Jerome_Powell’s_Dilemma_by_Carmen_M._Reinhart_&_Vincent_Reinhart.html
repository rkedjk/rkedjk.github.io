Jerome Powell’s Dilemma by Carmen M. Reinhart & Vincent Reinhart

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/federal-reserve-powell-policy-dilemma-by-carmen-reinhart-and-vincent-reinhart-2019-09

Carmen M. Reinhart and Vincent Reinhart are right about Jerome Powell having a “haunted look.” As Trump is really worried about the economy, which is showing signs of instability, he has been blasting the Federal Reserve. Powell is frustrated because he has to cut interest rate to boost the economy ahead of next year’s presidential election, knowing Trump is good at looking for villains to blame. In August amid recession fears, Trump tweeted: “My only question is, who is our bigger enemy, Jay Powell or Chairman Xi?” Even after the Federal Reserve has made a modest interest rate cut today, Trump slammed the central bank for “having no guts,” because he has been pressuring Powell to drop rates to zero. Trump has insisted that most problems with the economy were squarely the Fed’s fault. In response Powell said the ongoing trade war with China was one of the issues that was weighing on growth, without singling out Trump for causing the uncertainty. His priority is trying to help keep the economy growing without spurring inflation or strangling growth. The authors highlight the danger of caving to political pressure from Trump to cut interest rate “faster” than his European counterparts. “That instruction is not contained in the Federal Reserve Act.” But Powell’s “problem is that the US economy apparently does not require such stimulus. Job market remains robust, and wages are picking up. Global trade may be in recession, but the US economy is not as dependent” as its partners on trading globally. For years, White House officials had largely avoided commenting directly on the Fed’s monetary policy decisions, seeking to preserving the central bank’s independence and credibility. Trump has shattered that norm and even asked aides whether he could replace Powell. The authors say, the Fed “is supposed to deliver maximum employment and stable prices. Its mandate of sustainable economic growth thus requires Powell to attempt to offset the effects of policy uncertainty under Trump.” But on July 31, for the first time in more than a decade, the Fed lowered short-term interest rates. Today it cut again for the second time. Critics said Powell and his colleagues needed to stand up to Trump and do what is right for the economy. The Fed’s decision to keep capital cheap and plentiful made sense after the financial crisis. It allowed companies and consumers to get the money they needed to boost the economy. There are serious downsides to dropping interest rates that are already low, posing dangers to individual and institutional investors who feel compelled to buy stocks because they can not get good returns from safe, conservative investments such as US Treasury securities. When the market downturn comes these debt investors who felt forced into buying stocks will pay a heavy price for their risk taking. Not to ignore are what the close-to-zero rates have on the Social Security system and on pension funds. The lower that rates on Treasury securities fall, the less money Social Security makes on its $2.9 trillion trust fund, which consists entirely of Treasury securities. Besides, a quarter-point or half-point cut by the Fed will hardly generate job-creating investments by companies that are uncertain about the future because of trade wars, and Trump’s disastrous policies that risk to destabilise the global economy.
