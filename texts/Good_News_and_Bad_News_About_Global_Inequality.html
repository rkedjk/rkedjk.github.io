Good News and Bad News About Global Inequality

https://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/johncassidy/2014/01/good-and-bad-news-about-global-inequality.html

While much of the economics profession spent the weekend in Philadelphia discussing Larry Summers’s “secular-stagnation” thesis and other issues at the annual meeting of the American Economic Association, I was reading Angus Deaton’s new book, “The Great Escape: Health, Wealth, and the Origins of Inequality.” This is how Chapter 1 starts: Life is better now than at almost any time in history. More people are richer and fewer people live in dire poverty. Lives are longer and parents no longer routinely watch a quarter of their children die. Deaton, a Princeton economist who is renowned for his meticulous approach, has spent decades studying health, inequality, and economic development. During the past thirty or forty years, he reminds us, rapid rates of economic growth in parts of the developing world, particularly China and India, have dragged many, many people out of abject poverty. According to the World Bank, which is the recognized authority in this area (although some critics question its methods), between 1981 and 2008, the number of people living on the equivalent of a dollar a day fell by about seven hundred and fifty million—and this at a time when the over-all population in low-to-middle-income countries has been growing rapidly. Consequently, Deaton notes,> the fraction of the world’s population that lives below a dollar a day has fallen from more than 40 percent to 14 percent.… Not everywhere and not at all times, but a quarter century of growth has done much to reduce global poverty. One of the many merits of Deaton’s book is that it doesn’t focus exclusively on G.D.P. Money represents but one dimension of human well-being, he points out; health and longevity are just as important—arguably more important. There is nothing as unequal as people in some parts of the world living to grand old ages while others, in poorer countries, are systematically deprived of a normal life span. In 1950, according to U.N. figures, the average life expectancy in less developed parts of the world was just forty-two years. Thanks to improvements in education, medicine, and health-care delivery systems, things have changed quite a bit. By 2010, Deaton reports, the average life expectancy in less developed regions had risen to sixty-six years—a jump of more than fifty per cent compared to 1950. To be sure, most residents of poor and middle-income countries don’t live as long as people in northern Europe, where the average life expectancy is now close to eighty years, but the gap has narrowed quite a bit. Of course, there are exceptions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, where the scourge of H.I.V. and AIDS has had a big impact on over-all mortality rates. Take Botswana, a country often regarded as one of the best-run on the continent. During the postwar decades, the life expectancy there rose from forty-nine years to sixty-four years; by 2005, largely thanks to H.I.V./AIDS, it had fallen back to forty-nine years. To see a comparable drop, you have to go back to China’s Great Famine of 1958-62, which killed perhaps thirty-five million people. Still, in most parts of Africa, fewer people are dying at very young ages, and over-all longevity has increased. Even in the Democratic Republic of Congo, which is one of the poorest and most benighted countries in the world, infant-mortality rates have been declining at a rate of about one per cent a year. And throughout the region, changes in behavior and the availability of retroviral drugs are finally reducing the number of deaths from H.I.V./AIDS. As this process continues, life-expectancy rates should rise considerably. Back in the nineteen-sixties and seventies, many experts were concerned that a big increase in the earth’s population would doom mankind to widespread impoverishment, malnutrition, and deprivation. As Deaton rightly says, this Malthusian vision turned out to be ill-founded: “The world has done much better than the pessimists predicted.” That’s the good news. But how far has the progress extended? While many people have made the “Great Escape” from chronic poverty and short lives, as Deaton calls it, many of them haven’t moved very far, and perhaps a billion have been left behind completely. If you set aside China and India, which admittedly isn’t very wise, you find that, worldwide, the gap between rich and poor countries is as large as it’s ever been, maybe even larger. And even if you do include China and India, the two great success stories, it’s surprisingly hard to find evidence that inequality has fallen on a global basis.
