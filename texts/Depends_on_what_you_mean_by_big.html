Depends on what you mean by big

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2009/10/depends_on_what_you_mean_by_bi.cfm

CHARLES CALOMIRIS has a piece on the too-big-to-fail problem in today's Wall Street Journal, which would benefit significantly by focusing more on what is meant by big. He writes: Oliver Williamson, an economist at the University of California, Berkeley, just won the Nobel Prize for his pathbreaking work on the "boundaries of the firm," specifically for arguing that it can be more efficient to extend the boundaries of a single firm than for independent firms to contract with each other in the market. That theory explains why nonbank corporations operate world-wide supply chains. And then he goes on to detail specific reasons for bigness, which include the importance of being global, various efficiencies and economies of scope, and improved integration and liquidity in markets for debt and equity. So it seems that by bigness he means, at various points, geographic scope, employment level, and perhaps capitalisation, but he never says which variables critics of too-big-to-fail banks are trying to limit and whether that might be costly. To my knowledge, no one is trying to limit the extent to which banks can operate across state or national borders. The focus on efficiency is perhaps a little misleading, as well. Very large banks may well have better access to capital markets, but to what extent is that based on the implicit guarantee that comes along with being too-big-to-fail? In a too-big-to-fail world, smaller and independent banks are penalised, not because they are inefficient but because the cost of failure must be borne entirely by the banks owners and creditors, and can't be shifted onto taxpayers. Similarly, increases in size might improve a firm's operations by increasing its political resources, giving it sway over a larger set of policymakers, giving it the freedom to shop between a larger set of regulators, improving access to private and valuable information. But we don't really want to encourage these things. Another way of looking at the question is to note that there are certainly gains to being large, but there are also significant costs, and the global financial system and economy would benefit significantly if the costs of being large and failing were entirely internalised. The tricky part is how to move to such a world now, after governments have all but promised never to let another too-big-to-fail firm fail. But such a move is possible, and in an excellent post, Economics of Contempt explains what some of the key principles of a reform strategy ought to be. As it happens, the Obama administration is pursuing a strategy that could, with improvements, do a pretty good job addressing the too-big-to-fail problem. The key, says EofC is to establish clear authority to allow large firms to fail, to establish clear procedures for firms which are looking shaky or at risk of imminent failure, and—and this is very important—to tie resolution procedures to very clear and tangible changes in bank health—to reduce, in other words, regulator discretion. Simpler still: you say under what conditions a firm will be subject to action, you describe clearly what those actions will be, and you make sure you have explicit authority to undertake those actions. And what is interesting is that the risk factors EofC identifies aren't really related to size, in terms of capitalisation: I would make the [Prompt Corrective Action] PCA triggers less focused on capital levels, and more focused on the conditions that make Tier 1 [Financial Holding Companies] FHCs susceptible to modern-day bank runs. For example, I would make one of the PCA triggers contingent on the tenor of the Tier 1 FHC's overall liabilities. As of August 31, 2008, over half of Lehman's $211 billion tri-party repo book had a tenor of less than one week, which made it remarkably susceptible to a run in the repo markets — which, of course, is exactly what happened. Lehman was also relying on roughly $12 billion (at least) of collateral from its prime brokerage clients to fund its day-to-day operating business. These conditions had persisted for several quarters before Lehman's bankruptcy. The Fed should be required to take prompt corrective action once a Tier 1 FHC allows the tenor of, say, 20% of its overall liabilities, or 50% of its daily funding requirements, to drop below one week. (I just pulled those numbers out of the air; the Fed is in a much better position than I am to set then appropriate tenors and percentage of liabilities.) These are the kinds of PCA triggers that would be the most effective. A PCA regime focused on capital levels is unlikely to make much of an impact. It seems to me that any policy ginned up to handle too-big-to-fail which wouldn't have done much to reduce the impact of the Lehman collapse should probably be thrown out immediately. And at this point, it looks like the phrase "too big to fail" obscures more than it reveals. If we're seriously designing (or defending) policies at this point, we need to be serious about what it is, specifically, we're trying to reduce.
