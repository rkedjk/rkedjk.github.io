Big Tech Is a Big Problem by Kenneth Rogoff

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/regulating-big-tech-companies-by-kenneth-rogoff-2018-07

Kenneth Rogoff highlights the problems that tech giants – Amazon, Apple, Facebook, Google, and Microsoft – pose to innovation. They have grown too “big, rich, and powerful” that regulators and politicians are too afraid to take on them. They also make it difficult for startups to attract investment. Young entrepreneurs often have to fight an uphill battle to compete with the goliaths, and they sometimes complain about their ideas being stolen. The author says the dominance of the five tech giants had led to “slow productivity growth” and “low investment.” Instead of boosting job creation and innovation, the number of new business launches has been at a 30-year low, prompting economists and investors to point their fingers at tech giants. Being the world’s most powerful companies, Amazon, Apple, Facebook, Google have a combined value of some $2.5 tn. While “sky-high” valuations might be “good news for the tech oligarchs, whether it is good for the economy is far from clear.” Critics say the tech titans are all too keen to cling to their empires, that they stifle new ideas and creativity. Thanks to their deep pockets, they have a “massive funding advantage” and can “gobble up or squelch any new firm that threatens core profit lines, no matter how indirectly.” Or they use “their vast armies of programmers” to clone other people’s products, while having the legal resources to consolidate power and defend their profitable market share. Investors hesitate to fund startups because they fear competing with tech giants. It’s not about a startup vs startup, but a David vs Goliath battle. It is hard to say whether the concentration of money is the cause or effect of the startup decline. On the one hand, the emergence of fewer new startups makes it easier for existing firms to accumulate more power. However, as industries become more concentrated, it also raises the barriers to new entrepreneurship, choking off innovation. Analysts say, as these tech companies get more powerful, staff salaries get higher. This often provides even less incentive for their staff to leave and set up their own business, which in the past was a common path for entrepreneurs with courage, foresight and determination. If they do leave, the endgame is often to be acquired by their previous employer rather than grow large enough to compete with it. No doubt this trend in the tech industry indicates a persistent decline. Since 1980, the share of companies less than a year old has almost halved – from 15% of companies to just 8.1%, according to Census Bureau data. The total number of startups formed in 2015 was 414,000 – a huge drop from the pre-recession figure of 558,000 in 2006. Critics say while tech giants account for all new job creation, they also account for disruptive innovations and low growth. The author says the “most urgent intervention is to weaken Big Tech’s grip on our personal data,” that enables Google and Facebook “to develop targeted advertising tools that are taking over the marketing business.” He praises European regulators, who are “showing one possible path forward, even as US regulators continue to sit on their hands.” Economists Glen Weyl and Eric Posner “argue that Big Tech should have to pay for your data, instead of claiming it for their own use.” While the EU insists on individual consumers to have the right to know what happens to their data, China is said to have the advantage of beating the West in Artificial Intelligence, due to the absence of data protection and personal freedom. The US Congress and regulators find themselves in a dilemma. The 2016 Russian meddling had laid bare the necessity “to rein in” Internet-based firms that have “a veritable free pass in promulgating fake news.” The author says these Big Tech platforms must observe rules and laws that are applied to “print, radio, and television, in-depth reporting and fact-checking,” or else it will hurt both “democracy and the economy.” Regulators and politicians “need to wake up” and address the issue: “Big Tech is as much a part of the problem as it is a part of the solution.”
