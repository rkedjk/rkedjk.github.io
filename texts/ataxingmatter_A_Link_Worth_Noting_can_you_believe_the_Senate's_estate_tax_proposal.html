ataxingmatter: A Link Worth Noting: can you believe the Senate's estate tax proposal?

http://ataxingmatter.blogs.com/tax/2009/04/a-link-worth-noting-can-you-believe-the-senates-estate-tax-proposal.html

Hat tip to Mark Thoma, Economist's View, Using Inheritance Taxes to Promote Equal Opportunity, Apr. 10, 2009 and Mark Thoma's posting links to Michael Kinsley's April 10th commentary "Democrats for Rich Heirs?" in the Washington Post and to Bard DeLong's Hoo Boy, We Need Better Senators Badly (which in turn links to Think Progress's Lincoln's $250 billion estate tax plan would cut taxes for only 60 'small businesses'), both of whom address the outrageous action in the Senate last week, when ten purported Democrats joined with all 41 Republicans in voting FOR a $250 billion reduction in tax revenues in order to give the ultra-wealthy an even larger break on passing along ultra-wealth without ever being taxed. (You really have to read Kinsley--he has some good cutting comments that show how ridiculous the arguments in favor of a low estate tax really are--like, is anyone going to die sooner or more often once we lower the tax on estates?) The Senate continues to talk about this in terms of saving family farms and small businesses. That's been debunked so many times that one tires of hearing the argument again. The CBO notes that "almost all such estates are able to pay the tax biill without having to sell business assets." In fact, the people that complain are just doing that--whining. The Democratic senators supporting this travesty of tax policy injustice, who Kinsley points out are among the same who claim offense at multimillion dollar bonuses for bailed out Wall Street execs at AIG and elsewhere, should be overwhelmed with a barrage of complaints from every constituent. Here's the list of ten Democrats, available at this link: Baucus (D-MT), Bayh (D-IN), Cantwell (D-WA), Landrieu (D-LA), Lincoln (D-AR), Murray (D-WA), Nelson (D-FL), Nelson (D-NE), Pryor (D-AR), Tester (D-MT). Bayh is one of the ones who voted AGAINST Obama's budget, because he thought it was "too expensive"--so why does he support this amendment in favor of the ultra wealthy that will cost hundreds of billions? Let's face it. Ordinary Americans are currently suffering layoffs, loss of homes, worries about retirement, worries about health care, all from this economic crisis caused by the risk-taking speculation that made huge private profits for Wall Street and created huge socialised losses for taxpayers. Yet these renegade Democratic senators are voting to support billions in revenue reductions benefitting the ultra wealthy at a time when these trillions are being paid out to clean up the economic mess made by the rent-seeking of many of these same ultra wealthy--who already pay very low tax rates on their very very very very very high incomes (CEOS with $30,000 a day not uncommon; how about $400,000 a DAY taxed very cheaply at capital gains rates) and almost no taxes on their very very very very very very large estates (effective tax rates of about 14%--often on wealth that has never been taxed or has been taxed at very low rates when first earned). The Obama proposal--to retain the 2009 rates that allow a married couple's estate to pass along $7 million (at least) tax free and then tax any millions (or billions) above that amount at only 45%--is already an outrage. There are very few estates that pay any tax at all when the exemption amount is at the 2009 rate--no federal estate tax is paid in 997 out of 1000 deaths. See Center for Budget and Policy Priorities. The 3 out of a 1000 that pay are the very richest multimillionaires (often billionaires) in America. We should have a smaller exemption amount and a higher rate, even a progressive rate schedule, to expand the number of ultra wealthy who finally have to pay some tax when their estates are passed on to their heirs. Instead, these 51 Senators have passed a Budget Resolution that proposes exempting a whoppping $10 million per couple and taxing the excess at only 35%. That's only a 5.8% effective tax rate on an estate of $12 million, even though these estates, in many if not most cases, represent very low-taxed accumulations (at 0% or 15% rate) during the lifetime of the decedent. And the cost is not peanuts: annualized, it exceeds the annual budget of the Enivornmental Protection Agency and is about the amount that the government spends on Pell Grants to help students afford college. See Center on budget and Policy Priorities, above. Nor is this an argument about fairness or inappropriate "double taxation" of the same income. Most of this wealth accumulation has never been taxed during the lifetime of the decedent (either because it is in the form of appreciation of art or financial assets that isn't taxable til realized or in the form of tax-exempt income such as interest on municipal bonds or income on life insurance policies or otherwise "sheltered" through inventive strategies like secret offshore bank accounts) or it has been taxed at exceedingly low rates, especially when compared to the payroll plus income taxes paid on the salaries of ordinary, much-less-well-off Americans. Ordinary Americans--98% of us--pay income tax at the progressive rates of 10-35% on our compensation income every year, as well as significant payroll taxes on those same salaries. And most of us have an estate that is so small (especially after the decline in the value of our homes, the biggest asset for most of us) that it would not be subject to tax, even using the much lower exemption levels and rates that were in effect before the first of the Bush changes in 2001! Nor is it about saving family farms and small businesses. Those are red herring arguments, since there's not much if any evidence that the federal estate tax, even at the lower pre-2001 rates, cast families out of family farms and small businesses. There already is, after all, a provision for paying whatever little estate tax is due out of the income of the business over a period of 14 years , and very very few working "family farms" come in at that kind of value level anyway. (Besides, most family farms long ago became impersonable corporate entities, reaping all the benefits of corporate tax deductions for the main shareholder's being "required" by the corporation to live on the farm.) As a commenter on the WaPo op-ed site whose family once owned a farm notes, "Even if the full Clinton-era estate tax were revived a farm of that size (120 acres in Pennsylvania) would never come close to the threshold [of being taxed]. I can sympathize with those who own farms or businesses ten times larger than ours, but only up to a point." In fact, instead of being lost to the estate tax, it is much more likely that family farms are now liquidated at death because the heirs don't want to farm them. This argument about the estate tax destroying family farms is a pure propaganda piece, pushed by the coalition of wealthy families (like the WalMart heirs, the Mars candy family heirs, the Gallo wine family heirs, the Campbell's Soup Company heirs, etc.) that want to end the estate tax. Nor is it an issue of individualism--the right of a person to "decide" what to do with that person's accumulated resources because the person "earned" them. In many cases, the decedent didn't earn but himself inherited. Even if he earned it, he earned it in significant part because of the contracting, operating, working, selling, transporting and other important institutions created by governmental institutions, not "on his own", and often with a number of direct government subsidies, such as those that made the fortune of every Big Oilman possible, and of the Stansfords and others that made fortunes out of free land and public financing for their railroad projects, etc. This proposal is and has always been just one more step along the route to permanent and complete repeal of the estate tax for and on behalf of the ultra wealthy. It is outrageous to cut needed revenues in order to give this kind of bonus to the ultra wealthy when Congress can't bring itself to continue the "making work pay" cut in the payroll taxes that are crushing many ordinary Americans .
