Should We Assume the Worst?

https://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/jamessurowiecki/2009/02/should-we-assum.html

Felix Salmon takes Ben Bernanke and Barack Obama to task today for being too positive about the future prospects of the U.S. economy. They’re hardly wild-eyed optimists: in his testimony before Congress today, Bernanke forecast that economic growth won’t resume, at the earliest, until the second half of this year and that, regardless, the U.S. economy would shrink during 2009. He also suggested that “the downside risks probably outweigh those on the upside,” and went on to enumerate those risks and emphasize the need to “stabilize financial institutions and markets.” That wasn’t, though, gloomy enough for Felix, who argues that the government is making plans based on hope instead of reality. He wants policymakers to “assume the worst” and prepare accordingly. This seems dubious on a couple of accounts. First of all, we don’t want policymakers to assume the worst about the future when shaping economic policy. Nor do we want them to assume the best. Actually, we don’t want them to assume anything. Instead, we want them to come up with the most accurate forecast of the future they can, and to adopt the economic policies that make the most sense given that forecast. There’s no doubt that this is an incredibly complicated process, because the future that someone like Bernanke is trying to predict is a future that’s shaped by his own decisions. But that doesn’t mean the answer is for the Fed to say “things are going to be incredibly awful,” if it believes that they most likely won’t be. The second point is a more obvious one: Felix is just far more pessimistic than Bernanke (and than Obama) about the prospects for the U.S. economy in 2009 and beyond. That’s a reasonable position (even if one might wonder if Felix really thinks he’s better equipped to decipher the actual state of the U.S. economy than the chairman of the Federal Reserve is). But the fact that Bernanke thinks the U.S. economy has a chance of growing at the end of 2009 doesn’t mean that he’s basing policy on “hope” and failing to confront reality. It just means that he and Felix disagree.
