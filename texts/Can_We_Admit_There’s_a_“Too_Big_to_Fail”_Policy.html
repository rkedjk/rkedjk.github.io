Can We Admit There’s a “Too Big to Fail” Policy?

https://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/jamessurowiecki/2010/03/can-we-admit-theres-a-too-big-to-fail-policy.html

Felix Salmon has been doing a bang-up job in recent days of debunking the misinformed attacks on the relationship between credit default swaps and Greek interest rates. So it’s dismaying that on the subject of “too big to fail” banks, he’s engaging in precisely the kind of overwrought conspiracy-mongering that he’s otherwise so adept at puncturing. The occasion for Felix’s rant was an appearance on Thursday by Assistant Treasury Secretary Herbert Allison before the Congressional Oversight Panel for TARP. Throughout Allison’s testimony, the panel members pushed him to admit that there are certain financial institutions, like Citibank, that are legally considered “systemically significant,” and that the government has guaranteed them against failure. In other words, the panel members wanted Allison to admit that there is a formal “too big to fail” policy. (You can see one example of this around the eighty-fifth minute of yesterday’s hearing.) Allison, though, consistently demurred, offering answers like, “We cannot comment on a judgment about whether [Citibank is] systemically significant.” Felix sees this as “peddling fiction,” and speculates that Allison’s refusal to admit what’s obvious was either evidence “that Treasury [is] now even more captured by America’s biggest banks than it was in the initial months of the Obama administration” or “an admission that financial reform is going nowhere.” But it was neither of these things. (Financial reform may not be going nowhere, but that has nothing to do with what Allison said, or didn’t say, yesterday.) Instead, what Allison said was both accurate and politically, and economically, necessary. The refusal to say that the Treasury Department has a formal “too big to fail” policy was accurate, because Treasury hasn’t declared any financial institutions systemically important, and doesn’t, in any case, have the legal authority to do so. That’s why Treasury had to get Congress to pass TARP, after all—on its own, it couldn’t inject money into the banks. Felix says that Treasury has already designated some financial institutions TBTF by creating a category it calls “Tier 1 Financial Holding Companies.” But it hasn’t: that category is from Treasury’s proposal for regulatory reform, which Congress has not enacted (and the proposal doesn’t mention any specific financial institutions by name). And the reason the Obama Administration proposes giving the Federal Reserve authority to designate some financial institutions as Tier 1 FHCs isn’t so that it can legally bail them out. It’s so it can regulate them more tightly than other financial institutions and legally put them into a managed bankruptcy if they become undercapitalized. In that sense, designating a bank an FHC would be the opposite of designating it TBTF: it would instead be saying that if it got into trouble, it would be wound down via the resolution authority that the Administration wants Congress to create. The simple reason why Allison refused to say whether Citibank is systemically significant, then, is because he had no legal authority to do so. On top of that, though, it also makes economic sense for the government to refuse to answer the question, because doing so gives it far more bargaining power in the event that another big financial institution gets into trouble. The problem with having the government say publicly that it has a TBTF policy, as Felix wants it to do, is that this would effectively commit the government to guaranteeing the debts of the country’s major financial institutions. If it did so, and, say, Citibank were to get in trouble again, it would be much harder for the government to make creditors take a haircut, because they would be able to point to Treasury’s public guarantee. Given that one of the sharpest criticisms of the government’s actions during the crisis was that, in the case of companies like AIG, it failed to leverage its bargaining power, it’s peculiar to attack Allison for not giving away the store in advance. There is, of course, something frustrating about the fact that the government can’t say it’s going to bail out the big banks if they get in trouble, when clearly it will, just as it was frustrating that the government always denied that it was guaranteeing the debts of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, even while it was doing just that. But that frustration is the inevitable result of an inadequate regulatory system, in which we have massive financial institutions but no formal mechanisms for taking them over. Allison’s testimony wasn’t evidence that the Obama Administration has been coöpted or is giving up. It was evidence that Congress needs to get serious about enacting the reforms the Administration has proposed. If it does so, the government will then be able to say publicly “these institutions are systemically significant,” because it will also be able to say “and if they get in trouble, they will be wound down.”
