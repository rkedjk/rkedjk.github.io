How Much Europe Can Europe Tolerate? by Dani Rodrik

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/juncker-white-paper-wrong-question-by-dani-rodrik-2017-03

Rodrik links 'healthy democracy' with a situation where 'political integration' matches 'economic integration'. Thus the Republic of Ireland- which was economically integrated with the rest of the United Kingdom- was bound to be an 'unhealthy democracy' even if Da Valera had pursued sensible economic policies. Switzerland between '42 and '45 must have been an 'unhealthy democracy' because its political integration into Hitler's Europe fell short of its economic integration. Norway's democracy is extremely unhealthy compared to Sweden or Slovakia because it is less 'politically integrated' than 'economically integrated' into Europe. Is there any theoretical reason to believe 'political integration' should match 'economic integration' for Democracy to be healthy? Suppose we have free trade & factor movement on a Christaller plain. There is perfect 'economic integration'. Must there be equal 'political integration'? Suppose people speaking a particular language inhabit a contiguous area on this plain. Democracy becomes more healthy, not less so, if these people can elect representatives who deliberate in their own language and enact measures designed to maximise that language's possible stream of benefits for its speakers. Thus, Linguistic nationalism is perfectly compatible with 'economic integration' which is why Nineteenth Century 'Free Trade' Liberals typically supported Nationalist struggles for Independence on the Continent. It is not the case that Europe, by itself, created a mechanism to avoid internecine warfare. On the contrary, it was America which supplied the muscle- e.g. in Kosovo- to allow the Continent to gain its present peaceful state. The theory of Tiebout sorting suggests that Political integration is not necessarily a good thing (unless it gives scope for increased subsidiarity in which case it is the pre-condition for greater ex post diversity in fiscal models). Indeed, 'disintegration' solves a Preference Revelation problem and thus increases the supply of local Public goods. Suppose there is a 'major overhaul of European Institutions'. Would it solve anything? No. Any outcome implementable by overhauled Institutions could be either be implemented currently or else the proposed method of 'overhauling' lacks democratic support. It is no good saying 'overhauled institutions' will provide generous transfer payments if there is no democratic mandate for such generosity within the countries required to make a net contribution to the scheme. Suppose the thing could be done by fiat, without a democratic mandate. It would still not be the case that Greece, for example, would be rescued because Greece is still better off than its neighbours. Either it has to share the transfer with neighbouring States or it has to prevent an influx from those States. Europe is not in 'deep crisis'. It took the path of least resistance during the boom years and will continue to take the path of least resistance now the chickens have come home to roost.
