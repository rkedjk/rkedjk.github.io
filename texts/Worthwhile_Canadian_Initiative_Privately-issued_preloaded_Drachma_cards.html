But technology changes. The new currency doesn't have to be on paper. It could be preloaded electronic payment cards. If you introduce a new money, you want to do it quickly. But not everyone has a bank account, and I read that it takes months to print up a new paper currency. So unless the Greek government secretly ordered a big printing of Drachma notes when it first took power (which I doubt), Greece reverting to the Drachma is different from Argentina breaking the peg to the US dollar, because Argentina already had the Peso notes. Forget monetary theory for a minute, and think only about the technical/engineering stuff : is Andrew Lainton basically right about this ? Can something like this be done quickly and cheaply, without too much danger of counterfeit and fraud? (I don't know, which is why I'm asking.) Can lots of preloaded payment cards be produced quickly? Is it easy to tell whether a preloaded card is counterfeit? Is there some easy way to tell whether a preloaded payment card has ever been used? Or some way to make cards so it is easy to tell whether they have ever been used? If so, unused cards with different denominations could themselves be passed from hand to hand just like paper currency and coins. Now back to monetary theory (you knew I wouldn't be able to stay away). Could the issuance of Drachma cards be left to the private sector? In the olden days commercial banks printed their own paper currency. That's why they were called "banknotes". But modern governments (mostly) banned the practice, and made currency a government monopoly, that could only be issued by the government-owned central bank. Can Greece go back to the future, with commercial banks producing a plastic version? What about seigniorage? One reason a government like Greece would want to print its own currency is that it is bust, and can't raise enough funds through taxes and borrowing to finance its expenditure. So it wants to print money to finance the difference. If it costs $0.10 to print a $20 note it "earns" $19.90 for every $20 note it prints (or gets an interest-free loan of $19.90 for every year as long as that $20 note stays in circulation). (The second reason is that it can have a monetary policy that is more suited to its own economy.) But seigniorage comes from the government's monopoly over the issuance of 0% interest currency. If the government wants to earn seigniorage from privately-issued currency, what would be the best way to do this? It could impose a tax, or it could sell quotas at auction. What about monetary policy? What would be the best way for the government to implement monetary policy, if the issuance of currency were in private hands? It could vary the tax over time, or it could vary the quota over time. The simplest way to think about this is to imagine that each bank that issues a card for 100 drachmas incurs a 100 drachma liability to the government-owned central bank. This is like 100% reserves against privately-issued currency. And the central bank can either set an interest rate (tax) on that liability, or can set a limit (quota) on the total liability, with a monthly auction of the quota rights. We live in interesting times.
