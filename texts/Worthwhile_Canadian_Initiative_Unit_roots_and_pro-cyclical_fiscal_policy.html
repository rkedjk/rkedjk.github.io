Worthwhile Canadian Initiative: Unit roots and pro-cyclical fiscal policy

http://worthwhile.typepad.com/worthwhile_canadian_initi/2012/07/unit-roots-and-pro-cyclical-fiscal-policy.html

In a nutshell: if there is a unit root in GDP (so that some part of any fluctuation in GDP lasts forever), or if there is perceived to be a unit root, might that explain why fiscal policy is, or appears to be, pro-cyclical? This is something I have long wondered about. And reading Jeff Frankel's complaints (HT Mark Thoma ) about pro-cyclical fiscal policy made me wonder about it again. And I do mean "wonder about". I am not at all sure that this idea is right, or even theoretically coherent. But I wonder if it might be. A counter-cyclical fiscal policy is where the government increases spending and/or cuts tax rates in a recession (and cuts spending and/or raises tax rates in a boom). A pro-cyclical fiscal policy is the opposite. Macroeconomists argue about whether counter-cyclical fiscal policy is a good policy. Set that argument aside. But in a wide class of macroeconomic models, ranging from Old Keynesian through to Classical models, pro-cyclical fiscal policy is a bad policy. The debate is whether fiscal policy should be counter-cyclical or not cyclical at all. So why do we sometimes observe pro-cyclical fiscal policy? Is it just incompetence, ignorance, or some sort of failure of the political system? Maybe it's unit roots? Or perceived unit roots? Take an extreme (and unrealistic) case. Suppose that (real) GDP followed a random walk. Every year nature tosses a coin. If it comes up heads GDP increases by some fixed amount. If it comes up tails GDP decreases by the same fixed amount. Your expectation for next year's GDP (and for all future years) is whatever GDP is this year. All shocks are permanent shocks. If we lived in a world like that, I don't see how fiscal policy could possibly avoid being pro-cyclical. Or, at least, looking pro-cyclical in hindsight, because strictly speaking there aren't any cycles in this imaginary economy, though there will appear to be cycles if we fit a trend line (or dare I say HP filter?) to past GDP data. If a bad shock hits, we are permanently poorer. We will need to cut C+I+G, and presumably will want to cut all three of C and I and G, and presumably will want to cut all three right away. It is very unlikely we would want to cut private consumption and investment but keep government spending the same. If a bad shock hits, we will also need to cut C+S+T, and presumably will want to cut all three of C and S and T, and presumably will want to cut all three right away. Now if we have a proportional tax system, so that T=tY, then T will automatically fall when Y falls, so the government may choose to leave the tax rate t constant. But if we have a progressive tax system, (or a progressive tax/transfer system), the government will need to increase the marginal tax rate if it wants to keep the average tax rate the same and leave the ratio of taxes to GDP (T/Y) constant. If a bad shock hits, and if the government cuts G to keep G/Y constant, and raises marginal tax rates (if progressivity makes it necessary) to keep T/Y constant, that still won't be enough to keep the debt/GDP ratio constant, because GDP is now permanently lower, and any "primary" (i.e. ignoring interest payments on the debt) budget surplus will also be smaller. If the government wants to keep the debt/GDP ratio constant, it will need to cut G/Y and/or increase T/Y. Unless the debt just happens to be zero. In the extreme case of GDP following a random walk, pro-cyclical fiscal policy (or what would appear to be pro-cyclical fiscal policy) is inevitable. The random walk example is obviously unrealistic. But is it 100% unrealistic? It is very easy to build models in which temporary booms and recessions will have some permanent effect on GDP that lasts forever. (Stick any short run macro model where investment is procyclical on top of an AK long run growth model, for example). The economy will recover following a recession, but it will never recover (in expectation) all the way back to where it would have been otherwise. From what I read (but don't properly understand) from econometricians like Stephen, econometrics tests of unit roots just aren't powerful enough to tell us for sure whether or not there exist some permanent effects. They can only maybe tell us that, if there are permanent effects, those permanent effects won't be bigger than some proportion. Econometricians don't know if there are permanent effects, so we can't expect policymakers to know. And even if there were zero permanent effects on GDP itself, there will still be a permanent effect on the present value of GDP (i.e. on wealth). To what extent does the inevitability of (apparently) pro-cyclical fiscal policy in the case of the random walk carry over into a world where there might be some permanent effects? That's what I wonder about.
