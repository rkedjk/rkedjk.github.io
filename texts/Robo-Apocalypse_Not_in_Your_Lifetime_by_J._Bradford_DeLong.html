Robo-Apocalypse? Not in Your Lifetime by J. Bradford DeLong

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/rise-of-robots-social-work-by-j-bradford-delong-2019-05

" Yet even if machine-learning algorithms could communicate back to us why they have made certain decisions, they will only ever work in restricted environmental domains. The wide range of specific conditions that they need in order to function properly renders them brittle and fragile, particularly when compared to the robust adaptability of human beings." This is certainly true of the technology of today. But it is very dangerous to assume that things will continue this way. Think back to the days before the telephone was invented. All the basic building blocks were in place - people understood the basics of electricity, and they had the example of the telegraph to show that it could be used for long-distance communication. But just how to convert a human voice into an electrical signal and back to sound waves again was a problem waiting to be solved. I would say that the telephone was technically imaginable, but not yet technically possible. Yet it would have been rash indeed to have bet much money on the proposition that it would never happen. I believe that we are at a similar point with what I would call true artificial intelligence. I am not talking about anything that is around today. By true artificial intelligence, I mean machines that can learn the way humans can learn. Machines that can manipulate their environments in the ways humans can, that have the detailed sensory inputs humans have, that can learn from their mistakes, that can learn human language. Machines that can start out as "artificial babies" and be trained and taught just the way humans can. I am sure that this problem can be solved, because nature has already solved it. If evolution produced humans through a process of trial-and-error, is it at all safe to assume that producing something that can learn like a human will be forever beyond our reach? Also, I would claim that the solution to this problem is now technically imaginable, just as the telephone was technically imaginable in the years before its invention. Some smart kid may be building something in his garage right now that will blow us out of the water. We need to start thinking about this problem now, and not just bury our heads in the sand, saying it will never happen. It need not be the end of the world - in fact it would be a great benefit for us if we use it properly and structure our economies in a way that uses the technology for the benefit of all, not just the few.
