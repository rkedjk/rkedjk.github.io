Coping With Excess

https://judson.blogs.nytimes.com/2009/07/14/coping-with-excess/

About a year ago, the pentailed tree shrew — a small mammal from the rain forests of west Malaysia — had its photo splashed across the newspapers. Why? Because close observations of these animals revealed that they often drink large amounts of alcohol. Their preferred tipple? Nectar from the flowers of the bertam palm. (This nectar is often alcoholic owing to the fermenting actions of a number of wild yeast species.) Yet despite their enthusiastic alcohol consumption, pentailed tree shrews don’t get drunk. Nor do they suffer the diseases that a human alcoholic tends to suffer. In other words, they seem to have evolved to be immune to the excess. Which made me think. As everyone knows, many humans now live in an environment of gross food excess, with fatty, sugary foods available as never before. At the moment, chronic over-eating leads to obesity, diabetes and a suite of associated health problems. But would it be possible, in principle, to evolve so that we could eat more without suffering these consequences? Performing the relevant experiments with humans is necessarily . . . difficult. Apart from the fact it would include forced feeding and forced breeding, it would take at least a century to learn the results. But luckily, some other animals are easier to work with. The diamondback moth, for instance. Caterpillars, it goes without saying, enjoy eating. What is less often appreciated is that caterpillars can get fat if they eat a diet too rich in carbohydrate. As with humans, excessive fatness is unhealthy. (Insects tend to be careful eaters, and it’s difficult to persuade them to over-indulge: they are not natural gluttons. However, they need a certain level of protein, so if you feed them a diet low in protein but high in carbohydrate, individuals of some species will eat more carbohydrate than they need in order to get the protein they need.) Mel Evans/The Associated Press So, imagine two diets. One is rich in carbohydrate but low in protein, the other is the reverse. Caterpillars are fed exclusively on one diet or the other; they are reared to become adults, and allowed to breed with other animals from their diet group. The offspring are then fed the same diet as their parents. Repeat. After as few as eight generations, individuals from the high-carbohydrate diet will be much less prone to store the excess carbs as fat. Conversely, those from the low-carb diet will be more prone to deposit carbs as fat. Similar results have been found for rats. A few generations is all it takes to evolve rats that can eat rich diets without becoming obese. And if you can do it in rats and moths . . . odds are you’d get similar results in humans. But to my mind, what’s interesting about this is that it suggests another factor that could be contributing to the current obesity epidemic. The usual argument is that the tendency to lay down fat is something that has evolved in response to past famines. Known as the “thrifty gene” hypothesis, it goes like this: Fact: in agricultural societies, famine due to crop failure or war has been a recurring problem. Indeed, one compilation of famines due to crop failure found more than 350 instances in the last 2,000 years (190 in Britain alone), and the list is nowhere near complete. Fact: famines sometimes kill large numbers of people, especially children. Hypothesis: individuals who were more prone to put on fat in times of plenty were more likely to survive famine, and therefore more likely to contribute their “thrifty” genes to the next generation. “Thrifty” genes are therefore something that many of us have. Implication: In a modern environment, this tendency to store excess food as fat is detrimental. The thrifty gene hypothesis is contentious, and clear evidence for it is lacking. It isn’t known, for instance, whether fat people survive famine better than thin people (though it seems likely that they would). However, the logic of the argument seems to me to be reasonable. But the experiments on moths suggest that a tendency for obesity could also arise without famine. In those experiments, animals exposed to an energy-rich diet quickly evolved to become immune to the excess; conversely, animals that were more prone to storing fat were those with no history of abundance in their family tree. To me, this suggests that a tendency for obesity needn’t require that someone’s ancestors regularly went hungry; it could just mean that they didn’t gorge on doughnuts and cakes. Under this scenario, the fattest among us may not be those whose ancestors had the most experience of famine, but those with the least experience of excess. And, it’s worth noting, excess is another feature of agricultural societies. Yes, famines happen. But between famines, there is often a considerable surplus, and the richest members of society often eat extremely well. For example, at the coronation feast for Pope Clement VI in Avignon in 1342, the menu included 60 pigs, 101 calves, 118 cattle, 300 pike, 914 goats, 1,023 sheep, 1,446 geese, 10,471 hens, 46,856 cheeses and 50,000 tarts. All this for a guest list of about 3,000 people. Meanwhile, records from the reign of Elizabeth I show that in one year, her entourage (which numbered about 600) at Hampton Court Palace ate 53 wild boar, 760 calves, 1,240 oxen, 1,870 pigs, 2,330 deer and 8,200 sheep. They also drank 2,228,000 liters (that’s 600,000 gallons) per year of ale. Wassail, wassail! Many factors — what you eat today, what you ate as a baby, levels of exercise, your gut microbes and so on — contribute to obesity. But we know that humans in different places have, historically, eaten different things, and that the tastes and customs of our ancestors have left marks on our genomes. Peoples that have had long associations with cows, like northern Europeans and East Africans, have evolved to be better at digesting milk than other groups. Those whose ancestors have eaten diets high in starches like rice, wheat or tubers have evolved to be more efficient at digesting starches. So it wouldn’t be surprising if the genes involved in obesity were different from one group to the next, reflecting different histories of exposure to famine and excess. Never mind personal medicine: roll on the personal diet!
