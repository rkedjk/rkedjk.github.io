The Short-Selling Question

https://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/jamessurowiecki/2008/11/the-shortsellin.html

Bess Levin over at Dealbreaker writes of the Citigroup C.E.O. Vikram Pandit’s attempt to blame short sellers for the decline in the company’s stock price: “SHORT-SELLERS ARE NOT THE ISSUE, VIKRAM.” Levin’s take on short selling is the one that all the cool kids have: blaming short sellers for a stock’s problems is the last refuge of a scoundrel, and demonizing short sellers is a sure sign of a financially unsophisticated journalist. And there are good reasons to believe both of these things. Generally speaking, the more short sellers there are in a market, the healthier it will be, since there will be greater diversity of opinion and information. And historically, when company C.E.O.s attack short sellers, it’s usually been a sign that something is deeply wrong at the company, not with the shorts. That’s why I’ve been an ardent advocate of the virtues of short selling. And yet. Of late, it seems to me that the defense of short selling—or, to be more accurate, the attacks on the attacks on short selling—has become knee-jerk and surprisingly naïve, resting as it does on two assertions: Short sellers can’t drive down a stock on their own. If a stock plummets the way Citigroup’s stock did—falling more than sixty per cent in less than a week —there must be a good, as in a fundamental, reason for it. Neither of these assertions seems to me to be true. With regard to the first point, we know that information cascades exist in markets, and that investors often “rationally herd.” That means that investors often take their cues from what other investors are doing and imitate them, because they assume that the movement of a company’s stock price indicates something important about the company’s fundamental well-being. This phenomenon is more common, and has more dramatic effects, during bubbles and panics. The result is that there are situations in which sustained selling can, effectively on its own, beget more selling. With regard to the second point, unless you’re a complete believer in the efficient-market theory, you know that stock prices sometimes become disconnected from fundamental value. During the Internet bubble of the late nineteen-nineties, to take only the most extreme example, myriad companies were given multi-billion-dollar valuations by the stock market despite the fact that they had few prospects of ever earning meaningful profits. And, on the other hand, during the mid- to late-nineteen-seventies, some of the most profitable companies in the world were given valuations that radically underestimated how much money they would make in the future. Markets overshoot high and low, and those mistakes are not immediately arbitraged away. And during periods of high volatility (like the one we’re going through), markets can overshoot very fast and very far. Taken together, those two things mean that concerted short selling certainly can, in theory, drive a company’s stock price well below fundamental value, and keep it there for some period of time. And this is especially possible, at least theoretically, when it comes to financial institutions, because their stock price can actually have a profound effect on their underlying business. Felix Salmon wrote today of the Citigroup bailout, “it was the tumbling stock price which was responsible for this deal in the first place.” In other words, the declining stock price wasn’t simply an epiphenomenon of the problems with Citigroup’s business: the declining stock price actually exacerbated those problems. So if short sellers can drive down a financial institution’s stock, they can hope to do more than just start an information cascade among investors. They can also materially weaken the institution’s underlying business by inspiring doubt among depositors, lenders, and counterparties. Needless to say, you can’t do this to any company: only firms whose finances or businesses are already in some way fragile can succumb to these kind of selling frenzies. “Fragile,” though, is not the same thing as “insolvent” or “bankrupt.” But what’s distinctive about short selling is that, again in theory, it can play a major role in turning a fragile company into a bankrupt one. To be sure, as people always say, Wall Street’s general bias is to the upside. And there are plenty of cases where the Wall Street hype machine gets going and propels stocks up to completely unjustified levels. This, too, is a problem. But overinflated stock prices create a misallocation of capital and, eventually, losses for shareholders. They don’t, generally speaking, drive companies out of business. The over_de_flated stock price of a financial institution, by contrast, can help destroy its business. The negative consequences of mispricing (assuming, for the sake of argument, that we’re talking only about cases of mispricing) are, in other words, more significant on the downside than on the upside. Figuring out whether any of this is actually happening is, to be sure, very tricky: how do you reasonably distinguish between the normal give-and-take of the market and a bear raid or simply an information cascade, and who is supposed to do the distinguishing? (The number I would like to see is what percentage of shares traded, not held, are being sold short.) And it’s even trickier to figure out what, if anything, to do about it, although certainly restoring the uptick rule seems like a no-brainer. But I do think journalists are being, to borrow a phrase from Henry Paulson, either naive or disingenuous when they pretend that there’s no way that short selling may have played a role in exacerbating the financial chaos of the past six months, or that company C.E.O.s who claim otherwise are always just covering up for their own mistakes (even if that is what Vikram Pandit was probably doing). Even paranoiacs, after all, sometimes have real enemies.
