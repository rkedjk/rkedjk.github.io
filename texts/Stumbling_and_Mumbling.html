But what is the origin of this right?It’s doesn’t lie in nature. Property rights, as David Hume said , are “an artifice and contrivance of man” - they are social constructs. So, why should we construct intellectual property rights?The Spectator editorial says: Intellectual and artistic property rights are every bit as important as the rights of ownership to physical property. It is no less wrong to steal a film recording than it is to steal a teenager’s laptop. But this analogy won’t do. Stealing a laptop is a zero-sum activity; you gain its use, but the teenager loses it. Taking intellectual property, though, is positive-sum. As Thomas Jefferson said: He who receives ideas from me, receives instruction himself without lessening mine; as he who lights his taper at mine receives light without darkening me. There’s simply very little evidence that the production of music has declined as file-sharing has risen. Quite why this should be is interesting. Partly, it’s because the technical progress that’s caused file-sharing has also reduced the costs of making music; the greatest album ever made was mostly recorded in a bedroom. Also, artists can make money in other ways - for example by touring. And also, the motives for making music are non-financial - satisfying the muse, gratifying the ego, whatever.The argument from justice is that creating something generates rights over it; Mandelson hints at this when he says: Taking something for nothing, without permission, and with no compensation for the person who created and owns it, is wrong.
