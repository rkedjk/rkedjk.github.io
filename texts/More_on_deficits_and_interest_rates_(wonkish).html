More on deficits and interest rates (wonkish)

https://krugman.blogs.nytimes.com/2009/08/15/more-on-deficits-and-interest-rates-wonkish/

A bit more detail on the reasoning behind my earlier post on deficits and interest rates. I noted that over the past decade there has been a close negative correlation between deficits and interest rates: What’s going on? Let’s think in terms of the national income accounting identity, ignoring foreign trade: GDP = C + I + G (consumption spending, investment spending, government purchases) If we ignore a bunch of technical qualifications that don’t change the basic story, we can write private savings thus: S = GDP – T – C where T is tax collection. And we can use this to rewrite the accounting identity as S-I = G-T That is, the budget deficit equals the private sector financial surplus, which was the relationship I used in an earlier post on how deficits saved the world. Take investment spending I as a given, for the moment. Then we can draw the “savings-investment cross” diagram, a variant of the Keynesian cross: Here S-I is upward-sloping because savings rise with GDP, G-T is downward-sloping because revenue rises with GDP. I’ve shown what happens in this case if private investment spending changes for some reason — say, the emergence or collapse of a housing bubble. The S-I curve shifts up or down, and GDP moves with it; one consequence is that the budget deficit moves with the economy (exerting an automatic stabilizer role, but that’s not my focus this time.) What does this have to do with interest rates? Well, the Fed more or less follows a Taylor rule, raising rates when GDP is high relative to potential and reducing them when it’s low. Here’s the picture from the San Francisco Fed: Now, these interest rate changes also feed back to I, reducing the size of the GDP fluctuations (which is, of course, the point.) But that doesn’t change the fact that we would expect to see a positive relationship between GDP and interest rates, and therefore a negative correlation between interest rates and deficits. OK, all of this depends on the assumption that fluctuations in I, driven by animal spirits or whatever, are the driver of the business cycle. If what was happening was mainly changes in fiscal policy, that would shift G-T instead of S-I, so you’d expect the correlation to run the other way. However, the fiscal expansion we’re seeing now is taking place under very special circumstances: namely, short-term interest rates are up against the zero lower bound (see the figure above), so there should be, and has been, no effect of the stimulus plan on short rates. Long rates, which depend on expected future short rates, are a more complicated story. So that’s what’s going on in my head as I look at the situation. Two final notes: 1. You may wonder what happened to the supply and demand for funds. Where is it? The answer is that it’s implicit in the story; as Hicks showed back in 1937, if you ask the question, are interest rates determined by the supply and demand for money or by the supply and demand for saving, the answer is “yes”. 2. If you believe with Casey Mulligan and colleagues that the slump reflects reduced willingness to work rather than a fall in demand, you can ignore all of this.
