(This is inspired by Steve Williamson's post . I partly agree and partly disagree with Steve, but have decided to start from scratch anyway.) (Almost) every economist has learned the Keynesian Cross. (Almost) nobody (IMHO) understands it. (Or, less provocatively, we all understand it differently). Here is my interpretation of the Keynesian Cross model. This is how I understand it. In any market there are three quantities: Qd, Qs, and Q. Qd is the quantity demanded -- the quantity that buyers would like to buy. Qs is the quantity supplied -- the quantity sellers would like to sell. And Q is the quantity actually bought and sold. Those three quantities may or may not be equal, but they are three conceptually distinct quantities, and we need to keep them distinct. 1. The short side rule says that exchange is voluntary, and so Q=min{Qd,Qs}. Quantity actually bought and sold is whichever is less: quantity demanded or quantity supplied. 2. The Keynesian Cross asserts that Qd is a function of Q. If the relationship is linear, then Qd=a+bQ. That's the demand function. 3. The Keynesian Cross assumes that Qs is strictly greater than than Qd. There is always excess supply. Putting 1,2,and 3 together we get: 4. Q=Qd=a+bQ or Q=a/(1-b) as the solution to the model. Now for the interpretation. Q means the aggregate quantity of newly produced goods bought and sold. GDP, for example. The normal symbol is Y. But I deliberately chose Q, by analogy with quantities in simple micro markets. People get awfully confused by that strange "AE" and "Y" notation. AE (aggregate desired expenditure) just means aggregate Qd; and Y (income, output) just means aggregate Q. The short side rule is absolutely standard. Nothing weird or Keynesian here. That's exactly how Q gets determined in a partial equilibrium demand and supply model, if you impose a binding price ceiling or floor. 3 is a bit weird. There is no automatic adjustment mechanism that ensures that we get to where Qd=Qs. There is no supply function in the model whatsoever. There is no production function, and no market for inputs, and no real wage. The whole supply side of the economy is just ignored, other than to assume that Qs must exceed Qd for the model to make sense. And so, despite what you hear people say, 4 is not really an equilibrium condition in the normal sense of the word. It's a semi-equilibrium condition. A full equilibrium condition has Qd=Q=Qs.
