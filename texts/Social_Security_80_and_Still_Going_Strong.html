Social Security: 80 and Still Going Strong

https://www.usnews.com/opinion/economic-intelligence/2015/08/14/social-security-still-going-strong-on-80th-anniversary

In honor of Social Security's 80th birthday – FDR signed the Social Security Act on August 14, 1935 – the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities has updated its "Top Ten Facts about Social Security." I bet some things in it will surprise you. Most people think of Social Security as a retirement program, but a fifth of the 60 million people receiving benefits are disabled workers and their dependents, or young survivors of deceased workers. In other words, your Social Security payroll taxes are earning you not only an inflation-adjusted guaranteed-benefit retirement income but also life insurance and disability insurance protection. Social Security benefits are based on workers' earnings histories and a progressive benefit formula (see chart below), not on how much they've paid in or their luck in picking investments, as is the case with most private pensions. Social Security provides a foundation of retirement protection for nearly every American. With very low administrative costs and a large risk pool, it provides a higher annual payout per dollar contributed than private annuities. Center on Budget and Policy Priorities And don't underestimate the value of Social Security's life insurance and disability insurance protections. Among young workers, an estimated 36 percent of men and 31 percent of women will become disabled or die before reaching full retirement age. CBPP shows that Social Security benefits are much more modest than you might realize. The average Social Security retirement benefit in June was $1,335 a month, or a little over $16,000 a year. Social Security payments currently replace about 40 percent of past earnings for someone who worked all their adult life at average earnings and claims benefits this year at the traditional retirement age of 65. The retirement age for receiving full benefits is now 66, however, and it's scheduled to rise to 67 over the 2017-2022 period. Largely as a result of that increase, the wage replacement rate for a similar worker retiring at age 65 in the future will fall to about 36 percent. Advocates of raising the retirement age further should recognize that it would cut benefits across the board (see the box in this CBPP paper). While Social Security benefits are relatively modest, they provide the majority of nearly two-thirds of elderly beneficiaries' income. Almost a quarter of elderly beneficiaries get by almost exclusively on Social Security and that share rises to nearly half for those 80 or older. Social Security is especially important for minorities, for whom Social Security provides an even greater share of their income, and for women, who earn less, have less savings and live longer, on average, than men. Social Security is a highly effective anti-poverty policy. Without it, more than 40 percent of elderly Americans would be poor; with it, fewer than 10 percent are. It also lifts more than a million children and more than 6 million adults aged 18-64 out of poverty. CBPP's tenth fact may be the one that surprises you the most: relatively modest changes would place Social Security on a sound financial footing for 75 years and beyond. Alarmists who claim that there is a looming Social Security Disability Insurance funding crisis or that Social Security won't be around when today's younger workers retire don't have their facts straight. Cutting through the arcane details about Social Security trust funds, there is no real disability funding crisis. Policymakers have an easy – and in the past non-controversial – way to redirect payroll tax revenues between trust funds that would allow Social Security to pay full old age, survivors and disability benefits until 2034. After that, even if policymakers took no further action, Social Security could still pay three-quarters of scheduled benefits out of payroll tax revenues. While Social Security faces no imminent crisis, there is a long-term gap between its projected income and promised benefits of just under 1 percent of gross domestic product over the next 75 years. Closing that gap is a manageable task.
