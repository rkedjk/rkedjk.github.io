Will Libra Be Stillborn? by Barry Eichengreen

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/libra-looks-like-a-currency-without-a-purpose-by-barry-eichengreen-2019-10

Definitely the banks', including the central banks', perspective. Cryptocurrencies like Libra do not need credit operations to support the payment system, since they can harvest and monetize the information embedded in purchases and transfers. That means that the argument that central banks and banks themselves have used to justify government support for banks and banks' credit operations -- that support for credit operations is essential to protect the payment system -- is simply not valid. By the same token, worries about the possibility of crises compromising the reserves backing a cryptocurrency are misplaced; operators of the currency have no need to risk their reserves in any way. They can maintain a 100 percent reserve in hard currencies and still be profitable. Similarly, one has to ask why there is a public interest in cloaking "the intimate details" of anyone's financial dealings at the same time as there is such a huge fear of money-laundering through cryptocurrency transactions. The two arguments cut in different directions, and reflect more the vagaries of current banking regulation than any intrinsic problem with cryptocurrencies. Remember cryptocurrency transactions are indelible, and only anonymous if the operators allow anonymous accounts (as banks do now). Lastly, it’s hard to see how the existence of cryptocurrencies with 100 percent reserve backing could complicate monetary policy? Essentially, they are risk-free money market funds, which pay out in free services. If one fund becomes dominant, there might be a problem as there would be with any monopoly, but that’s what anti-trust regulations are for. There is nothing intrinsically problematic about more efficient and transparent payment arrangements.
