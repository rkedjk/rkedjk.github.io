Doha on ice

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2008/10/doha_on_ice.cfm

WELL, that settles it. The Doha round negotiations are definitely on ice now. Last week Peter Mandelson, the EU’s trade commissioner, quit to become Gordon Brown’s business minister. Very nice for Britain—he is a capable man—but it leaves the EU’s negotiating stance in the WTO talks in a lurch. The replacement in Brussels is to be Catherine Margaret Ashton. Various places on the web say she has an economics degree. That is not a bad start (although most nations send lawyers as their negotiators since economists occasionally have their own ideas about what trade policy should be). But it's only a start. The trade portfolio is an amazingly technical thing. Just to understand what people are talking about requires a great deal of study. In 1990, I went from being a professor teaching trade at Columbia University to following the Uruguay round negotiations for the Council of Economic Advisers of George Bush (the real President Bush). Although I had a PhD in trade and had been studying it for years, I found myself completely at sea in the discussions. There are masses of details to learn about the existing agreements and trade law, all expressed in a pea-soup of jargon. And then one has to master the negotiating positions of the major players and what they think about each other’s positions. Oh, and it helps to have an excellent understanding of the domestic trade politics of all the major players since it is almost always the case that different ministries from the same nation have different views. Then there is all the unwritten exchanges that go on in this sort of talk. In a trade negotiation, nothing is agreed until everything is agreed. Of course there is staff, but the most sensitive deals are discussed in the “green room” with just principles plus one. The fact that the EU has switched negotiator in mid stream means that it will be almost impossible to take things up where they left off when the talks broke down this summer. By the time the new American administration is in place, Dame Ashton’s term at the commission will be up. By late 2009 the US and EU will be restarting negotiations with fresh talent and limited institutional memory. That's reason enough to be pessimistic, but if the WTO does not renew the term of Pascal Lamy, its director-general, next year (or if he doesn’t want another term), then it may be time to kiss this round goodbye.
