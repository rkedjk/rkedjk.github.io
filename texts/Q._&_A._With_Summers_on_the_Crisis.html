Q. & A. With Summers on the Crisis

https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2009/03/13/q-a-with-summers-on-the-crisis/

During the Q. and A. after Lawrence Summers’s speech today, he said his No. 1 aim had been to persuade people that the economy would not necessarily fix itself. It is caught in a self-reinforcing cycle, in which fear begets more fear, and the government had to take steps to break the cycle, he said. It also needed to put the economy back on a more sustainable path, he added. Also during the Q. and A.: Mr. Summers defended Timothy F. Geithner, the Treasury secretary. Some economists and investors have accused Mr. Geithner of laying out a financial rescue plan that is too vague. The “easy thing to do,” Mr. Summers said, would have been “to lay out a nine-point plan with illusion of specificity.” He said the Bush administration had essentially done this and then changed its plan “each month.” The Obama administration would instead focus on conducting stress tests of the banks, rather than rushing to take action before it knew what the best course of action was. “My suggestion is, we let this stress test continue,” he added. “We allow the process of supporting and reactivating the capital markets to be carried out. And we evaluate what the results have been some time from now.” He suggested that, in time, Mr. Geithner’s approach would be judged as the right one. Ruth Marcus of The Washington Post noted that when Mr. Summers had been an academic economist, he wrote about the role unionization played in increasing long-term unemployment. She then asked him for his view of the Employee Free Choice Act, a bill aimed that increasing unionization that is now before Congress. Mr. Summers didn’t directly answer the question. But he said the government needed to take action to strengthen unions. Like better education and health care systems, he said, a strong trade-union movement was crucial to sustainable economic growth. “I think it is hard to avoid the conclusion that the way in which our labor laws have functioned and been enforced and acted on over many years have not been constructive in terms of having a strong trade union movement.” He called for “an attempt to redress that balance” and said the administration would be listening to many perspectives on how to do so.
