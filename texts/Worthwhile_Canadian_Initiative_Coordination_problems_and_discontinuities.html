On the other hand, if there are only two possible values for P(i), for example P(t-1) and P*, then both P(t-1) and P* will be Nash Equilibria, if a > b. That's a coordination problem, because all firms can get stuck in the bad equilibrium P(t-1), unless someone convinces them to all jump to P* together. In a symmetric game, where all firms face the same loss function, the unique Nash Equilibrium is P(i)=P=P*. We only get multiple equilibria if b = 0, where it doesn't matter what they do, as long as they all do the same thing. Let Loss(i) = a(P(i)-P) 2 + b(P(i)-P*) 2 where P is the mean P(i) across all firms. The parameter 'a' measures the extent to which each firm wants to follow whatever the other firms are doing. It is a coordination problem (I think), but it's a bit more than just a coordination problem. There must be something else too, like a discontinuity, or something, to get that coordination problem. For example, maybe it's better if we all drive on the left hand side of the road (because, in the northern hemisphere, it reduces the number of tornadoes) . But we can get stuck in the Nash equilibrium where we all drive on the right, because any individual who deviates will crash. That's a coordination problem. If we all start out driving on the right, we get stuck there. We need someone to tell us all to stop our cars and switch sides at precisely midnight, if we want to jump to the better equilibrium. There's a discontinuity in that example, because a compromise, like driving in the middle of the road, is never individually optimal. Each firm's profit-maximising price is a function of all the other prices. And it's presumably an increasing function of other firms' prices. We call this "real rigidity" or "strategic complementarity". That helps us get price stickiness, but it isn't sufficient by itself. We need something more. We need some sort of discontinuity, like small menu costs, to get us stuck. Otherwise each individual firm will change its price just a little, when P* changes, even if it expected all other firms would hold their prices fixed. But if all firms change their prices just a little, it wouldn't make sense for each firm to expect all other firms to hold their prices fixed when P* changes. The only Nash equilibrium is for all firms to change their prices to match the new P*. Real rigidities/strategic complementarities (large positive 'a') mean that the menu costs (or whatever) we need to assume to get sticky prices are smaller than they otherwise would be. But they aren't alone sufficient. We still need the menu costs, or something. Strategic complementarities (in pricing) are a (stickiness) multiplier, but they still need something (some stickiness) to multiply. A load of us old guys were bashing our heads against this one back in the 1980s/90s. New Keynesian macro didn't start with Woodford. But try telling young people that today; they won't believe you.
