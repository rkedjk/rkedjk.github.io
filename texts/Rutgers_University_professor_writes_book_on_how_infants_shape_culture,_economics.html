Rutgers University professor writes book on how infants shape culture, economics

http://blog.nj.com/njv_bob_braun/2010/04/rutgers_university_professor_w.html

CAMDEN -- Babies tell us a lot about ourselves, and a Rutgers University professor is recording the lessons little guys are teaching. Janet Golden, a historian on the Camden campus, is writing a book — tentatively titled "Our Babies, My Baby" — about how our ideas about infants shape our culture and our economics. "It’s about both how we view babies culturally and how the idea of babyhood has driven consumer spending," says Golden, who received a National Endowment for the Humanities grant to do research. Among her primary sources is an archive at the University of California at Los Angeles of so-called "baby books" filled in by thousands of families during the 20th Century. Published both nationally and locally, these books not only recorded the lives of infants but also traced how free enterprise inveigled its way into new families to sell products — from baby formula to furniture to life insurance (for babies and fathers). While some baby books were expensive collectibles, others were published by manufacturers of "must-have" baby products and left space for ads by local merchants. They were often gifts to new families. "Babies began to be used to sell things," says Golden, who writes widely on child medical issues, including fetal alcohol syndrome and breast-feeding. Golden, the mother of two adult sons, says the way babies are popularly viewed has radically changed over the course of a century — from the collective concern of an entire society to statements of individuality. "It’s gone from how we had to help ‘our babies’ for the good of the country — lowering infant mortality, for example — to promoting the things parents must have and must do for ‘my baby’ to ensure their children are personally successful,’’ says Golden. She uses the baby book archives along with diaries, government documents and advertising to trace how babies went from becoming assets in a self-sufficient, farm-based economy to "expenses" in a specialized, urban society. Along the way, she explains how girly pink and boyish blue were invented to individualize the baby books. She found Amelia Earhart’s baby book and learned Maud Humphrey was a major baby book artist. She was the mother of Humphrey Bogart. Golden also plans to show how advice to young parents — usually mothers — has changed. Fortunately, moms often ignored warnings against holding or playing with infants because it made babies "too dependent’’ or "too excitable." But there’s far more here than popular culture. Golden traces the development of government policy and the clash between formula manufacturers — who first began selling their products in the 1860s — and advocates of breast feeding. "In the earliest years, upper-class women did not wish to breast-feed their children," she says. "Now, we face a problem of the poorest women not wanting to do it — with government subsidizing the purchase of formula." Babies, she says, first introduced urban migrants to a consumer society, and now support a multi-billion-dollar industry for parents, grandparents, other relatives and friends. Babies were, and are, used in advertising— even, once, to sell cigarettes. Karen Becker, a professor of marketing at The College of New Jersey, notes how the current economy affects working parents: "Many parents work two jobs or go in debt to ‘provide’ material things marketed to their children. The marketing messages combined with the guilt often felt by working mothers work simultaneously to reinforce a materialistic culture. If mom can’t be there, she can buy some thing (toy, video game, special food) to show her love and devotion.’’ Golden says, ``We’ve made babies the opening wedge into lifetimes of consumerism."
