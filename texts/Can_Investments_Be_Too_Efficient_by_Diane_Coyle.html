Can Investments Be Too Efficient? by Diane Coyle

Dear Ms. Coyle, This reminds me of the saying about richness and thinness of socialites. Although some of your arguments are solid, I think the conclusion, just like the title, are a bit misleading. All your arguments, in my opinion, just point out to the need of much, much better cost benefit analyses, not the superiority of pure seat of the pants, irrational decisions. True that long term benefits are sometimes ignored or unforeseable and that intergenerational generosity is at the heart of much human progress, but I am sure that quite a few boondoggles could be cited as counterexamples. An, albeit flawed, attempt at telling vision from mirage is better than no critical analysis at all. In particular, one might point out that more solid techniques to evaluate (always math involved, I am afraid) the impact of the imponderables is desirable, since this is often just done via worst-best case scenarios rather than mathematical probabilistic analysis. You can never be too efficient. Efficiency counts. It's just necessary to define your desired outcomes correctly.
