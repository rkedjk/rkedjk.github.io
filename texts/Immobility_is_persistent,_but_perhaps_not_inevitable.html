Immobility is persistent, but perhaps not inevitable

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2013/02/mobility-4

This week's Free exchange column discusses new research on rates of inter-generational social mobility (summary here). We are inviting experts in the field to comment on the piece and related research. Miles Corak, an economist at the University of Ottawa, responded here. Francisco Ferreira, lead economist at the World Bank's Development Research Group, responded here. Gregory Clark, an economist at the University of California, Davis, contributed posts here and here. Up next is a comment from economist Jason Long, of Wheaton College. BOTH the Free Exchange column and Miles Corak’s post point to the exceptionally high social mobility of the U.S. in the 19th century as evidence that in the right circumstances the grip of the past can be loosened and society can enjoy greater social mobility. That very well might be the case, but we should view the record of the 19th century U.S. with caution as a source of lessons for policy makers today. While our work documents the high mobility in the U.S. relative to Britain then or the U.S. today, it is not yet clear why that was the case or whether the factors that drove that high mobility are in any way policy-relevant today. In our paper, Joseph Ferrie and I suggest two possibilities as to why the 19th century U.S. was exceptionally mobile. First, the U.S. was way ahead of Britain when it came to universal public schooling. That’s not to say that the quality of public schooling in the U.S. was universally or even typically high, simply that it was widely available and far less variable in quality than in Victorian Britain, where schooling ranged from elite private schools to institutions that provided little more than child-minding. Second, people in the U.S. in the 19th century were not only socially mobile, they were also geographically mobile. We find that over a thirty-year period nearly two thirds of the men in our U.S. data changed their county of residence, compared with just over one quarter in Britain. We think this matters, as the U.S. of the 19th century was, to put it perhaps a bit simplistically, developing rather than developed. There was more regional specialisation and less economic integration than in Britain then or the U.S. now. We think this geographical differentiation may have allowed longer-distance internal migrants to exploit a sort of “locational arbitrage” opportunity: they could move to a place that favored their skills and inclinations as a way of boosting their mobility prospects. All this to say, to the extent that the U.S.’s high rate of social mobility a hundred years ago was driven by progressive public schooling policy, today’s policy makers can draw a positive lesson from the past. But that is much less the case if high mobility was due essentially to the rapidly-changing, unsettled nature of the U.S.’s incomplete development process. The second point is also, I’m afraid, somewhat pessimistic…at least for those who think that progressive public policy should seek to enhance social mobility. In a paper focusing just on Britain over the past hundred and fifty years, I show that social mobility in Victorian Britain was higher than we used to think based on older sources. With this upward revision, it appears to be the case that earnings mobility then was in fact not much different than in the late twentieth century. Like Greg Clark’s, this work highlights the significant persistence of mobility patterns over time, even in the face of dramatic social and institutional change. Finally, and more positively, it is important to note that, even if it is difficult for societies to increase their mobility rates through policy change, there are clear and dramatic actions that individuals can take to enhance their own or their children’s mobility prospects. One obvious example is investing in education. Another, alluded to above, is migration. While mobility was much lower in Britain than in the U.S. in the 19th century, many in Britain chose to “opt out” of their home labour market entirely by migrating to the U.S., Canada, or elsewhere. In an extension to our first paper, Ferrie and I show that British migrants to the U.S. had mobility rates equal to or even greater than the native-born in the U.S. Of course, migration today is much more restricted than it was in the past; nevertheless, the prospect of economic mobility still serves as a powerful draw to would-be migrants and remains a potent aspect of the American Dream.
