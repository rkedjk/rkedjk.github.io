Economists and the European democratic deficit

http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/criq.12197/full

People living on the periphery of the Eurozone are acutely aware of the crisis of democratic legitimacy that the European Union is facing. I am writing in March 2015, in the immediate aftermath of Syriza's decision to accept a four-month extension of Greece's bailout deal. It is too soon to say whether this was merely tactical, or represents the latest example of a newly elected government jettisoning its election promises under pressure from other European governments and the Troika (the European Commission, European Central Bank and IMF). But while the experience since 2008 of ‘programme countries’ such as Greece, Portugal and Ireland has been particularly extreme, there is a growing sense of disenfranchisement throughout the EU, which is beginning to have alarming political consequences. The unambiguously fascist Golden Dawn may soon be breathing down Syriza's neck, while the French National Front poses an existential threat to the entire European project. Ruling the Void, by the late Peter Mair, is a short, readable, insightful essay on the subject by an eminent political scientist.1 An odd book for an economist to bring up in his first Critical Quarterly column, you might think. But economics and economists are an important part of the story, even if Mair doesn't mention them explicitly. Most of us think we know what we mean when we say that a political system has democratic legitimacy. It means that we can kick the bums out at election time. Far more importantly, it means that we can kick the bums' policies out as well. It seems obvious that from this perspective, a political system increasingly defined by treaties preventing national governments from doing what their electorates might want them to do has a problem. Decisions that matter are being taken, not by parliaments, but by treaty-interpreting courts, or by treaty-implementing regulatory agencies, the European Central Bank, and other independent bodies staffed by technical experts and not directly accountable to the electorate. Mair succinctly describes a burgeoning political science literature that seeks to prove in somewhat scholastic fashion that, appearances to the contrary notwithstanding, the EU is in fact a democratically legitimate political system. Of course, it all depends on how you define the concept: ‘the contemporary scholarly literature is awash with the various current meanings of democracy and the many different nuances of legitimacy, such that almost any system of rule can be found to be acceptable – even that by judges and their equivalents’.2 One move that is frequently made is to distinguish between ‘input legitimacy’ and ‘output legitimacy’, terminology coined by Fritz Scharpf in 1970. Input legitimacy involves political participation ‘by the people’, which is not a forte of the EU. Output legitimacy, on the other hand, involves ‘Government for the people’, and ‘derives legitimacy from its capacity to solve problems requiring collective solutions’.3 The EU, it is said, gets the business done, and that is what really matters. Which is where economists, and economics, come in. First, these ‘problems requiring collective solutions’ are as often as not economic. Second, while voters have gradually been losing the ability to shape policy, experts working for international organisations trying to solve these problems have been gaining it, and these experts are very often economists. Third, economics has been used as a justification for this power shift, both in obvious ways (the EU was always justified to voters in terms of economic outcomes) and in more subtle ones. Output legitimacy in this context is essentially utilitarian.4 Projects like the European Single Market, or the proposed new Trans-Atlantic Trade and Investment Partnership, are justified on the grounds that they will raise aggregate euro or dollar income by a certain amount. (As it happens, the gains typically sound very large when expressed in billions of euros or dollars, but very small when expressed as a percentage of GDP. But that is another matter.) The assumption is that if aggregate GDP increases, this is a good thing, regardless of any distributional consequences that may arise. GDP is implicitly equated with aggregate welfare: by increasing it we are increasing the welfare of the community as a whole. Economists working for international institutions, and their bosses, and many other economists besides, push for labour market reforms, more competition, fewer state subsidies, fiscal probity, privatisation, and other policy measures on the grounds that they will increase GDP, and are thus a good thing. When such policies are enshrined in international treaties or EU law in an attempt to make them ‘democracy proof’, problems of democratic legitimacy arise, as well as complaints of technocratic, economistic and undemocratic governance. It seems fair to say that economists, or at least some economists, are part of the problem; but is it fair to blame their discipline? As it happens, many economists would strenuously deny being utilitarian. Their argument is that they cannot be utilitarian, since they don't believe that utility is a measurable concept like weight or mass; they therefore cannot possibly be in the business of maximising the sum of all utilities enjoyed by the population as a whole. It makes no sense, they point out, to claim that something makes me twice as happy as something else (utility is not ‘cardinal’, like weight or height). All that we can say is that it makes me happier (utility is ‘ordinal’). We can rank states of the world, not attach numbers to them. Utility cannot be compared across individuals, nor can one add together the utilities enjoyed by everyone in society. Utilitarianism is therefore logically impossible. This is in fact what we teach our students in first year. (As an aside, the fact that utility can't be compared across people is perhaps just as well. If it were, we would surely notice that a thousand dollars of extra income makes a lot more difference to a beggar than a banker, and who knows in what radical directions such a train of thought might eventually lead.) But this state of affairs is deeply unsatisfactory to many economists, who feel that their discipline ought to be a force for good in the world. If utility is not comparable across people, then how can economists ever say that a policy shift is good or bad, given that any major change in policy will inevitably lead to both winners and losers? Here economics lecturers say various things to students at different points in the undergraduate curriculum, not all of them consistent with each other. They start by being purist: strictly speaking, they say, we can only prefer one policy outcome to another if it makes at least somebody better off, without making anyone else worse off; in other words, if the policy shift leads to what is known as a ‘Pareto improvement’. But if economists took this injunction seriously, then they could never say that free trade was preferable to protection, or that the European Single Market was a Good Thing, since trade and competition produce both winners and losers. What is an economist determined to be useful to the world, by distinguishing between good and bad policies, to do? One solution is to assume the problem away altogether, and seek refuge in an imaginary world with just one ‘agent’. If Robinson Crusoe suddenly finds that he can trade with the outside world, he will be better off: no need for interpersonal comparisons here. This is how the textbooks show undergraduates that free trade is, usually, preferable to protection, and economists may simply assume, without giving the matter much thought, that those undergraduate lessons are applicable in the infinitely messier real world. The second is to acknowledge the problem, but to shift towards a metric that, unlike utility, is impeccably cardinal – money. According to the Kaldor–Hicks compensation criterion, a shift from one state of the world to another – say from protection to free trade – can be judged as beneficial to society if it would be possible for the winners to financially compensate the losers, and still remain better off. The losers don't have to be compensated – to make such a claim would be to stray into political territory, off-limits to economists. It is the possibility of such compensation that matters. In other words, while the change may not be a Pareto improvement, it could potentially become one (given suitable compensation). This is sufficient to allow economists to conclude that the policy change in question is beneficial. This is, of course, just another, more complicated, way of assuming the problem away, given that the compensation is not paid, and that the policy shift produces losers. What we are left with is something that looks an awful lot like crude utilitarianism – if A gains 1,000 dollars, and B loses 500 dollars, this is good for society. This seems very close to the assertion that if total GDP increases by 500 dollars, this is a good thing – just as those economists working for international organisations, and their bosses, tend to assume. Princeton's Uwe Reinhardt calls this bastardised utilitarianism.5 Bastardised in what way? First, this is utilitarianism with the potentially left-wing bits stripped out. If A is a lot richer than B, we no longer have to worry about whether those extra 1,000 dollars that A gains will matter as much to him as the 500 dollar loss will matter to B. By focusing on the money, rather than on the underlying welfare of the people earning the money, we no longer need to worry about income distribution. Second, economic theorists have demonstrated that the Kaldor–Hicks compensation criterion makes no sense on its own terms. Almost immediately after the criterion was proposed, Tibor Scitovsky pointed out that it is logically possible for state A to be preferred to state B on Kaldor–Hicks grounds, but for state B also to be preferable to state A.6 This should have killed off the concept, but the desire to be useful is a powerful one, and economists have tended to avert their eyes from the problem. The result is a utilitarianism that is remarkably un-self-reflective and philosophically naïve: an understandable human consequence of the fact that economics denies the logical possibility of utilitarianism in the first place. 7 Efficient policies attempt to increase aggregate welfare, that is, to improve the conditions of all, or almost all, individuals and groups in society, while the objective of redistributive policies is to improve the conditions of one group at the expense of another. Now, in a democracy, redistribution of income and wealth can only be achieved by majority decision since any issue over which there is unavoidable conflict cannot be resolved by unanimous agreement. Efficient policies, on the other hand, may be thought of as positive-sum games where everybody can gain. Hence, such policies could be decided, in principle, by unanimity … It follows that redistributive policies can only be legitimated by the will of the majority, while efficient policies are basically legitimated by the results they achieve.8 In other words, redistributive policies require political decision-making, while decisions about efficient policies can safely be delegated to ‘non-majoritarian’, technocratic institutions, since everyone benefits from efficient policies. The problem with this line of argument is, of course, that the set of economic policies involving no distributional consequences is vanishingly small: notice the telling ‘almost all’ in the quotation above, and the way in which the statement shifts between efficiency in the sense of Pareto and something far more akin to Kaldor–Hicks. This is an argument in favour of technocracy that is self-refuting. How is this relevant for the EU's democratic legitimacy? Giandomenico Majone is an eminent political scientist who has become a vocal critic of the EU's democratic deficit.In earlier work, however, he argued that it makes sense for elected governments to delegate regulatory powers to ‘non-majoritarian’ or unelected bodies such as the European Commission, who are not subject to political pressures, and can provide technical expertise. A key distinction for Majone is between ‘efficient’ and ‘redistributive’ policies:In other words, redistributive policies require political decision-making, while decisions about efficient policies can safely be delegated to ‘non-majoritarian’, technocratic institutions, since everyone benefits from efficient policies. The problem with this line of argument is, of course, that the set of economic policies involving no distributional consequences is vanishingly small: notice the telling ‘almost all’ in the quotation above, and the way in which the statement shifts between efficiency in the sense of Pareto and something far more akin to Kaldor–Hicks. This is an argument in favour of technocracy that is self-refuting. But it is easy enough to see how making a link between ‘efficiency’ and ‘distribution’ on the one hand, and ‘technocracy’ and ‘democratic politics’ on the other, might make sense to economists who believe, more or less fuzzily, in Kaldor–Hicks efficiency, and regard themselves as well qualified to wield this concept in the service of society. And in turn it is perhaps not so surprising that such economists, and their bosses, often see ‘structural reforms’ and other market-enhancing measures as the solution to the Eurozone's problems, and are hostile to the notion that electorates should be allowed to overturn status quo policies, because, after all, there is no alternative. To which there are two responses. First, as noted above, economic theory provides no justification for neatly separating efficiency and distribution, or for maximising GDP to the exclusion of everything else, or for taking the politics out of policy-making. And second, as the creation of the Euro and the Eurozone crisis show, technocrats are more than capable of royally screwing up: so much for ‘output legitimacy’. And in fact, had they paid more attention to what academic economists have had to say about the economics of monetary unions, they would not have screwed up nearly as badly.9 All of which goes to show that ‘economists say’, and ‘economics says’, can be two very different things.
