Rethinking the Japanese Experience

https://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/jamessurowiecki/2009/04/rethinking-the-1.html

In making the case for nationalizing the country’s most troubled big banks, Paul Krugman has, like many, argued that those banks are zombies, “unable to supply the credit the economy needs,” and that therefore taking them over is essential to economic recovery. “The longer we live with zombie banks, the harder it will be to end the economic crisis.” And he’s pointed many times, as most supporters of nationalization have, to Japan’s economic malaise during the nineteen-nineties as the quintessential example of what happens when you try to keep banks afloat rather than nationalizing them. Thus his negative take on the Obama Administration’s approach to the banking crisis: “It’s Japan all over again.” (Along the same lines, Nouriel Roubini and Matthew Richardson wrote in February that “unless we want to become like Japan in the 1990s,” we need to nationalize “the banking system.”) In this context, Krugman’s post today on Japan’s economic recovery (which took place from 2003-2007) is pretty extraordinary. Referring to a recent post on the lessons the U.S. should draw from Japan’s experience, Krugman says, “It’s hard, looking at the data, to see much role for banking reform in Japan’s recovery.” Just to underscore that: Krugman says that there’s not much evidence that Japan’s banking reforms did much to help its economy recover. But if that’s the case, then the Japanese experience doesn’t actually prove that nationalization is necessary to get the economy moving again, or that the Obama Administration’s current approach is going to prolong the crisis. I’ve written before that the relevance of the Japanese experience to our own has been overstated, since the U.S. has successfully weathered two major banking crises without nationalization. But Krugman’s post goes a step further than that, showing that even if you do think the Japanese experience is relevant, it does not demonstrate that we have to nationalize the banks in order for the economy to recover. That’s a striking thing for Krugman to say. It’ll be interesting to see if it gets much attention.
