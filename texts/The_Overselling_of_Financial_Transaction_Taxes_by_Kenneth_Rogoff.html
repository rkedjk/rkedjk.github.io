The Overselling of Financial Transaction Taxes by Kenneth Rogoff

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/financial-transaction-tax-shortcomings-by-kenneth-rogoff-2016-06

"The fundamental problem with FTTs is that they are distortionary; for example, by driving down stock prices, they make raising capital more expensive for firms. In the long run, this lowers labor productivity and wage levels. " This string of assertions appears to be at the heart of the argument against a FTT, so {1} Please tell us why would an FTT "drive down" stock prices? They would presumably slow down the pace of transactions {that is what is being taxed} but why would they change the fundamental value of the underlying asset? {2} U.S. corporations are self funding -- internally generated cash flows exceed capital expenditures. In fact, for the past many years US corporates have been buying back stock to inflate their share prices rather than issuing stock to fund capital expenditure. So why would an FTT have a negative impact on capital expenditure? {3} The linkage between growth in labor productivity and growth in wages in the US was a tight one from 1950 until 1975, but not any more. From 1980 to 2010 wages stagnated as productivity continued to climb. Asserting that a FTT would somehow be harmful to wages is a real stretch. What has been harmful to wages in the past 35 to 40 years is the "transfer of income" from wages to profits by US corporations. Seems to me productivity growth and wage growth was pretty good in the US from 1950 to 1964 when there was an FTT, and you admit yourself that the tax in the U.K."does not seem to cause much harm". And the truth is HFTs are already "taxing" stock transactions in the US, so why not let those revenues accrue to the US Treasury instead of the some members of asset management industry and the brokers that facilitate their skimming?
