The Resentment Zone: Losing Means-Tested Benefits

https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2010/03/22/the-resentment-zone-losing-means-tested-benefits/

Nancy Folbre is an economics professor at the University of Massachusetts Amherst. I wouldn’t call it a “dead zone” as some bloggers do, but “resentment zone” seems like an apt description of the income range in which families lose eligibility for means-tested benefits like food stamps, Medicaid and the earned-income tax credit. The size and shape of that zone may help explain why some lower-middle-income families are Tea Party movement enthusiasts. Losing access to government benefits has the same effect on household budgets as paying more taxes. Benefit reduction means that the bite taken out of an additional dollar of income, or the effective marginal tax rate, goes up as income goes up. Many state and federal benefits start phasing out somewhere between the poverty line and twice that line ($22,000 to $44,000 for a family of four in 2009). Taking both income taxes and the loss of benefits into account, marginal tax rates in that range of the income distribution are often higher than the rate of 35 percent applied to the taxable income over $372,950 of married couples filing jointly for 2009. The rate at which benefits are phased out depends on which benefits people are taking advantage of. Participation rates for many programs, like food stamps, fall far short of 100 percent of those eligible. Most government surveys don’t collect data on program participation. One that does, the Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP) is widely considered inaccurate. But an innovative study of Wisconsin households that merges data from tax returns and state administrative data shows that some low-income families faced a combined state and federal marginal tax rate of 44 percent in 2000. Some fell off a cliff when they earned just enough income to disqualify them from the state’s public health insurance program. Reasonable assumptions based on hypothetical program participation show that many households with children face a hefty marriage penalty in the form of higher marginal tax rates because the pooled income of husband and wife reduces their eligibility for benefits. It’s difficult to estimate the size of the resulting disincentives to work longer hours or to get married. But even if these are small, the resentment factor can be large. That resentment factor may be growing stronger as the Obama administration struggles to expand two important means-tested benefits: health insurance and financial aid for college students in the form of Pell grants. The Senate health care bill includes subsidy phaseouts that increase marginal tax rates, with a particularly sharp spike for families with taxable income between $40,000 and $50,000. Pell grants aimed at students from low-income families leave middle-income families in the lurch as tuition and fees at public universities go up. The marginal tax rate built into that program ranges from 22 to 47 percent. The structure of benefits that both programs offer is complex, making it difficult for families to ascertain their eligibility or assess the possible net benefits (which may also depend on their participation in other programs). Many Tea Party enthusiasts would like to eliminate means-tested benefits altogether. That might make some people feel better. After all, if you’re not getting any benefits, you can’t complain about losing them. And maybe some people, comparing their relative position, look down rather than up. Intensifying the effects of poverty and unemployment could make those who barely escape them feel victorious. But I think many of the disaffected would welcome simpler, universal benefits, like extending Medicare as a choice to everyone or minimizing tuition and fees at public community colleges and universities. Short of such ambitious goals, progressive policy-makers should think harder about simplifying both public programs and the tax code. You don’t need to study behavioral economics to know that complexity, confusion and frustration tend to amplify resentment.
