Are the Stress Test Results Bad News?

https://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/jamessurowiecki/2009/05/are-the-stress-test-results-bad-news.html

When news broke early this morning that the government’s stress test would require Bank of America to raise $34 billion in equity, S. & P. futures prices dropped ten points in a matter of minutes. Yet by the time the market opened this morning, the market had turned positive, and Bank of America’s stock finished today up more than fifteen per cent. Numerous pundits have already pegged the jump in the stock as mystifying and irrational, given the fact that having to raise $34 billion hardly looks like a sign that Bank of America is healthy. But there are two important factors at work. First, as big as $34 billion sounds, it’s far, far less of a shortfall than many people were predicting. (Just a couple of weeks ago, reasonable commentators thought it was plausible that, together, Bank of America and Citigroup might need “a few hundred billion” in capital between them.) Second, and more important, if the leak is correct, Bank of America does not need to raise $34 billion in new capital, but $34 billion in new equity, which means that it can meet that requirement by converting a portion of its TARP money from preferred shares into common shares (although I suspect it will try to raise at least part of the equity in the private markets). That conversion would, to be sure, dilute the stakes of existing shareholders. But it would also solidify the health of the bank in regulators’ eyes, and the long-term benefits of that seem to outweigh the costs of dilution (that’s presumably why the stock is up). This distinction between new capital and new equity hasn’t been made very clearly in the media, perhaps because whoever’s doing the leaking hasn’t been very clear. (Here are a couple of examples of the confusion.) But it is a crucial distinction, because effectively what the government is saying is that Bank of America has more than enough capital to be considered solvent—it just needs more of that capital to be in the form of equity. We can debate whether the focus on equity (as opposed to other forms of capital) makes sense. What matters most, I think, is that the stress tests appear to be concluding—as the I.M.F.’s recent Global Financial Stability Report did—that even if the economy gets significantly worse, the U.S.’ big banks have enough capital to weather the storm. Given that, it’s not too surprising that bank stocks are continuing to rally.
