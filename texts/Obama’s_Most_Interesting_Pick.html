Obama’s Most Interesting Pick

https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2008/12/02/obamas-most-interestin-pick/

Edward L. Glaeser is an economist at Harvard. Some members of President-elect Barack Obama’s all-star economic team have been well known to the public for years, if not decades. The dour, but reassuring, figure of Paul Volcker — cigar in mouth — brought credibility back to the Federal Reserve before I was a teenager. My former boss Lawrence Summers has received both more praise and more approbation than any human being should have to endure. But Christina Romer, who has been selected to be chairwoman of the Council of Economic Advisers, is something of a cipher to most non-economists. That is a pity, since she is among the most interesting and impressive of Mr. Obama’s choices. She is one of the world’s pre-eminent economic historians and her scholarship makes her particularly well-suited for advising a president during this tumultuous period. Professor Romer first burst onto the economic scene in the mid-1980s with her work on the history of the business cycle. The pre-Romer consensus had been that the United States economy was far more volatile before World War I than after World War II. Mr. Summers, together with Brad DeLong, an economist from the University of California, Berkeley, had written papers attributing the seeming stabilization of American output to improvements in economic policy-making, like the establishment of the Federal Reserve System. Professor Romer revisited the historical gross domestic product numbers, which the Nobel laureate Simon Kuznets had assembled by using old data on commodity outputs. Mr. Kuznets had assumed a link between the production of commodities (like crude petroleum and pig iron) and the production of all goods and services that was far too high. According to Professor Romer’s new estimates, the business cycle was not appreciably more volatile before World War I than it was after World War II. Her work has been among the most influential written on economic history in the last 30 years. On an intellectual level, Professor Romer’s work challenged the view that over the 20th century, economic policy has consistently improved. More recently, she has returned to this theme, arguing that the quality of macroeconomic policy sharply deteriorated from the 1950s to the 1960s. Professor Romer displayed plenty of courage in challenging well-established orthodoxies, and that was a good thing. I yield to no one in my admiration for Lawrence Summers, but it is better for him and the country that he has colleagues who are willing and able to argue with him. Much of Professor Romer’s work since the 1980s has focused on monetary policy. With her husband, David Romer, she produced a new methodology for estimating the impact of monetary contractions on economic output. The novelty in this work relied on a close reading of Federal Reserve minutes, which suggests a tolerance for the written word that is rare among economists. The Romers’ work confirms Milton Friedman’s finding that monetary contractions can significantly and durably increase unemployment. The importance of monetary policy is another running theme in Ms. Romer’s work. She has argued that expansions in the money supply, not New Deal spending, led to the end of the Great Depression. The key facts are that the monetary expansion during the late 1930s was quite large, and that New Deal fiscal expansion (i.e., new government spending) was too small to explain the size of the recovery. A later paper generalizes this argument to postwar recessions, making the case that monetary policy has been far more important to recovery than fiscal stimulus. In most cases, fiscal responses, like lower taxes or more government spending, were too little too late. What do these papers imply about the right policies to fight the current downturn? The most straightforward interpretation of Professor Romer’s work is that lower interest rates are always going to be more effective at fighting recession than government spending. A second interpretation is that fiscal policy can work but only if that spending is speedy and on a gigantic scale. The first interpretation would lead Ms. Romer, as chairwoman of the economic council, to be skeptical toward a trillion dollar stimulus package; the second interpretation would make her a big spending booster. Whichever route her ideas take her, President-elect Obama has appointed a first-rate economist to lead the Council of Economic Advisers. Her scholarship is deep and important. Her intellectual tenacity and good judgment give her the right temperament to head the economic council. Academics don’t always make good policy makers, but I’m delighted that Professor Romer is going to Washington.
