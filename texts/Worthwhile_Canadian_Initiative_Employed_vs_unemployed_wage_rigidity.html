Worthwhile Canadian Initiative: Employed vs unemployed wage rigidity

http://worthwhile.typepad.com/worthwhile_canadian_initi/2011/08/employed-vs-unemployed-wage-flexibility.html

I'm going to disagree. If (note I said "if") you believe that wage rigidity is the problem, then: as employment falls on the way down, it's wage rigidity of the employed that's the problem; but what prevents employment coming straight back up again is wage rigidity of the unemployed. Scott Sumner and Alex Tabarrok say it's (mostly) the wage rigidity of the employed that's the problem. Suppose you believe that (nominal) wage rigidity in the face of a decline in the (nominal) demand curve for labour is what causes unemployment in a recession. Is it wage rigidity of the employed workers or of the unemployed workers that's the problem? The reason is simple. "Rational People [and firms] Think at the Margin" is #3 of Greg Mankiw's Ten Principles of Economics. But there is a left-side margin and a right-side margin. The left-side margin applies to decreases in employment. The right-side margin applies to increases in employment. On the way down, when the firm is contemplating decreasing employment below the existing level, the firm is comparing the wage of existing workers with their marginal revenue product. "If I decrease employment by one worker, will I save more in wages than I lose in revenue?" If the answer is "yes", then the firm will increase its profits by reducing employment. But a sufficient cut in wages of the currently employed would (if wage rigidity is the problem) change that answer to "no". Now, assume that the employed do have rigid wages, and so the firm reduces employment until the marginal revenue product increases sufficiently to equal the wage of the employed. We have equality on the left-hand margin, and we've hit bottom on employment. What now prevents employment from rising back up again? The firm now looks at the right-hand margin. "If I increase employment by one worker, will I gain more in revenue than it costs me in wages?" If the answer is "yes", then the firm will increase employment. And a sufficiently low wage for the currently unemployed (if wage rigidity is the problem) would ensure the answer is "yes" rather than "no". So if you believe that wage rigidity is the problem, then it's wage rigidity of the currently employed that is the problem when employment falls, but it's wage rigidity of the currently unemployed that stops employment immediately rising straight back up again. You need wage rigidity of both the employed and the unemployed to explain why employment goes down and stays down (until the demand curve increases again). (Sure, this is setting aside the question of whether the firm could replace the employed with the unemployed at lower wages with no decline in employment even for an instant. Or whether the employed won't let the firm hire the unemployed at lower wages than they get. And this is setting aside efficiency wage theories of unemployment where it is the behaviour of the unemployed if they became employed that is the problem.) The underlying macroeconomic model I have in mind is one where: the Aggregate Demand curve slopes down; something causes the AD curve to shift left; prices are perfectly flexible; but nominal wages are sticky. So we move down along an upward-sloping Aggregate Supply curve, the price level falls, nominal wages stay the same, real wages rise, and output and employment fall. And stay down, either until nominal wages eventually fall, or the AD curve shifts right again. Textbook. Now, if you don't like this conclusion (that you need rigid wages of both the employed and the unemployed to make the theory work) you might try sticky prices as an alternative. Or assume that both prices and wages are sticky. (And this still doesn't answer Tyler Cowen's question. I may try that in a later post. I wonder if he would accept Red Green's answer?
