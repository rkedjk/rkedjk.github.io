It’s a Free Agent Nation, Except in Washington

https://blogs.hbr.org/fox/2011/03/its-a-free-agent-nation-except.html

I’ve just been rereading Daniel Pink’s famous 1997 Fast Company article on “Free Agent Nation.” It has withstood the test of time pretty danged well. In 1997, Pink had recently left a job as Vice President Al Gore’s speechwriter to strike out on his own, and discovered (or decided, if you prefer) that he was part of an important new movement sweeping the land. Not everyone believed him. I didn’t — I was a happy employee of a very large corporation (Time Warner), and I was and remain skeptical of sweeping pronouncements of economic change. What bothered me most about Pink’s article was its celebratory tone. Sure, the downsizings of the 1980s and early 1990s had forced lots of people to make a go of it as free agents. But did they really want to work that way? Wouldn’t they just go back to big corporations if the chance was afforded them? Score that one mostly for Dan Pink. He and many others have done awfully well as a free agents. And, on a more macro scale, large corporations simply haven’t afforded the opportunity for a mass return to their ranks. From 1997 through 2005, the net change in employment at existing firms was -11.4 million, according to data gathered by the Census Bureau and tweaked by Tim Kane at the Kauffman Foundation. It was only because of the 28.7 million jobs created at new firms over that period that overall employment didn’t go backwards over that period. The Kauffman Foundation plugged this as meaning that U.S. job growth was “driven entirely by startups.” Which isn’t untrue, but it gives a picture of job growth being driven by the Groupons and Facebooks of the world. In fact, most “startups” are and remain single-employee enterprises. They are new members of Free Agent Nation. Ying Lowrey, an economist at the U.S. Small Business Administration, reported on this at the annual meeting of the American Economic Association in Denver in January. She estimated that between 1997 and 2008, startups had created an average of 2.5 million “entrepreneurial jobs” (that is, jobs for their founders) a year, and just 1 million “paid employment jobs.” I learned about Lowrey’s study in a new essay in the journal Democracy by Andrei Cherny. Cherny, who’s a sucker for grand historical parallels, contends that Lowrey’s presentation at the AEA marked the beginning of the “Individual Age” just as Frederick Jackson Turner’s 1893 paper on the closing of the American Frontier marked the end of the Agrarian Age. Maybe, maybe not. But Cherny’s big point in the essay seems to be right: something significant has changed about how Americans make a living, and politicians have been extremely slow in adapting to this transformation. This was a theme in Pink’s article, too, and it is striking how little has changed in the intervening 14 years. The business world has adapted pretty well to the needs of Free Agent Nation. Sometime in the past decade, for example, the infotech infrastructure available at low or no cost to the individual free agent swept right by what most corporate IT departments are able and/or willing to provide employees. But areas where government is involved, from health care to the tax code, have been painfully slow to evolve. Cherny, who ran as a Democrat for Arizona state treasurer last year (and lost), sees both Democrats and Republicans stuck in old, institutional ways of looking at the economy. Democrats fight for the benefits of employees; Republicans for the rights of employers. The fact that, for an ever-larger percentage of workforce, employees and employers are one and the same doesn’t seem to have sunk in, at least not to the D.C. mainstream. Well, actually, it may have sunk in a little: The Obamacare health reforms do seem to provide better options and more protections for free agent workers, and the move from employer-dependent pensions to 401(k)s and IRAs makes sense in a labor market with more independent workers. But Obamacare is still an employer-centric system, in contrast to Ron Wyden’s much more free-agent-friendly reform proposal. And while 401(k)s and IRAs are in many ways suited for a mobile and independent workforce, they so far haven’t been nearly as effective a means of saving for retirement as old-style pensions are. That’s partly because, with old-style pensions, the employers are on the hook if there are shortfalls — which, as we’ve seen in the bankruptcy of big automakers and the political battles raging in Wisconsin in other states — isn’t really a sustainable setup. But it’s also because, as a rule, professionally managed pension funds perform better and pay less out in fees than individually managed retirement accounts. The Dutch have a retirement savings system that nicely splits the difference; you sure don’t hear much talk about creating such a system here. Then there’s the federal tax code. For wage slaves, it’s usually not all that complicated. For free agents (and I speak as a part-time free agent), filling out tax returns can be an epic task. None of this should be a big surprise, given that free agents don’t really have anybody to look out for them in Washington or in the state capitals. Employees have unions, although they are of course a much-weakened force for private sector workers, and currently under assault in the public sector. Employers have the Chamber of Commerce and a bunch of other lobbying groups. Retirees have the AARP. And free agents have, well, the Freelancers Union, a pioneering New York-based effort (profiled in Pink’s article, back when it was still called Working Today) that has understandably been more focused on providing health insurance for its members than becoming a political force. Something has to give here. To get us laws that reflect the new workplace reality, Free Agent Nation, by its very nature dispersed and allergic to large organizations, needs to develop a unified voice. Can it?
