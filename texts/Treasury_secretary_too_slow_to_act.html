Treasury secretary too slow to act

http://www.sfgate.com/cgi-bin/article.cgi?f=/c/a/2008/10/13/EDVM13FTJK.DTL

As the financial crisis has deepened, governments and central banks have rolled out one unprecedented initiative after another. On Tuesday, the Federal Reserve announced that, for the first time in its history, it would buy unsecured commercial paper. On Wednesday, Britain unveiled an enormous bank recapitalization scheme. On Thursday, the U.S. Treasury hinted that some of the $700 billion appropriated to mop up toxic securities would be used to buy equity stakes in banks instead. On Friday, G7 leaders vowed to use "all available tools" - whatever that means - to stabilize the global financial system. Every economist worth his salt understands that governments must do two things to stabilize financial markets: They must inject capital directly into banks; and they must temporarily guarantee all new inter-bank lending. Economies need banks. And banks need capital - resources of their own, in addition to deposits - to operate. They can lend their capital. They can use it as a buffer against financial shocks. Having "skin in the game" gives them something to lose if they are imprudent. Unfortunately, the crisis has resulted in the destruction of upward of $500 billion of bank capital in the United States that is yet to be replaced. Treasury Secretary Henry Paulson's previous plan, under which he would have bought toxic securities with a current market value of a dollar for exactly one dollar, would have done nothing to solve this problem. U.S. banks would have had no more funds of their own to lend. In the current environment, there is only one solution: The government needs to inject capital into the banks by purchasing financial stakes in them - partial nationalization, if you will. Absent that, our banking system will not start up again. But even solvent banks are reluctant to lend to other banks, given fear of more defaults and failures. The solution, adopted by Britain last Wednesday, is a temporary government guarantee on new inter-bank lending. Implementing this in the United States is more complicated, because the nonbank financial sector made up of hedge funds, private equity, insurance companies and the like is larger here. But it is by no means impossible. Yet, while the Treasury Department finally recognizes the urgency of recapitalizing the banks and acknowledges that there is no alternative to taxpayer funds, it has not provided the details of its plan. And it refuses to come to terms with the need to guarantee inter-bank lending because it sees the problem as just too intellectually complex. Without U.S. leadership, or even a willingness of the U.S. Treasury to agree to specifics, substantive action by the G7 nations was impossible. This is why the finance ministers' communiqué on Friday contained only empty platitudes ("all available tools"). What's next? As Secretary Paulson dithers, Britain this morning injected public funds into its banks. On Sunday, other European countries, for their part, released details about how they will recapitalize their financial institutions. Even more importantly, they committed to immediately guaranteeing all new credits extended by their banks to one another. As bank lending in Europe starts up again, financial markets there will stabilize. But here in the United States, the situation will only worsen. If we don't do the same here - now rather than, say, next week - funds will begin to flee America for Europe. If you were a corporation, where would you keep your working balances? In well-capitalized European banks, or in their insolvent American counterparts? In whose stock markets would you invest? In those of European countries taking decisive action or those of an America whose government was unable to act? Paulson can announce today that the government will inject capital in the amount of 5 percent of bank assets into each and every bank in the United States to bring the average capital ratio back up to a workable 10 percent. The injection should take the form of preferred stock, meaning that the taxpayer gets first claim on a bank's dividends, if and when it recovers. That stock should come with voting rights, giving taxpayers a say in the behavior of the banks in which they are now invested. The Treasury secretary should also announce that the government is guaranteeing all new bank loans, whether to other banks, insurance companies or other nonbank financial institutions. Or he can keep acting like a deer in the headlights.
