Worthwhile Canadian Initiative: The federal government should not cut taxes

http://worthwhile.typepad.com/worthwhile_canadian_initi/2014/10/the-federal-government-should-not-cut-taxes.html

The deficit is equal to government spending less government revenues. It is the excess of expenditures over taxes and other receipts in any given year. The deficit is distinct from the debt: the total amount that the government owes. Even though the federal deficit is shrinking, the federal government still has a substantial debt: its "net worth" at the end of 2013 was negative, at -$547 billion dollars ( Fiscal Reference Table #47). Measured relative to the size of Canada's economy, that's around 29 percent of GDP. In 2013, the federal deficit had shrunk to $13 billion. This year it may disappear entirely. The federal government has announced that, once the budget is in balance, its first priority will be "to provide tax relief to hard-working Canadian families." There is no consensus as to what the "right" ratio of debt to GDP should be. Comparing our net financial liabilities - gross debt less governments' financial assets - to that of our G7 counterparts, Canada appears to be a model of fiscal prudence: (As an aside: Canada had Conservative governments briefly in 1979/80, from 1984 to 1993, and from 2006 onwards; Canada had Liberal governments from 1970 to 1979, 1980 to 1984, and from 1993 to 2006.) The net financial liabilities pictured above include provincial balances, with Alberta's reserves netted out against other provinces' debts. They also set the Canada and Quebec Pension Plans' reserves against governments' liabilities. If one looks only at what Canadian governments owe, the picture is not quite so rosy: While the federal government might not actually lose money this year, it is still carrying hundreds of billions of dollars of liabilities, as are most of Canada's provinces. Moreover, although the federal government's budget is close to being in balance, the provinces' budgets, especially those of Ontario and Quebec, are distinctly unbalanced: In the long run, provincial deficits - and provincial debt - create problems for the federal government. Ever since Confederation, provincial governments have found themselves unable to raise enough taxes to pay for roads, education, policing, health care, and social services - the goods and services that are provincial responsibilities according to Canada's constitution. Basically, provinces aren't very good at raising tax revenue. If one province tries to raise its tax rate, people and businesses can move to another province with lower taxes. Once upon a time equalization protected provinces from the base-eroding effects of high tax rates - any loss in tax base was offset by increased equalization payments (see this paper by Michael Smart). However there is now a ceiling on equalization payments: total equalization payments can grow no faster than Canada's nominal GDP. Yes, the provinces could make some dent in that $50 billion or so deficit by raising taxes - but it will be tough. Moreover, population aging is facing provinces with a double-whammy: as the Baby Boomers hit their senior years, they start paying less in taxes (because they've retired) and start demanding more in health care and other services. With limited scope to raise taxes, Ontario and Quebec (the big indebted provinces) will inevitably find themselves having to cut spending. There is some scope for some provinces to cut some spending - Ontario's Drummond Report made a few suggestions. Unfortunately, provincial spending cuts have the potential to rebound upon the federal government. First, provinces finance education, roads, policing, infrastructure - things that are necessary to the functioning of a strong and just economy. If the inability of provinces to pay for goods and services hurts economic growth, and increases poverty or inequality, this is a problem for a federal government that cares about these things. Provincial spending cuts also cause political challenges at the federal level. When people can't get the health care they feel they need, they don't sit down and read the 1982 Canada Act to figure out who to blame. When health care once again becomes a hot political issue, the federal government will come under intense political pressure to increase transfers to the provinces (see also this analysis by Marc Joffe). While the federal government is currently in a much stronger fiscal position than the provinces, it also faces challenges in the longer-term, as I have argued before. With increasing numbers of retirees and fewer workers, and rising income inequality, more income is coming in the form of capital income. However capital income is harder to tax than employment income. Other changes, such as the growth of the service and virtual economies, as well as rising self-employment, are also making taxes harder to collect. On the corporate side, tax avoidance remains a concern. All the signs suggest that governments will face increasing difficulties collect tax revenues going forward. The federal government may have (temporarily) restored the budget to balance. But Canada as a nation is facing some serious fiscal challenges. This is not the time to cut taxes.
