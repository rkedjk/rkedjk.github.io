Worthwhile Canadian Initiative: Someone is Getting Health Spending Under Control

http://worthwhile.typepad.com/worthwhile_canadian_initi/2011/04/someone-is-getting-health-spending-under-control.html

The sustainability of provincial government health spending is a big issue and drug spending in particular has garnered a fair amount of attention. Whereas in 1975, drug spending only made up about 2 percent of public sector health spending in Canada, today it makes up nearly 10 percent and indeed has been one of the fastest growing health expenditure categories. The common perception is that government drug spending is on an inexorable upward treadmill driven by technological extension and an aging population. Yet, the accompanying figure yields some surprises when provincial comparisons are made. Real per capita provincial government drug expenditures in 1997 dollars from the Canadian Institute for Health Information National Health Expenditure Database are plotted down below for the period 1995 to 2010. The trend is definitely upward over this time period for all the provinces but there is something interesting going on particularly in Manitoba and British Columbia where real per capita spending has actually declined over the last few years. Alberta and New Brunswick have also flattened out. However, the remaining provinces have continued with strong upward trends since 2005. British Columbia is definitely the most interesting case given that their drug costs per capita appear to have been successfully restrained since 2001. From the second highest provincial per capita spending in the late 1990s, it is now the lowest. While the determinants of spending are complex, let me venture the following. British Columbia has implemented income based drug benefit programs for seniors and non-seniors since 2003 and pioneered Reference Based Pricing (physicians are required to prescribe the cheapest drug for certain medication categories or obtain government permission to prescribe something more expensive) for their provincial drug plan starting in 1995. These policies have probably been a factor though obviously I am not controlling for anything else. If anyone else has any thoughts on drug costs and policy in British Columbia (or Manitoba or anywhere else for that matter) please share. Nevertheless, I think there are two important points to be made here. First, one of the advantages of a federal form of government is policy experimentation that allows new policies to be developed in separate provinces and then implemented nationwide depending on provincial needs. Case in point – income based drug benefits for seniors were implemented in British Columbia in 2003 and since then, they have recently been implemented in Nova Scotia, Newfoundland & Labrador and Saskatchewan. Second, despite the rhetoric over rising health care costs, it apparently is possible to restrain rising costs. Given that it has happened with respect to drugs in several provinces provides hope for the future of sustainable public health care.
