The answer is that in expansionary fiscal policy the government prints bonds and uses them to buy stuff, and that in expansionary monetary policy the government prints money and uses it to buy bonds. If you do both together they don't cancel each other out, but rather the economy is left with (i) the government buying more stuff (and no tendency for private agents to buy less stuff out of fear of extra future taxes because there are no extra future taxes coming because there are no extra bonds left to pay off) and (ii) private agents having more money which they will tend to spend (and no tendency for spending to stay constant as people take their extra cash and hide it under the mattress because the government has already spent the money once). Expansionary fiscal policy makes it a sure thing that expansionary monetary policy is effective. Expansionary monetary policy makes it a sure thing that expansionary fiscal policy is effective by removing the channels for interest-rate and tax crowding out.
