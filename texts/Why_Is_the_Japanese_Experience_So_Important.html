Why Is the Japanese Experience So Important?

https://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/jamessurowiecki/2009/03/why-is-the-japa.html

In the debate about how to deal with our banking crisis, pundits have made frequent reference to a few key historical episodes, using them as examples of good or bad policy, and extrapolating from them to tell policymakers what they should do today. The experience that’s referred to most often is that of Japan during the nineteen-nineties. After a massive real-estate bubble burst at the beginning of the nineties, destroying massive amounts of wealth and leaving the country’s banks effectively insolvent, the government floundered in its attempts to get the economy moving again. In particular, it failed to take over or otherwise deal with the troubled banks, and Japan found itself caught in what came to be known as “the Lost Decade,” with its economy effectively stagnating until the early years of this decade. The lesson, we’re told, is that if the U.S. follows the Japanese path, dithering about and trying to keep troubled banks afloat rather than taking them over and cleaning them up, then there’s a good chance we will be looking ahead to our own Lost Decade. Now, as The Economist’s Ryan Avent pointed out a couple of weeks ago, in an important post that for some reason didn’t get much attention, the lessons of Japan’s experience — and specifically, the reasons why the Japanese economy failed to get moving again — are not as obvious as they’re sometimes made out to be, which in turn means that basing policy on what the Japanese did or didn’t do is harder than it sounds. But there’s also another interesting question, which is: Why, in this debate, have people paid so much more attention to Japan’s banking crisis than to the U.S.’s own past banking crises? It’s not, after all, as if the U.S. economy has never had to deal with big insolvent banks before. On the contrary, as I’ve pointed out before, in both the early eighties (pdf) and the early nineties, many, if not most, of the big American moneycenter banks were, by today’s standards, insolvent. (Go back to 1990, in fact, and you see uncanny echoes of today’s crisis: on August 1, for instance, a Stanford economist made headlines by appearing on ‘Nightline” and saying several of the nation’s biggest banks “were very close to true insolvency,” only to be rebuked by bank regulators and Wall Street analysts for being too pessimistic. Nouriel Roubini redux, anyone?) Yet I’d guess there have been ten references to Japan’s experience with insolvent banks for every one reference to the U.S.’s experience with them (with the obvious exception of references to the S&L crisis, which has little in common with what we’re dealing with today). The interesting question is why. There are certainly important differences between what we’re going through today and what we went through in, say, 1990-1991: deflation seems to be a bigger worry today, the problems appear to be more global in nature, the government has already intervened more deeply in the system than it did then, and banks cannot (as they did in that earlier period) make easy money by borrowing cheaply and investing in Treasuries, and so on. But I suspect one of the reasons why people don’t refer back to the 1990 crisis is this: precisely because that crisis resolved itself without dramatic government intervention, we assume in retrospect that the problems weren’t that serious. Most pundits today begin with the assumption that if banks are technically insolvent (as they arguably are today), then not having the government take decisive action to clean them up will be disastrous. Since the 1990-1991 crisis suggests that this assumption is not necessarily true, it’s basically been written out of history. Instead of beginning with the question “what does the history of banking crises, in the U.S. and other developed countries, tell us about bank insolvency and how to deal with it,” people start with the conclusion “allowing insolvent banks to stay in business is a mistake” and then work backward to the evidence. This doesn’t mean Japan’s experience — or Sweden’s, for that matter — is irrelevant to our own. Nor does it mean that the differences between 1990 and today are not significant: they are massive. But in trying to figure out what will work today, it makes no sense to simply ignore our own past experiences while such paying such close attention to the experiences of others. We’ve dealt with similar problems before, and that could tell us something important about how to deal with them now.
