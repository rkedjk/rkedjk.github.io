Downward spirals

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2008/11/downward_spirals.cfm

INFORMED readers will know that all hurricanes are caused by the flapping of butterfly wings—or could be, at least. But what if we wanted to know for sure? When it comes to wind-and-rain hurricanes, the question is answerable only with the benefit of maths that basically no one can explain intuitively. When it comes to financial hurricanes, we now have the answer in a must-read column by New York University finance professor Lasse Pedersen. Like many others, I’ve learnt more about global finance in the past year than I ever hoped to know. Despite all my swotting, I found Mr Pedersen’s 1,500 words extremely illuminating. I’ll just focus here on one question that his effort cleared up for me. How can American homeowner defaults cause the world’s credit markets to seize up, wreaking havoc on world equity and bond markets along the way? Liquidity spirals seem to be the answer—three interlinked spirals, in fact. Some market participants, say investment banks, are hit with a loss while they are near margin constraints or risk limits. This creates funding problems that induce them to reduce their positions in, say, the credit market. This moves prices against them (and others with similar positions) leading to further losses. And spiral number one (the loss spiral) restarts, as this creates new funding problems. This action increases market volatility, which widens bid-ask spreads. This reduces market liquidity, which typically leads to further funding problems and spiral number two (the margins spiral) restarts. As the losses mount, big players in the market wake up and implement tighter risk management which leads to more funding problems and spiral number three (the risk-management spiral) restarts. The swirling continues until a new equilibrium is reached. In September, the “new equilibrium” meant an almost total shut down of the main credit markets worldwide, starting with interbank lending and spreading outward to encompass commercial paper and much more. Each institution, acting in an individually rational way and following time-honoured banking traditions, amplified the downward spiral in a manner that became self-enforcing. Unfortunately, the same trick does not work for upward spirals; private institutions acting in individually rational ways cannot escape the credit crunch equilibrium. We need the government to kick start the thing—to get many institutions back into the markets. A random comment—one nice outcome of the crisis is that brilliant finance guys like Mr Pedersen are now under-employed. The hedge funds they used to advise ran out of petrol when credit markets seized up. That’s why they have the time to write clear, concise accounts of credit market dynamics for the general public. And, a prediction—we’re going to be hearing a lot more about “liquidity spirals” in coming weeks, and especially about how to prevent them, as the G20 groups sit down to fix the world's regulatory systems. What odds on them hiring Mr Pedersen et cie to explain it to them?
