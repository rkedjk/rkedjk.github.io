Upbeat data, downbeat Fed

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2010/12/american_economy

THEY say a central banker is someone who lies awake worrying that someone, somewhere, might be happy. That seems to be what the Federal Reserve is trying to prove by raining on a parade of upbeat economic and market developments with a morose economic assessment. “The economic recovery is continuing”, it allowed, a mite more upbeat than last month's description of the recovery as “slow”. That's it for the good news. The rest of the statement repeats last month's litany of negatives: household spending is constrained by powerful headwinds, employers are reluctant to hire, and expected inflation is edging lower. The statement seems a little out of place amidst the accumulation of upbeat economic news, and the tax deal announced last week which eliminates the single greatest risk to the outlook, namely, an inadvertent tightening of fiscal policy. Just today a report of strong retail sales led to a round of forecast upgrades. Retail sales rose 0.8% in November from October, and by 1.2% excluding autos. Both were stronger than expected (though some of the rise came from higher petrol prices), and confirm what we suspected from anecdotal reports: the holiday shopping season is off to an auspicious start. Discretionary categories like apparel, sports/books/music, and general merchandise were all strong. Key September and October components were also revised up. Morgan Stanley sees consumption rising 3.5% annualised in the current quarter, and overall GDP up 4.4%. JPMorgan raised its fourth quarter number to 3.5% from 2.5%, and Macroeconomic Advisers, with one of the lowest estimates on the street, raised its projection to 3.1% from 2.7%. Even before the additional stimulus in the tax deal is felt, this tells us that the economy had more underlying momentum than we realised. This has the bond market running scared: the 10-year yield is up 11 basis points today to 3.38%. The consensus, which was too bearish a few months ago, may be swinging too far in the opposite direction. As Paul Krugman noted in his column yesterday, the economy is not a sick patient just waiting for a magical antibiotic to eradicate a pesky virus. It is being held down by deleveraging, a long and drawn out process that takes years, not months, to correct. I've previously argued that while deleveraging will be a headwind for some years to come, the economy can still accelerate if deleveraging proceeds slowly; that can come through faster income growth, a slowing in the pace of increased saving, or both, and indeed, that is what seems to be underway. But that doesn't remove the headwind entirely, and the American economy will feel it again when stimulus starts to unwind again in a year's time. This quarter's upside growth surprise could give way to downside scares before long. My colleagues and I recently debated what this outlook means for financial markets. Between emerging markets' half-hearted efforts to tamp down inflation, Europe's chaotic swing towards austerity and America's swing away from it, multiple forces will tug on yields. Some of us see Treasury yields getting to 4%; others think that will be a ceiling: the overall environment remains deflationary. Whether 2011 will a bloodbath for bonds, a la 1994, or just a gentle reversal, a la 2003, depends on the Fed. It's unlikely to halt its current plan to buy $600 billion of Treasuries through next June. Conceivably, it could decide not to expand on that beyond June. Markets would then start to price in the first tightening in policy, which would chip away at bond prices. I take the opposite message from today's Fed statement, however: the news of the last few weeks has not fundamentally changed its outlook, the dominant themes of which remain lots of excess capacity, growth barely above potential, and inflation expectations headed down, not up. This does not sound like a central bank prepared to take its foot off the accelerator any time soon.
