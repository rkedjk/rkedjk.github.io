A People’s Democracy in America by Laura Tyson & Lenny Mendonca

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/initiative-and-referendum-in-america-by-laura-tyson-and-lenny-mendonca-2018-02

Despite Trumpism and plutocracy in Washington, Laura Tyson and Lenny Mendonca believe American progressivism - a radical tradition born in the 1890s - is not dead. They say today's malaise reminds of the "massive inequalities of the Gilded Age" when "robber baron" families like the Carnegies, Rockefellers, Vanderbilts etc. built up vast fortunes from monopolies in the late 19th century. It was an era of rapid industrialisation and growing inequality in America, with the super-rich holding the greatest concentration of wealth. The Gilded Age gave way to the Progressive Era, when the backlash against the excesses and inequalities of the earlier period prompted widespread anger, ushering in social activism and calling for political reform. President Theodore Roosevelt was elected in 1901, and he moved to break up companies, trusts and monopolies. John D Rockefeller’s Standard Oil was split into 34 separate companies. And taxes were levied on the wealthy. In an insightful commentary the authors highlight how the first Progressives "sought comprehensive reform." From the time America won independence from Britain, national government was deliberately kept weak. By 1900 rapid industrialisation and urbanisation had created huge economic challenges and social upheaval. The Progressives believed that America's eighteenth-century institutions must be modernised and that government should take a more active role in p life. Progressivism had a broad set of beliefs with a wide range of different opinions and its targets were multiple. Progressives were mainly urban and college educated activists. In the cities they fought political corruption and monopolies in utilities like electricity and gas. While embracing idealism and rejecting social darwinism, they believed that the problems the society faced (greedy, poverty, racism, class warfare and violence) could be addressed by providing good education, a safe environment, and promoting workers' welfare. The authors say, thanks to Progressives' successful campaign, changes to the Constitution were adopted. The Sixteenth Amendment saw the introduction of the federal income tax. What was significant was the Seventeenth Amendment in 1913, with senators being elected by the people, rather than by state legislatures, which were often dominated by big businesses. The Nineteenth Amendment in 1919 granted women the right to vote. Across the country, Progressives "wanted citizens to rule more directly," free from the dominance of powerful elites. They took on big businesses - banks, industrial corporations - that were seen as sucking the life-blood of workers and crushed their individual freedom. The fought politicians in Washington who had been bought and corrupted by the bankers and robber barons, destroying the true revolutionary tradition of America. Today the new generation of Progressive are seeking "to give citizens power over policy," and bring “deliberative democracy” to the fore. This direct democracy ensures that political decisions should be the product of fair and reasonable discussion and debate among citizens. About half of American states have a mix of three forms of direct democracy: recall, popular referendum and the initiative. Most of these provisions were adopted between 1898 and 1918 when state constitutions were amended. America is a big country and it takes time "to use initiatives in a more coordinated national campaign." But an initial success can be reaped "from the minimum-wage campaigns" which shows "the promise of such an effort." The authors point out that this year sees "initiatives for democratic reforms - including redistricting, stricter ethics standards, and broader voting rights - /that/ are in the process of being qualified across the country." Indeed, the "original Progressives would be proud." Although it has "taken more than a century, but their effort to ensure that democracy actually works, by putting power in the hands of citizens through the initiative process, created what may be the most powerful reform tool in US history." Hence a disastrous presidency will not plunge the US into the abyss, as long as it has a strong civil society and a progressive movement that keep the country going.
