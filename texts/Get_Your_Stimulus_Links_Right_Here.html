Get Your Stimulus Links Right Here

https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2010/06/09/get-your-stimulus-links-right-here/

There has been a lot of good blogging lately on the debate over another stimulus package (the subject of my column on Wednesday). Some links: * Kevin Drum makes the case for a tax cut: “For Obama, then, it would be the best of all worlds: a fast stimulus that gets bipartisan support, something that boosts the economy while dampening the inevitable criticism he’d get for blowing up the deficit. But he’s not pushing for this. Not even quietly. And this suggests that Tyler is right: Obama’s advisers might be in favor of further fiscal stimulus, but not by much.” * The aforementioned Tyler is Tyler Cowen. He writes: “If a bigger … stimulus would set so many things right, they’d gladly lay tons of political capital on the line to see it through and proclaim triumph at the end of the road. Except they expect it would bring only a marginal improvement. And for that marginal improvement they have only a marginal desire to: 1. Raise the long-term national debt (if it’s fiscal stimulus), 2. Put their reputations behind policies which might backfire or irritate Congress, 3. Put additional pressure on the independence of the Fed (if it’s more aggressive monetary policy)…” * Matt Yglesias thinks we talk too much about supply and not enough about demand: “We right now have the capacity to produce more—much more—than has ever been produced before in the history of the planet. There are dozens of supply-side policies that could be improved in every country on earth, but that’s not a new fact about the world. What’s new is the lack of demand, the willingness of the key leaders in Tokyo, Frankfurt, Washington, Berlin, and now it seems London as well to tolerate stagnation and disinflation in the face of some of the most exciting fundamental new opportunities for human economic betterment ever.” * Mark Thoma has been full of good stimulus posts lately. * Edward Glaeser points out that states that received more highway money and other such infrastructure funds do not appear to be doing better than states that have received less. Jared Bernstein — responding to this related post from Mr. Glaeser — offers a different take. * Brad DeLong wants bigger deficits now. * Martin Wolf describes the opposition to stimulus: “Let us translate this proposal into ordinary language: ‘If you are unwilling to starve yourself when desperately ill, nobody will believe you would adopt a sensible diet when well.’ But might it not make sense to get better first?” Note: This paragraph, from the original version, has been changed above to make clear Mr. Glaeser was describing only a portion of the stimulus program: Edward Glaeser asks why states that received more stimulus money are not doing better. Jared Bernstein answers (in response to an earlier post from Mr. Glaeser) that it’s partly because much stimulus money was aimed at the worst-off states.
