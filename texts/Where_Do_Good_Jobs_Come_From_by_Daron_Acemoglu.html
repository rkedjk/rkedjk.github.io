Where Do Good Jobs Come From? by Daron Acemoglu

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/automation-vs-job-creation-by-daron-acemoglu-2019-04

I don't think I said anything about safety standards being unimportant, although I must confess that I sometimes wonder if some of the things done in the name of safety actually improve it. But don't get me wrong. I am sure many of them do. But my important point is that it seems inevitable to me that eventually, there will just be essentially no more need for human work at all. It does seem possible that we might always want certain tasks to be done by people, even if machines could actually do them. Things like nursing, for example, or waiting tables in fancy restaurants, perhaps even some sales jobs - things where people will just want to deal with a person rather than a machine. But I am not sure that even those jobs will continue to be necessary. We have not yet tried to push the boundaries of what people can get used to. And I am sure that things like automobile assembly will eventually be completely automated. So even if some jobs remain, how many people can they employ? What of those who have no aptitude for such jobs? Also, even if we just keep improving productivity, employing the same number of people to make more and more stuff, how much such productivity do we expect the planet to support? Especially as previously unindustrialized countries like China join the ranks of rich, industrialized countries and begin loading the environment similarly? We would be far better off to use that productivity to give ourselves more leisure time than to keep producing more and more. Both of these objectives can be met by instituting a basic income or negative income tax, paid for, at least in part, by sales taxes and carbon taxes. Think of it as us paying ourselves for our ownership of all the historic knowledge that goes into making anything today. I build machines, but I know that actually, my contribution to the process is very small. How many machines would I be able to build if I had to figure out how to smelt iron, for example, or invent the wheel. To me, everybody should share the ownership of those ideas, and everybody should get paid for them, not just those of us who are in a position to take advantage of them. And eventually, all production will be done by historical ideas like that.
