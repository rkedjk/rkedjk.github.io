“Stocks are somewhat overbought at this level,” Mr. Birinyi says. It’s like walking along Fifth Avenue and seeing a red light, he said: “If you’re a New Yorker, it doesn’t really mean you shouldn’t cross. You can cross if you want — but you’d better look where you’re going.” While the market may now be vulnerable to a short-term decline, he says he is convinced that we are still in the midst of a powerful “secular bull market” with plenty of room to run. The bond market has been rallying, too. The Fed’s quantitative easing, aimed explicitly at lowering the interest rates of Treasuries in the 10-year range, isn’t likely to extend that rally for very long, said Mohamed A. El-Erian, chief executive of Pimco, the world’s largest bond manager. Yields of 10-year Treasuries, now at about 2.5 percent, are close to fair value, he said. “The Fed is taking action aimed at pushing you as an investor into risk assets” like stocks, he said. But he added that “people don’t really like to be pushed into something,” so the Fed’s moves “are still attracting some skepticism,” which may make markets quite volatile. Problems that could derail the markets and the economy abound. Ethan S. Harris, chief North American economist at Bank of America Merrill Lynch, said gridlock in Washington could quickly negate the short-term market benefits of the Fed’s quantitative easing. If Democrats and Republicans in a forthcoming lame-duck session of Congress don’t reach a compromise on taxes fairly quickly, he said, it could “rattle the markets, and if the two sides don’t reach an agreement by year-end, the level of uncertainty will be very, very high.” That’s because a variety of taxes are scheduled to rise on Jan. 1 if Congress doesn’t act. Mr. Harris calculates the net negative impact of such an across-the-board tax hike — which neither Republicans nor Democrats favor — as the equivalent of subtracting roughly 2 percent from G.D.P. next year. That could send economic growth to zero. Presumably, there will eventually be some kind of compromise — perhaps an extension of the Bush-era income tax cuts for all taxpayers, which the Republicans prefer, but on a “temporary” basis of maybe two years, he said.
