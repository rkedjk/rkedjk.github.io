Tipping the scales

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2010/06/global_imbalances_1

THIS week, let me remind you, the question at Economics by invitation concerns the issue of Chinese revaluation. Should China revalue now, is revaluation an important part of rebalancing, and should rebalancing be a top priority? In one contribution, Mark Thoma writes: Addressing imbalances should be a global priority, but the first step in this process is to gain a better understanding of the forces—including government polices—behind global financial flows. We need to understand how global imbalances contributed to the crisis so we can avoid a repeat in the future. This echoes the conclusion of a new piece at Vox, in which Kati Suominen notes: To date studies on the exact contribution of global imbalances or their drivers to the crisis are inconclusive. More hard evidence is needed to answer the question in the title: Did global imbalances cause the crisis? Meanwhile, positions are changing. Many observers, including Ben Bernanke, now prioritise regulatory failures as the leading cause for the crisis... But arguing that the imbalances played a sideshow in 2008-09 is not to claim that they are innocuous. They could still perpetrate the crisis predicted by the alarmists. As a matter of prudence, policies to contain them could still be warranted. As Ms Suominen mentions earlier in the piece, many of those predicting a massive crisis (including Nouriel Roubini) did so based on the expectation that it would be precipitated by a sudden unwinding of imbalances. In fact, it was a credit crunch resulting from fear of bank insolvency (itself a result of the housing market implosion) that did the global economy in. But just because imbalances weren't necessarily the main factor in the last crisis doesn't mean that they won't be in the next one. Ms Suominen provides a nice chart: The numbers are a little hard to read, but the year in which global surpluses peak is 2007. The nice thing about this chart is that it provides a look at a number of potential economic threats. One relates to America's substantial deficit, which has as its pair surpluses in China and oil-exporting nations, with smaller contributions coming from Germany and Japan. Prior to the crisis, some bears warned that trouble would ensue when some portion of this relationship broke down, leading to a sudden stop in the recycling of dollar surpluses back to America. This threat has actually diminished somewhat as a result of the crisis. America's trade deficit did shrink considerably through the recession, and its debt is increasingly financed by domestic sources. This particular imbalance is still a threat in a world struggling to return to broad-based growth. Given weak American growth, big Chinese surpluses make for constant tension and the potential for a trade war. Large and persistent oil exporter surpluses pose their own problem, primarily because they reflect growing demand for oil. The huge oil surplus in 2008 corresponded to the oil price spike in 2008, which helped tip the world into deep recession. That vulnerability still exists, particularly given red hot emerging market growth. And then you have German surpluses, which are largely matched by equivalent deficits elsewhere in Europe—in Britain, Spain, Italy and so on. The crisis in southern Europe is forcing an immediate stop to that particular imbalance. That, in turn, will push Germany to either trim its surplus or attempt to ship it elsewhere (which, in practice, means America). Whether or not global imbalances "caused" the recent crisis (and clearly there were some other significant factors) it's hard to avoid noticing that they're a frequent crisis accomplice. The sooner they can be safely unwound the better. The difficult question is whether there's a path to safe resolution of imbalances running through the minefield of impending crises.
