What Will Make These Fiscal Showdowns Stop?

https://blogs.hbr.org/fox/2013/01/what-will-make-these-fiscal-sh.html

Almost a quarter century ago, journalist Alan Ehrenhalt set out to describe the nation’s political landscape by looking at the priorities and concerns not of voters but of those who run for office. The result was a book, The United States of Ambition, that’s one of the best, most revelatory accounts I’ve ever read of my country’s political process. I was covering Alabama politics and government for The Birmingham News when the book came out in 1991, and almost everything Ehrenhalt wrote rang true, especially the chapter on Alabama (and it’s always surprising and heartening when a journalist from outside gets it so right on a topic one knows well). I remember being perplexed that the book got only modest attention while E.J. Dionne’s Why Americans Hate Politics, which came out at the same time and was fine but not nearly as interesting as Ehrenhalt’s book, was a big bestseller (clearly, I had a lot to learn about book marketing). But here’s the most important thing about The United States of Ambition: Despite all its awesomeness, its central prediction hasn’t panned out at all. Ehrenhalt thought that the intense demands of modern electoral politics naturally favored Democrats — because they believed in government and were thus able to sustain their interest in and enthusiasm for politicking and governing for decades on end. Republicans might still win a lot of presidential elections, and would occasionally get caught up in waves of outrage that swept ambitious, committed conservatives into power. But eventually they’d always lose interest and go back to, say, running businesses, allowing Democratic careerists to sneak back in to dominate statehouses and Capitol Hill. Wrote Ehrenhalt: The Democratic party is the party of government in the United States, or, more precisely, the party that believes in government … As such, it is the obvious magnet for people who think running for office is worth the considerable sacrifice it entails. Those are the sorts of people who get elected at this juncture in American politics. They are the ingredients of a majority. So … guess what’s happened since Ehrenhalt’s book was published: Democrats have won the popular vote in five of six presidential elections, yet Republicans have mostly dominated the U.S. House of Representatives and have held a majority in the Senate about half the time. The GOP also now controls 24 state governments (25 if you count Nebraska, where the legislature is technically nonpartisan but the state leans heavily Republican) to 12 for the Democrats (the rest are split). There are also 29 Republican governors to 20 Democrats and one independent. There are lots of potential explanations for this: gerrymandering; the odd dynamics of House races, where occasional low-turnout midterm elections can set the tone for years; the rise of billionaire political donors; a new media and lobbying landscape that allows liberals and conservatives to assemble politically oriented careers at least as lucrative as any they could hope to follow in the purely private sector. But I also wouldn’t rule out what I’ll call the Downs Law of Political Science (after Anthony Downs, author An Economic Theory of Democracy): the more convincing and elegant a theory of political behavior is, the less likely it is to predict what happens next. Politics is just messy. I mention all this because the juxtaposition of Democrats’ national success and the continued strength of increasingly conservative Republicans at the state level and in the House is one of the main reasons why we keep getting into standoffs like the fiscal cliff debate of the last few weeks. And rest assured, we will keep getting into them — the New Year’s Day fiscal cliff vote put off the reckoning for a couple of months, but then there will be another battle over the debt ceiling, and more showdowns after that. The president can claim an electoral mandate, albeit not a huge one, for his policy priorities. But the Republicans still maintain a solid, if smaller, majority in the House, and most GOP House members have more to fear from primary challengers from the right than from Democratic general election opponents — meaning that, on the individual level, they have no incentive to compromise. Barring a spectacular economic growth spurt that rapidly reduces deficits, it will take a real change in the political landscape to put Washington on a less confrontational, more predictable fiscal path. Predicting such change, though, is really, really hard. The November election appeared to back up the decade-old thesis of John Judis and Ruy Teixeira that minorities, women, younger voters, and affluent professionals would form the heart of an “emerging Democratic majority.” In this view, the Republicans are simply fighting a last losing battle or two before submitting to minority status for a generation. As noted above, though, any political theory that simple and straightforward should be looked upon with suspicion. It’s easy enough to envision a less immigration-phobic Republican Party attracting the many minority voters who hold conservative social and economic views. And even if things pan out more or less as Judis and Teixeira envision, there’s lots of room for Republican triumphs and gridlock along the way (as there was for Democrats in the Republican-dominated era that appears to have passed). Another possibility would be that Howard Schultz actually persuades politicians in Washington to “come together” in a bipartisan spirit to get things done, gosh darn it. Or that our political system unravels completely, leaving us all to fend for ourselves with freeze-dried food and even more guns than we have now. I’m neither optimistic nor pessimistic enough to put much stock in either of those scenarios. So I’m going to give Alan Ehrenhalt’s theory another shot, and try to divine the political future by looking at what drives people to run for, and stay in, office. What Ehrenhalt missed in 1991, I think, was the sustaining power of ideas. Yes, a belief in government is one thing that can motivate. But for decades the most powerful idea in U.S. politics — the idea that has generated the most grassroots excitement, the most inspired rhetoric, the biggest political donations, and the most committed candidates — has been economic libertarianism. This libertarianism — the belief that smaller government, lower taxation, and more-freewheeling capitalism will deliver greater prosperity — has run into something of a political dead end, in that only a small percentage of the U.S. electorate fully subscribes to it. That’s why Ron Paul’s presidential campaigns never got anywhere, and why we have a House GOP that constantly talks of cutting spending, but knows that actually cutting spending in a big way (which would likely require paring Medicare and defense) wouldn’t go over well at all with Republican voters. Yet the teachings of Ayn Rand and Friedrich Hayek have continued to inspire more intense political commitment than any other ideology on offer. This can’t go on forever. The libertarian fervor will fade, and some other set of ideas will come along to fire up ambitious young politicians, in the process shifting the political balance — one hopes for the better, although there’s no guarantee.
