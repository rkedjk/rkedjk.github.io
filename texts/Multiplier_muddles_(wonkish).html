Multiplier muddles (wonkish)

https://krugman.blogs.nytimes.com/2009/07/14/multiplier-muddles-wonkish/

Greg Mankiw asks why two New Keynesian analyses — by Eichenbaum et al and by Cogan et al (both large pdfs, neither written in English) — reach such different conclusions about the size of the fiscal multiplier. One main answer, I think, is that in Eichenbaum et al fiscal policy is modeled as a response to the liquidity trap, going away when the trap ends — and they allow for the possibility that fiscal policy will help the economy escape the trap. Cogan et al, on the other hand, model the liquidity trap by simply assuming fixed interest rates for two years. It’s probably also worth noting that the big factor causing a low multiplier in Cogan et al is the assumption that consumers believe that high government spending now will mean higher taxes in the future, so that they reduce their consumption. Investment also falls because of the expectation of future interest rate increases. You can choose to believe this if you want, but it’s not properly described as a demonstration of what modern macro necessarily implies. On the other hand, the big factor causing a high multiplier in Eichenbaum et al is the possibility of a deflationary spiral, which fiscal policy helps avoid. It is nice, by the way, to have Eichenbaum et al showing that the paradox of thrift is real even in a model with lots of maximizing bells and whistles. Has someone told Gene Fama and John Cochrane? Oh, and Cogan et al lose a lot of points for doing exactly what Brad says: comparing Romer-Bernstein with an analysis done for non-zero-bound conditions.
