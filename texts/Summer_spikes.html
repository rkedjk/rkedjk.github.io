Summer spikes

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2010/08/labour_markets_2

LAST week, Casey Mulligan noted that teenage employment continues to rise in the summer as students are let out of school, despite the recession—a sure sign, in his view, that labour supply is a crucial factor in determining the unemployment rate, even in recessions. High unemployment, he supposes, is due to the unwillingness of labourers to take jobs, which he attributes to various government policies that create bad incentives for unemployed workers. At the same time, he can't seem to absorb the fact that most of the economists supporting countercyclical, Keynesian policies agree that there is some role for supply issues in unemployment, just not a very big one relative to demand. As a response to Mr Mulligan, I noted that jobs teenages fill in the summer probably aren't like the jobs most adult workers occupy. They're explicitly short-term (indeed, of fixed duration), requiring few skills, with no opportunity for advancement or benefits. Just as important, they're largely seasonal positions targeted specifically at teenagers—camp counselors, lifeguards, landscaping workers, amusement park attendants, and so on. Tim Duy followed up on this point, with data showing that retail sales are strongly seasonal—demand increases with supply in the summer, in a complementary fashion. But Mr Mulligan remains unconvinced. This week he has a new post up, in which he tracks the summer spike in employment and unemployment across young worker age brackets. In the summer, employment shoots upward for teenage workers but is flat for workers age 25 to 34. If demand were the main issue, Mr Mulligan says, we should expect to see rising employment among older workers, as well. UNLESS, he fails to note, the increased demand is concentrated in seasonal sectors that are attractive to teenage workers but not older workers. This point seems blindingly obvious, but Mr Mulligan seems to pay it no mind. Let's turn to the data. On average, teenage employment rises by around 1.5 million from May to July. Where does it all go? Well, employment at "amusement parks and arcades" (these are Labour Department categories) typically rises by about 200,000 during the summer. So does employment at golf courses and country clubs. Landscapers hire another 200,000 workers during the summer months. Employment at "fitness and recreational sports centers" rises by about 150,000. What's interesting about this isn't just that rising employment demand comes from seasonal sectors. It's also important to note that the increased demand largely comes from the kids not attending school—the people going to amusement parks and arcades, country club pools, camps, sports facilities and similar places are kids. This is largely a little self-contained corner of the economy oriented around the particular timing of the school year. It seems to me that it would be pretty irresponsible to try and draw broad conclusions from such an unusual economic niche. And let's also focus on the big story Mr Mulligan is ignoring. Here's teenage employment over the past ten years: The seasonal pattern is clear. Mr Mulligan is very, very interested in discussing that last little uptick in employment growth, from May to July, representing the million or so summer jobs American teenagers are currently filling. What he doesn't seem very concerned about is the fact that the 2010 increase is a good half a million shy of the rise in the summer of 2006. Or the fact that the absolute level of teenage employment in 2010 is 2.5 million jobs short of the summer peak in 2006. Of the teenagers entering the labour force, far fewer are able to find work, even in the summer employment niche; teenage unemployment is currently running at over 26%, compared to a pre-recession level of about 16%. Let me be clear; I'm perfectly willing to accept that there are supply issues involved in unemployment. The San Francisco Fed estimates that jobless benefits have probably pushed up the unemployment rate by about 0.4 percentage points, and I have no reason to disagree with them. But there is every indication that demand is playing a primary role in holding down employment. Teenagers out of school for the summer are overwhelmingly not sitting around enjoying unemployment benefits. Mr Mulligan links to an op-ed on the recent health insurance reform package as an example of Barack Obama's "supply-harming" legislation, but the vast majority of teenage workers aren't getting health benefits from their employers. More important still—Mr Obama's health care policies haven't taken effect yet. Is Mr Mulligan really anxious to argue that teens are sitting at home instead of cutting grass because they're concerned that in 2013 their effective marginal tax rate will adjust in a disadvantageous way thanks to government health insurance subsidies? Again, he'll have to do much better than this to demonstrate that it's Keynesians, and not those touting a "Great Vacation", that are all wet.
