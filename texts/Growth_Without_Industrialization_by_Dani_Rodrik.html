Growth Without Industrialization? by Dani Rodrik

https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/poor-economies-growing-without-industrializing-by-dani-rodrik-2017-10

A welcome article. There is some very strange thinking going on in some places with regard to developing economies. Firstly that exports are intrinsically infinite, eg that consumption is infinite in the importing developed countries which clearly cannot be the case. Secondly that agriculture that utilises some 50 to 60% of a population - the undeveloped model - can trend however slowly to 3% of the population - the developed model - without major incipient unemployment. At the start of the 1900s the freshly unemployed from farming in developed countries were recruited into consumer goods production. This simply will not happen today to the same extent in developing economies. The engine that funded the Industrial Revolution in Britain was a flood of private funds seeking returns. That capital came from the abolition of slavery and the compensation paid to slave owners by the British Government circa 1833, some 16.5Billion sterling in today's money - in a single year - and without it the Industrial Revolution as we know it may never have happened. With regard to China much has been funded by FDI, a similar one off event. Furthermore only 1/5th of China has industrialised and industrialisation appears to have slowed, as has FDI. So it is not as though there is a shortage of potential capacity and that is just China. Very simply, it is beyond any sensible horizon that developing countries can implement widescale industrialisation, aka consumer product manufacture in one shape or another. There simply is not the market for exports and nor is the domestic market present. All that needs monitoring to see how bad things could be around the corner for developing countries is to watch India's agricultural sector which is under stress and shedding labour. That Indian labour has found what appears to be temporary relief through jobs in construction, another transitionary process, which is now shedding labour as the sector stalls. There is a noticeable difference between vegetables and buildings, vegetables have a short shelf life and are constantly consumed at the same level, buildings are not. There is the very distinct possibility that unemployment could grow in India where a million new jobs per month are needed for the next 3 decades to stand still. Consider that unemployment may well steadily grow in India for the first time since WW2. If so there is no reason the pattern could not be present elsewhere. So generally industrialisation aka consumer goods looks somewhat like a cul de sac for some. Service sector development will require truly massive investment in infrastructure, in some cases estimated as 75% of GDP. Quite how that can be funded is the moot point, but certainly not by aid from developed countries. Apart from anything else why should capital develop infrastructure which only has local benefit, it doesn't fit the capital model Growing populations coupled with growing unemployment with the overlay of water shortage - which is also in growth in many of the same locations - is a recipe for major social problems which will not be containable. Resource shortage is the basis of conflict and water is a very basic resource. There also is a very strange thought processes going on about the whole situation with some would be policy influencers, namely - That developed countries can or are willing politically to transfer wealth unendingly to developing countries to provide uplift (the GR crisis is a good example of resistance to wealth transfer and that is intra Europe). That sufficient funds are available to do so (consider 75% of GDP is required for infrastructure development in some countries). That the demographic pressures from a ageing profile in developed countries can be addressed by compensatory young inward migration - which quite frankly is a lunatic economists model as it simple expands the problem with time. That a population explosion in developing countries has hidden benefits long term - which is completely unproven because such an event has never happened before. You would need to be munching on magic mushrooms to think these are automatically helpful attributes. Such optimism is akin to flipping a coin and never expecting anything other than 'Heads'. In moving to closing I will refer to Bulgaria, another microcosm of nutty unbalanced economic outcomes. A small country which has a very significantly shrinking population due to the young migrating elsewhere. A feature as it happens of quite a bit of Southern Italy, and parts of Poland too. All in Europe, a generally affluent region. How can anybody think it helps a society to export their educated young and rapidly accelerate an aging demographic and collapse tax revenue. How can is do anything other than sow the seeds of social and cultural destruction At the heart of what is going on is technology having profound impacts and along the way raising expectations. Technology in the form of medicine is saving lives and sparking population expansion amongst the poor, which ultimately is labour supply. Capital, through exploiting technology, is also seeking to improve productivity and reduce process labour content at the same time and increasingly demands skilled workers. The two processes are in opposition and move the supply and demand point globally. There are positives outcomes that try to balance these effects but whether balance can be achieved is another point entirely. The basic Ronald Reagan mantra of make the pie bigger so everybody gets a slice runs into logistical problems and cultural issues and at the end of the day is why the current globalisation model will stall. The established correlation between affluence and reproductive rate shows voluntary reproduction only reduces with affluence (to the point that affluent populations actually shrink), thus unless affluence grows in impoverished populations the population will increase - despite whatever forecasts are made - unless constrained by disaster or influenced by local government intervention. The upshot is there will be no shortage of labour which will in itself likely impede investment in developing economies. Contemporaneously you have AI developing, outcome unknown, but likely affecting skilled jobs
