Taking comfort in easy solutions

https://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2009/05/taking_comfort_in_easy_solutio.cfm

I OFTEN feel nostalgic for the days when reforming Social Security was the hot economics issue. Perhaps lawmakers do as well. Despite everything else on their plate, there appears to be motivation to restore the Social Security programme to solvency. Why deal with the thorny issue of financial reform when you can deal "easily" with Social Security? The Washington Post reports [Lindsey] Graham described Social Security as "a math problem" that could be easily solved if both parties were willing to work together. He sketched out a plan that would include lower benefits for wealthy Americans, a higher retirement age and additional revenues. With the stock market devastated by the recession, the traditional Republican option of diverting Social Security taxes to new private retirement accounts is, he said, "off the table." That's disappointing. I wonder if not privatising Social Security a few years ago turned out to be a missed opportunity. Imagine, some fraction of that 12.6% (including employee and employer contributions) of your income buying shares (though the plan was not necessarily to invest in the stockmarket) right now instead of being ploughed into a government programme that will probably cut your benefits. The return on your tax dollar toward Social Security is less than 2% and will most certainly fall further. You might be saying, "But if we privatised my portfolio would be down 30% now!" Keep in mind, if you have many years before retirement there's a good chance your portfolio will recover. Plus, you would be buying shares now, at relatively low prices. Compare that to your projected Social Security benefit which will also probably decline. And unlike a private account, it will not rebound with the market. Of course older workers will not get a chance to see their portfolios recover, but they would only have had a few years worth of contributions and they do not face benefit cuts. Recent events do highlight a weakness with investing private accounts in equities--asset values can plunge right before you retire. But private accounts can include different investment strategies and staggered annuity purchases that provide some hedge against that. Of course, privatising would not have been that easy. That 12.6% needs to fund current obligations. So tax dollars going to a private accounts would probably have been "add-on" rather than "carve-out". Though part of "the math" will probably include a tax increase. I'd rather see my additional taxes go toward a private, funded account, than propping up a system which plans on cutting my benefit. But back to the question of why Social Security has returned to the spotlight. Mark Thoma, channelling Paul Krugman, reckons it has more to do with politics. Remember that the "Beltway obsession with Social Security reflects ideology and fashion, not the real problems facing America." They may think that they can wait until health care reform is completed before turning to this issue, but if they continue to have these meetings and push this agenda, there's a good chance Social Security will become a bargaining chip during the health care debate. However, trading Social Security against health care when there are so many other possible tradeoffs within the budget (on both the revenue and spending sides) is not an outcome I'd like to see, though I'm sure conservatives would love the chance to do just that. I agree that Social Security is not our most pressing economic problem at the moment. But that does not mean it is not a problem. The sooner Social Security's finances are sorted the less costly restoring long-term solvency will be. As America piles on more debt, a sustainable plan that provides for its aging population becomes even more important.
