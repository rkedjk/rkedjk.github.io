Did Low Interest Rates Cause the Great Housing Convulsion?

https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2010/08/03/did-low-interest-rates-cause-the-great-housing-convulsion/

Edward L. Glaeser is an economics professor at Harvard. Between April 2000 and April 2006, the Standard & Poor’s/Case-Shiller Repeat Sales Index for 20 metropolitan areas shows that real housing prices rose by 71 percent. Between April 2006 and April 2009, that same index showed that real housing prices dropped by 36 percent. It would be nice to understand what caused this great housing convulsion, but we do not. The most common explanation for the great surge in prices is the availability of easy credit, which took the form of low interest rates, high loan-to-value ratios and permissive approval of mortgages. These variables certainly affect housing prices, but they don’t seem to have moved nearly enough to explain the great price fluctuations of the past decade. I’m going to address interest rates today, and next week, with far less certainty, I’ll turn to other aspects of the credit market. Both of these posts describe joint research with Joseph Gyourko of the Wharton School and Joshua Gottlieb, a Harvard Ph.D. student. The chart below shows housing prices, as measured by the more nationally representative Federal Housing Finance Agency housing price index, and the real interest rate, as measured by the 10-year Treasury bond rate, which is corrected for expected inflation by using the Livingstone survey of inflation expectations. Over the course of the boom, from 1996 to 2006, real interest rates fell by 1.2 percentage points, or 120 basis points. Low interest rates did accompany the rise in prices, but was the drop in rates big enough to explain the rise in prices? The first way to get at the connection between interest rates and housing prices is to look at the data. From 1980 to 2008, prices typically fell by 6.8 percent as interest rates rose by one percentage point. This measured effect gets weaker as we add other controls, like a time trend. The connection between interest rates and prices is stronger at lower real interest rates, but even at low real rates, when we include a time trend, prices seem to rise by 8 percent as interest rates fall by one percentage point. There are many reasons to be skeptical about this kind of empirical approach (short time period, reverse causality and others), which leads many housing economists to put more weight on economic theory. The most common way of thinking about the impact of interest rates on prices is to assume that renting and owning should have roughly equal costs, which should mean that: Rents = (Interest Costs + Maintenance, Tax and Other Costs) x Housing Price – Expected Appreciation. If rents stay constant, this equation implies that housing price increases should exactly offset interest-rate declines. Using standard assumptions about appreciation, taxes and so forth, this model implies that a drop of one percentage point in interest rates could cause as much as a 24 percent rise in prices – which is much larger than the data suggests. Our paper explores four forces that weaken the implied connection between interest rates and housing prices. When it’s easy to build, interest rates will have a modest impact on housing prices because new construction will satisfy demand. The ability to refinance mortgages also reduces the implied impact of interest rates on prices. But our most important alteration to the standard model is to allow interest rates to change and follow a standard random process. The movement of interest rates means that people who buy when interest rates are low will probably be selling when interest rates are higher. This simple shift causes the implied relationship between interest rates and prices to drop by more than a half. Including the tendency of real interest rates to revert to historical norms causes the model to predict that a one percentage point drop in real rates should cause prices to rise by 8 percent, which is just about what the data show. So theory and data both predict that the 1.2 percentage point drop in real interest rates that American experienced between 1996 and 2006 should cause a price increase of somewhat less than 10 percent, yet prices actually rose over this period by more than 40 percent. Since interest rates have stayed low, low real rates can’t explain the post-boom drop in prices either. Standard models and statistical work don’t predict that the observed swings in real rates should have led to such massive housing price fluctuations. I am not claiming that interest rates weren’t important, or that they didn’t play a role in creating the cocktail that led to a housing price bubble. I suspect that they did. I am not defending the wisdom of monetary policy. I am only dismissing the notion that we really understand what caused the bubble, where really understanding means – to me – that we have a well-specified theory that has been tested in many different ways and not rejected by the data.
