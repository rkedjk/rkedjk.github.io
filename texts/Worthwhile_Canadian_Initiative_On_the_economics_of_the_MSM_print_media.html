Worthwhile Canadian Initiative: On the economics of the MSM print media

http://worthwhile.typepad.com/worthwhile_canadian_initi/2008/12/on-the-economics-of-msm-print-media.html

Paul Wells, Andrew Potter and Dan Gardner have posted recently on the plight of the economic model of MSM journalism. What will happen to popular discourse if people stop buying newspapers and magazines because pretty much everything that's in them is available for free on the internet? Well, let's consider what's at stake. From page 309 of Paul Wells' book Right Side Up on the aftermath of the 2006 election: I do not believe there was nearly enough effort made by many of my colleagues, especially among newsroom managers, to understand how long-term trends toward fluffy, gossipy Ottawa coverage turned us into sitting ducks as soon as we tried to argue we were standing up for any kind of journalistic principle. We had razed forests to tell Canadians about Paul Martin's chef and Jean Chrétien's golf balls and Harper's formidable gut and the odd little fishing vest he wore in Cancun and the way he shook his daughter's hand when he dropped her off at school. And we were supposed to be the guardians of - what, precisely? Sadly, his colleagues did not take this scolding to heart in the most recent election. As I ranted over here, "if you wanted to actually learn something about [the] issues, the very last place you should go searching for enlightenment is the mainstream media". Too many of them - with all-too few exceptions - made no effort to understand the issues; in particular, those who used the word 'complicated' to describe the Liberals' 'Green Shift' proposal have no business referring to themselves as professional journalists. I cannot believe that public discourse would have been degraded any further if the number of media types covering the last election had been reduced by half. Or two-thirds. Which brings us to the MSM journalists' defense of their profession against the blogosphere. If all the journalists have to offer is insider access and re-writes of press releases, then they might as well hang up their skates; the insiders and PR types will eventually shift their attention to the bloggers. And quite frankly, the prospect of insiders and PR flaks scrambling to make their views known through a more fragmented set of stenographers doesn't fill me with despair. Until recently, the MSM were able to act as oligopolists: media technology required a large fixed cost for distribution, and this led to the development of large networks and/or conglomerates. With that oligopoly position came oligopoly rents. But that technology has changed: it is no longer necessary to invest in an expensive distribution network to reach an audience, so media have moved closer to a competitive model. And in a competitive model, price equals marginal product. In this sort of transition, the losers are the former oligopolists. But the winners are the consumers.
